Section 3.1 · Foundation

Why are Plant and Animal Tissues Different?

Structure follows function — and plants and animals live very differently

A tissue is a group of cells that are similar in structure and work together to perform a specific function. The formation of different tissue types creates a division of labour — increasing the body's efficiency and enabling complex life processes. In unicellular organisms like Amoeba, one cell does everything. In multicellular organisms, different cell groups specialise and share the work.

🌳
Plants — Fixed and Photosynthetic
Plants are fixed in one place. They need rigid support to stay upright. They rely on photosynthesis for food. Their tissues reflect these needs: rigid cell walls, stomata for gas exchange, vascular tissue for long-distance transport of water and food.
🐾
Animals — Mobile and Heterotrophic
Animals move from place to place. They obtain food from external sources. They need flexible cells, a nervous system to coordinate movement, muscles to move bones, and digestive tissues to process food.
🏗️
Hierarchy of Organisation
Cells → Tissue → Multiple tissues → Organ → Multiple organs → Organ System → Organism. Each level has emergent properties not present at the lower level.

Section 3.2 · Plant Tissues

Meristematic Tissues — Zones of Growth

The actively dividing cells responsible for all plant growth

Growth in plants requires actively dividing cells. These groups of dividing cells are called meristematic tissues. Meristematic cells are small, have thin cell walls, a large nucleus, dense cytoplasm, and no vacuoles — features that allow continuous, rapid division. Plants have three types of meristematic tissue, each responsible for a different kind of growth.

Apical
⬆️
Apical Meristem
Located at the tips of roots and shoots. Divides continuously to increase the length of the plant. Responsible for growth in height (shoot tip) and depth (root tip).
Onion root tip experiment shows roots grow only from their tips
Lateral
↔️
Lateral Meristem
Arranged in a ring along the circumference of stems. Divides outward and inward to increase the girth (diameter) of the stem. Creates the annual growth rings visible in cross-sections of trees.
Annual rings in a cut tree trunk — each ring = one year's growth
Intercalary
🔄
Intercalary Meristem
Located at the base of internodes or above nodes. Allows plants to regenerate after being cut, grazed, or mowed. Grasses regrow after cutting because of this tissue.
Grass regrows after mowing; hedges become bushy after trimming
🧅 Activity 3.1 Onion Bulb Root Growth Experiment — Proving Apical Meristem
Two onion bulbs in water jars — Jar A untouched, Jar B with root tips cut on Day 3. Measure root length daily for 7 days.
Jar A (control): Roots continue growing in length throughout all 7 days.
Jar B (tips cut on Day 3): Roots stop growing after Day 3 — no more increase in length.
Conclusion: Roots grow ONLY from their tips. The tip contains the apical meristem — the zone of continuous cell division.
Link to Ch.2: Mitosis observed in onion root tip cells (Ch.2) confirms these tip cells are actively dividing.
Annual Rings 🌲 Reading a Tree's History in Its Growth Rings
The lateral meristem in a tree trunk creates new layers of wood each year. When you cut a tree, the cross-section shows concentric ring-like patterns — each ring represents one year of growth. Wide rings = favourable conditions (good rainfall, warm climate). Narrow rings = unfavourable conditions (drought, cold). Scientists use annual ring counts to estimate a tree's age and reconstruct past climate conditions — a science called dendrochronology.

Due to continuous division, meristematic tissue adds new cells to the plant body. Some cells remain meristematic; others lose the ability to divide and undergo changes in structure and function to become permanent tissues — specialised for support, transport, or storage. This process is called differentiation. Meristematic tissue becomes permanent tissue through differentiation.

Section 3.2.4 · Plant Tissues

Permanent Plant Tissues

Specialised tissues that no longer divide — each built for a specific job

Permanent tissues are of two types: Simple (made of only one type of cell) and Complex (made of more than one type of cell). They are organised into three major systems: Dermal (outer protection), Ground (main body), and Vascular (conducting/transport).

Protective Tissue — Epidermis
🛡️
Epidermis — Outermost Shield
A single layer of tightly packed, flat, rectangular cells forming the outermost layer of all plant parts. Covered by a waxy cuticle made of cutin — reduces water loss and protects against mechanical injury and parasites.
💧
Root Hair & Stomata
In roots, epidermal cells form root hairs — increasing surface area for absorbing water and minerals. In leaves, the epidermis contains stomata (pores) for gas exchange and transpiration. Transpiration creates a pull that draws water up through xylem.
🏜️
Thick Cuticle in Xerophytes
Plants in very dry (xeric) habitats develop a much thicker cuticle to reduce water loss. Desert plants like cacti have thick, waxy epidermis. Aquatic plants have thin/no cuticle — they need water to pass through freely.
Supporting Tissue — Simple Permanent Tissues
🟢
Parenchyma
Food Storage & Photosynthesis
Living cells with thin walls. Loosely packed with large intercellular spaces. Stores food in non-green parts. Performs photosynthesis in green parts (chlorenchyma). In aquatic plants, specialised parenchyma forms large air spaces (aerenchyma) to help the plant float.
The "flesh" of most fruits and vegetables is mostly parenchyma tissue
🔵
Collenchyma
Flexible Support
Living cells with unevenly thickened corners due to pectin deposition. Provides support and flexibility — allows stems and tendrils to bend without breaking. Pectin gives it rubber-like elasticity. Found in young stems, leaf stalks.
Coriander leaf stalks are soft and flexible because they contain collenchyma
🟤
Sclerenchyma
Rigid Strength
Mostly dead cells with very thick walls due to lignin deposition. Lignin makes them hard and strong — forms the woody structure. Found in stems, leaf veins, and hard coverings of seeds and nuts like coconut husk and walnut shell.
Coconut husk fibres are hard and brittle because they are sclerenchyma — rigid lignified walls

Section 3.2.4 (iii) · Complex Permanent Tissues

Xylem and Phloem — The Vascular Highway

Complex tissues that transport water and food across the entire plant

Xylem and phloem are called complex permanent tissues because they are made of more than one type of cell working together. Together they form the vascular tissue system — the plant's transport network. The key question: How does water reach the leaves of a tall tree? How does food made in leaves reach the roots?

Xylem · Water Highway ↑
Water & Mineral Transport
Transports water and dissolved minerals from roots upward to all parts of the plant. Also provides structural strength.
Tracheids & Vessels — tubular, thick-walled, dead at maturity; conduct water
Xylem Parenchyma — the ONLY living component; stores food
Xylem Fibres — sclerenchymatous; provide mechanical strength
Phloem · Food Highway ↕
Food (Sugar) Transport
Transports food (sugars) from leaves to all parts of the plant — both up and down. Mostly made of living cells.
Sieve Tubes — long tubular cells with perforated walls; transport sugars
Companion Cells — specialised parenchyma; regulate loading/unloading of sugars in sieve tubes
Phloem Parenchyma — stores food, resin, tannins, latex
Phloem Fibres — sclerenchymatous; provide mechanical support
Three Plant Tissue Systems
Dermal
🛡️
Dermal Tissue System
Forms the outer covering of the plant. Protects inner parts, reduces water loss, enables gas exchange.
Includes: Epidermis, root hairs, stomata, cuticle
Ground
🌿
Ground Tissue System
Forms the main body of the plant between dermal and vascular tissues. Stores food, provides support.
Includes: Parenchyma, collenchyma, sclerenchyma
Vascular
🔄
Vascular Tissue System
The plant's transport network. Conducts water, minerals, and food to all parts of the plant.
Includes: Xylem and Phloem

Ready to Go Beyond
🌲 Cork — The Bark of a Tree
In young plants, the outer protective layer is a single-layered epidermis. As plants age, cells below the epidermis develop the ability to divide and act as lateral meristematic cells — forming the cork cambium. Division of cork cambium produces cork cells. Cork cells are dead, compactly arranged, and contain a substance making them impermeable to water and gases. This forms the bark of the tree — a secondary protective layer replacing the epidermis as the plant grows thicker.
Section 3.3 · Animal Tissues

Animal Tissues — Four Fundamental Types

Every animal function — from movement to memory — is rooted in these four tissue types

🧱
Epithelial Tissue
Covering and lining tissue. Forms the outer body surface and lines all internal cavities. Tightly packed cells provide protection, enable exchange, secretion, absorption, and sensory functions.
🔗
Connective Tissue
Connects, supports, and binds. Most diverse tissue type. Includes blood, bone, cartilage, tendons, and ligaments. Each has a different matrix composition giving different physical properties.
💪
Muscular Tissue
Movement tissue. Specialised for contraction. Three types: skeletal (voluntary), smooth (involuntary), cardiac (heart). Each differs in structure, control, and location.
⚡
Nervous Tissue
Communication and control. Made of neurons — specialised to receive, process, and transmit electrical messages. Controls and coordinates all body activities.
Section 3.3.1 · Animal Tissues

Epithelial Tissues — Structure Meets Function

One tissue type, five different structural adaptations — each for a unique job

Epithelial tissue forms the outer covering of the body (skin) and lines all internal organs — mouth, lungs, blood vessels, intestine. It consists of closely packed cells with very little intercellular space. This dense arrangement prevents entry of germs, reduces water loss, and helps in absorption, secretion, and movement of substances.

🫁
Exchange Epithelium
Gas & Liquid Exchange
Single layer of thin, flat cells. Thin walls allow rapid diffusion of gases and liquids. Must be thin — thicker walls would slow exchange and reduce efficiency.
Location: Lining of blood vessels and alveoli in lungs
🧱
Protective Epithelium
Mechanical Protection
Multiple layers of flat, tightly packed cells. Outer cells are dead/keratinised for extra toughness. Resists friction, mechanical injury, and pathogen entry.
Location: Skin (epidermis), lining of mouth and oesophagus
🏭
Secretory Epithelium
Production & Release
Specialised cuboidal or columnar cells adapted for producing and releasing mucus, enzymes, hormones, sweat, and saliva.
Location: Salivary glands, sweat glands, stomach lining
👂
Sensory Epithelium
Smell, Taste, Sound, Balance
Specialised receptor cells with hair-like cilia. Detect stimuli and transmit signals to the nervous system. Cilia amplify the receptor surface area.
Location: Nostrils, taste buds, inner ear
🫃
Absorptive Epithelium
Nutrient & Water Uptake
Single layer of tall, pillar-like cells often with microvilli (finger-like projections). Maximises surface area for efficient nutrient and water uptake.
Location: Lining of small intestine (villi and microvilli)
Section 3.3.2 · Connective Tissue

Connective Tissues — The Body's Binding Framework

From blood to bone — the most diverse tissue type in the body

A tissue that connects and supports other tissues is called a connective tissue. All connective tissues have cells embedded in a matrix — and it's the matrix that determines the physical properties. Blood has a watery matrix; bone has a hard, mineral matrix. This is why both are connective tissues despite being so different.

🩸
Blood
Fluid Connective Tissue
Watery, fluid matrix (plasma — 55%). Contains formed elements: RBCs (carry O₂ via haemoglobin; live ~4 months), WBCs (immunity; collect at infections causing redness/swelling), Platelets (clotting). RBCs are red due to iron-rich haemoglobin.
During exercise, muscles need more O₂ — blood flow increases; face turns red
🦴
Bone
Rigid Connective Tissue
Hard, solid, rigid matrix containing calcium and phosphorus compounds. Provides strength, rigidity, and structural support. Protects vital organs (skull protects brain; rib cage protects heart and lungs). Forms the skeleton — 12–15% of adult body weight.
Skull, collar bone, long bone, kneecap — all different bone types
👂
Cartilage
Flexible Connective Tissue
Soft, jelly-like matrix. Provides flexibility and cushioning. Found in ear, nose tip, ends of bones at joints. Acts as shock absorber. Between vertebrae — cartilage discs allow spinal flexibility.
Press your ear or nose — soft structure that retains its shape = cartilage
🔗
Tendons
Muscle-to-Bone Connector
Tough, fibrous connective tissue. Connects muscle to bone. When a muscle contracts, the tendon transmits force to the bone — producing movement. Feel the Achilles tendon above your heel when you point your toes.
Tendons are designed to withstand enormous pulling forces
⛓️
Ligaments
Bone-to-Bone Connector
Strong, elastic connective tissue. Connects bone to bone at joints. Provides stability, limits movement, and prevents dislocation. Stretched to the limit when you raise your leg at the knee — the joint cannot go beyond what the ligament allows.
Ligament rupture is a common sports injury — causes joint instability

Section 3.3.4 · Nervous Tissue

Nervous Tissue — The Body's Control Network

How you think, sense, and respond — all in microseconds

When you pull your hand from something hot, or remember a song lyric, that's your nervous tissue at work. The brain is the control centre — coordinating activity, memory, and response. Muscles cannot function independently; they receive instructions from nervous tissue. During exercise, the brain signals the heart to beat faster to meet increased oxygen demand.

The Neuron ⚡ Structure of a Nerve Cell (Neuron)
The cells of nervous tissue are called neurons (nerve cells), specialised to receive, process, and transmit messages. Every neuron has three main parts:
Cell Body (Soma): Contains the nucleus and controls cell activities. The processing centre.
Dendrites: Short, branched projections from the cell body. Receive signals from other neurons and carry them toward the cell body.
Axon: A single long fibre carrying messages away from the cell body toward the axon terminals. Can be up to 1 metre long in the human body.
Axon Terminals: The end tips of the axon. Transmit messages to the next neuron or to a muscle/gland cell across a synapse.

Sections 3.4 – 3.6 · Musculoskeletal System

Joints, Muscles & the Skeletal System

How bones, muscles, and joints work together to produce coordinated movement

The musculoskeletal system = bones + muscles + joints + cartilage + tendons + ligaments. It helps us stand upright, move, maintain posture, and protect delicate organs. Movement is produced when muscles contract → tendons transmit force → bones move at joints. The entire system functions under the control of the nervous system. Adult human skeleton = ~12–15% of body weight.

Types of Joints
🔵
Ball and Socket Joint
Shoulder · Hip
Rounded head of one bone fits into a cup-shaped hollow of another. The shoulder joint: rounded top of humerus fits into the shallow hollow of the scapula.
✦ Movements: Forward, backward, sideways, circular (full range of motion)
🚪
Hinge Joint
Elbow · Knee
Like a door hinge — moves in only one plane. Elbow bends and straightens in one direction only. The kneecap (patella) protects the knee hinge joint.
✦ Movements: Bending and straightening only (one plane)
🔄
Pivot Joint
Neck (skull-backbone)
Skull connected to backbone through a pivot joint. Allows the head to turn side to side — like a doorknob rotating in its socket. Feel gentle movement at the back of your neck when you shake your head 'no.'
✦ Movements: Side-to-side rotation
🔒
Fixed Joint
Skull
Bones of the skull are joined by fixed joints — they cannot move. Forms a hard, protective case for the brain, eyes, and ears. No movement = maximum protection.
✦ Movements: None — permanently fused for protection

Vertebral Column (Backbone)

🦴
Skull
A hard case of flat bones joined by fixed joints. Protects the brain, eyes, and ears. The brain is the most important and most vulnerable organ — the skull's rigid protection ensures it stays safe even during vigorous movement.

Ready to Go Beyond
🩸 Bone Marrow Transplant — Stem Cells Saving Lives
Stem cells in the bone marrow are special cells that can divide and produce new blood cells continuously throughout life. In a bone marrow transplant, stem cells from a healthy, compatible donor are given to patients with blood cancers like Leukaemia or disorders like Thalassemia. The donor's stem cells migrate to the patient's bone marrow and begin producing healthy blood cells. This can be life-saving and represents one of the most powerful clinical applications of tissue science.

Indian Botanist · Plant Morphology & Anatomy
A renowned Indian botanist known for his contributions to plant morphology and anatomy. His scientific work explored the structural organisation of plant tissues and their relationship to function.

🔬
Indian Scientists Plant Tissue Culture
Meet Scientists
Sipra Guha Mukherjee & S. C. Maheshwari
Indian Scientists · Plant Tissue Culture Pioneers
In collaboration, they made a breakthrough discovery in plant tissue culture. Their research led to the development of a complete plant through anther culture — growing a whole plant from pollen cells (anthers) in an artificial nutrient medium under controlled laboratory conditions.
This pioneering work greatly contributed to crop improvement and modern agriculture. Anther culture allows scientists to create plants with specific genetic traits quickly, supporting plant breeding programs worldwide.
Key Takeaway
From understanding plant tissue structure and function, these scientists developed practical applications that feed billions. Basic tissue biology directly enables agricultural biotechnology.
🥕
Plant Biologist 1958 · Totipotency
Think as a Scientist
F. C. Steward
1904–1993 · American-British Plant Biologist
In 1958, Steward demonstrated that single cells taken from the vascular phloem of a carrot could be grown in a nutrient medium to produce a complete carrot plant — the first experimental proof of plant cell totipotency.
The phloem cells first dedifferentiated (regained the ability to divide), formed an undifferentiated mass (callus), then redifferentiated into roots, shoots, and eventually a full adult plant when grown in appropriate conditions with nutrients and growth chemicals (see Table 3.6 — light and air are both essential for maximum growth).
Key Takeaway
Every plant cell carries the complete genetic blueprint to become an entire organism. This is totipotency — and it underpins the entire modern plant tissue culture industry: propagating orchids, bananas, and genetically improved crops from single cells.

🌲
Threads of Curiosity
Annual Growth Rings — Trees as Climate Records
Every year a tree grows, its lateral meristem adds a new ring of wood. Wide rings indicate favourable conditions (good rainfall, warm temperatures); narrow rings indicate stress (drought, cold, pest attack). This science — called dendrochronology — allows scientists to read centuries of climate history from a single tree trunk. Ancient trees like bristlecone pines have provided climate data going back 5,000 years. The number of rings = the exact age of the tree. Each ring is a precise scientific record written by the lateral meristem.
🥕
Think as a Scientist · F.C. Steward's Experiment
From One Cell to an Entire Carrot Plant — Totipotency Proven
Steward's experiment used carrot phloem cells in a liquid nutrient medium with stirring (to shear off single cells), light, and air. The combination of light + air + liquid medium gave the maximum 20% increase in fresh weight. Without air (no O₂ for respiration) or without light (no photosynthesis for energy) — growth was reduced. This showed that totipotent cells need BOTH energy sources. Commercial applications include: (1) rapid mass propagation of disease-free banana and orchid plants; (2) production of genetically identical (clonal) elite crop varieties.
Bridging Science and Society
🌿 Crown Gall Disease → Genetic Engineering Tool
Crown gall disease causes tumour-like swellings on plant stems due to uncontrolled cell division caused by the bacterium Agrobacterium tumefaciens. Instead of only trying to cure the disease, scientists studied HOW the bacterium transfers its DNA into plant cells. This knowledge became the foundation of plant genetic engineering. Today, Agrobacterium is used as a biological delivery tool to introduce useful genes into crop plants — producing improved varieties, disease-resistant crops, and plants that produce valuable pharmaceutical compounds. A disease became a technology.
Ready to Go Beyond
🩸 Blood Components in Detail — Activity 3.2 Extended
Blood is a connective tissue with a fluid plasma matrix (55% of volume). RBCs (red, biconcave, no nucleus in mature form — maximises haemoglobin space) carry O₂. WBCs (white, various shapes) fight pathogens — when infected, they migrate to the site causing redness, swelling, and pus formation. Platelets (cell fragments, no nucleus) initiate blood clotting by releasing chemicals that start a cascade reaction forming a fibrin net that traps blood cells into a clot. All blood cells originate from stem cells in bone marrow — the same stem cells used in bone marrow transplants.
The Quest Continues…
🤔 Will It Be Possible to Obtain a Complete Animal from an Animal Cell?
Plants demonstrate totipotency — any cell can become a whole plant. For animals, the question is being actively researched. Most animal cells are NOT totipotent in the same way — they lose the ability to become any cell type as they differentiate. However, stem cells retain some of this ability. Embryonic stem cells can become any tissue. Adult stem cells (e.g., in bone marrow) can produce specific tissue types. The cloning of Dolly the sheep (1996) showed that a nucleus from an adult cell, when placed in an enucleated egg, could produce a full organism. Advantages of animal cell totipotency: regenerative medicine, organ printing, replacing damaged tissues. Challenges: ethical questions about cloning, genetic identity, safety, and ecological risk.

Skeletal: voluntary, striations, multinucleate. Smooth: involuntary, no striations, spindle-shaped. Cardiac: involuntary, branched, faint striations — never fatigues.

Animal — Nervous
Neurons: cell body (processing) + dendrites (receive) + axon (transmit). Signal travels: stimulus → dendrites → cell body → axon → axon terminals → response.
