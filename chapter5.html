Section 5.1 · Classification

How Can We Classify Mixtures?

Two fundamental types — defined by how their components are distributed

Homogeneous Mixture
Solution
Uniform composition throughout — every part looks and tastes the same. A well-stirred sugar-water solution is equally sweet in the first and last sip.
🧪 Examples: sugar in water, vinegar, aerated drinks (CO₂ in water)
Always remains uniform — a solution stays a solution
Heterogeneous Mixture
Non-Uniform
Non-uniform composition — different parts look or behave differently. Sand particles are visibly distinct in water and settle over time.
🪨 Examples: sand & water, oil & water, iron filings & sulfur
Components can often be seen with the naked eye
🔬 Activity 5.1 Group Experiment — Observe Three Mixtures with Laser Light
Three groups each prepare a mixture in a transparent beaker: Group A adds salt to water, Group B adds chalk powder to water, Group C adds a few drops of milk to water. A laser pointer is shone through each beaker and observed from the side.
Beaker A (salt + water): No visible particles. Laser beam path is NOT visible — light passes straight through. No settling. This is a true solution.
Beaker B (chalk + water): Particles visible. Laser beam path IS visible (light scattered). Particles settle on standing. This is a suspension.
Beaker C (milk + water): Appears homogeneous but laser path IS visible — particles scatter light. Does NOT settle. This is a colloid!
Safety First: Do not look directly into a laser beam — it can cause irreversible eye damage.
✨
True Solution
Particles smaller than 1 nm. Homogeneous, transparent. Does not scatter light. Cannot be filtered. Never settles. Examples: salt water, copper sulfate solution.
🪨
Suspension
Particles larger than 1000 nm. Heterogeneous, visible to naked eye. Scatters light. Can be filtered. Settles on standing. Examples: sand in water, chalk in water.
🥛
Colloid
Particles between 1–1000 nm. Appears homogeneous but scatters light (Tyndall Effect). Cannot be filtered. Does NOT settle. Examples: milk, blood, fog, smoke.
Section 5.2 · Solutions

Solutions — Homogeneous Mixtures

Solute + Solvent = Solution

A solution forms when a solute (substance that dissolves) is mixed into a solvent (substance that dissolves the solute). In sugar water, sugar is the solute and water is the solvent. The relative amounts matter — which is why we need to measure concentration.

ORS — Right Proportion Matters
Oral Rehydration Solution requires exact amounts of salt and sugar in a fixed volume of water. Change the ratio and it is no longer ORS — it won't work as intended for dehydration.
🌾
Pesticide Sprays
Farmers must mix the right concentration of pesticide in water. Too little fails to protect crops; too much damages crops, soil and the environment.
🩺
Saline Drip
Hospital saline drips use 0.9% m/v sodium chloride — exactly 0.9 g of salt in 100 mL of water. This concentration is isotonic (safe for blood) and replaces lost fluids.
Section 5.2.2 · Quantifying Solutions

Expressing Concentration

Three percentage methods — each suited to different real-world situations

% m/m or % w/w
⚖️
Mass by Mass
Grams of solute per 100 g of total solution. Used for solid solutes in solid or liquid solvents. Common on food labels for salt, sugar, protein content.
Formula: (mass of solute ÷ mass of solution) × 100
% m/v or % w/v
💉
Mass by Volume
Grams of solute per 100 mL of solution. Used in medicines and labs where measuring liquid volume is easier than weighing.
Formula: (mass of solute ÷ volume of solution) × 100
% v/v
🍾
Volume by Volume
Millilitres of solute per 100 mL of solution. Used when mixing two miscible liquids — perfumes, cosmetics, vinegar (5% v/v acetic acid).
Formula: (volume of solute ÷ volume of solution) × 100
Example 5.1 🧂 Mass by Mass Percentage — Salt in Water
10 g of salt is dissolved in 90 g of water. Calculate the mass by mass percentage.
Solution
Mass of solute (salt) = 10 g · Mass of solvent (water) = 90 g
Total mass of solution = 10 + 90 = 100 g

% m/m = (mass of solute ÷ mass of solution) × 100
= (10 ÷ 100) × 100 = 10% m/m
Example 5.2 🍬 Mass by Volume Percentage — Glucose Solution
5 g of glucose is dissolved in water to make 100 mL of solution. Calculate % m/v.
Solution
Mass of solute (glucose) = 5 g · Volume of solution = 100 mL
% m/v = (mass of solute ÷ volume of solution) × 100
= (5 ÷ 100) × 100 = 5% m/v — this is why IV glucose bags are labelled "5% w/v"
Example 5.3 🌾 Volume by Volume — Pesticide Spray
1 mL of liquid pesticide is mixed with water to make 100 mL of spray. Calculate % v/v.
Solution
Volume of solute (pesticide) = 1 mL · Total volume = 100 mL
% v/v = (1 ÷ 100) × 100 = 1% v/v

Section 5.2.3 · Solubility

Solubility of Substances

How much solute can dissolve — and what happens when you change temperature?

Key Definitions
Solubility
The maximum amount of solute that can dissolve in 100 mL or 100 g of solvent at a given temperature. Expressed in g per 100 g of water.
Saturated Solution
A solution that cannot dissolve any more solute at a given temperature. Adding more solute just makes it settle at the bottom.
🌡️
Solids in Liquids
Solubility of most solid solutes increases with temperature. Heating the solvent lets more solid dissolve — this principle is the basis of crystallization.
🫧
Gases in Liquids
Solubility of gases in liquid decreases with temperature. Hot water holds less dissolved oxygen — this is why warm rivers have less aquatic life.
📈
Solubility Curve
A graph of solubility vs temperature. Compound B (KNO₃-like) rises steeply; Compound A (NaCl-like) is nearly flat. Use the curve to predict how much solid crystallises on cooling.
📊 Activity 5.2 Interpreting Solubility Curves — Compound A and B
From solubility curves of Compound A and B in water (solubility in g per 100 g water vs temperature 10–80°C):
At all temperatures, Compound B dissolves much more than Compound A — B has higher solubility throughout
Compound A's solubility at 20°C is less than its solubility at 60°C (both increase, but A barely rises)
Compound B at 60°C: 287 g per 100 g water · At 40°C: 241 g per 100 g water — cooling precipitates 46 g of solid
Solubility of B increases more than that of A with temperature — B's curve is much steeper
Section 5.3 · Separation Methods

Separating Homogeneous Mixtures

Three powerful techniques for solutions and miscible liquid mixtures

5.3.1
❄️ Crystallization
The process of forming pure crystals from a saturated solution by cooling. A crystal is a solid with particles arranged in a regular geometric pattern. Used to separate two solids (where one is present in small quantity) and to purify solids.
Principle of Purification by Crystallization
Based on differences in solubility at different temperatures. At high temperature, more solid dissolves. On cooling, excess solid precipitates as pure crystals, leaving impurities in solution.

🔬 Activity 5.3 Growing Copper Sulfate Crystals in the Lab
Prepare blue, well-formed crystals of copper sulfate (CuSO₄) from a saturated solution using controlled slow cooling.
Step 1: Add 1 g copper sulfate to 25 mL water with a drop of dilute sulfuric acid (prevents side reactions). Heat gently in a water bath while stirring.
Step 2: Gradually add more copper sulfate until solution becomes saturated.
Step 3: Filter the hot solution to remove insoluble impurities. Cover the filtrate with a watch glass.
Step 4: Let the solution cool SLOWLY — slow cooling allows larger, well-shaped shiny blue crystals to form.
Step 5: Filter, rinse with cold water, dry on a watch glass. Result: large, blue copper sulfate crystals!
⚠️ Safety: Copper sulfate is toxic. Do not touch with bare hands. Perform under adult supervision. Sulfuric acid must be added by the teacher only.
🧬 Think as a Scientist
Hypothesis: Rapid cooling in ice-cold water produces smaller, less well-formed crystals compared to slow cooling at room temperature.

Experiment Design: Prepare a hot saturated solution of copper sulfate. Divide it into two equal parts. Cool Part 1 slowly at room temperature and Part 2 rapidly in ice-cold water. Compare crystal size and shape after filtering and drying.
Ready to Go Beyond — Natural Crystals
Crystals in Nature and in India
Crystals occur in mines, caves, and the Earth's crust. Mawsmai Cave in Sohra (Cherrapunji) has fascinating natural crystal formations. Quartz crystals are among the most beautiful. Rock salt, candy sugar (mishri), snowflakes and frost are all crystals you may have seen.

Salt crystallization was an ancient process in coastal India. Panga salt was made by boiling concentrated sea brines; karkatch salt was obtained by evaporation of seawater — producing salt crystals of different sizes.

India's Scientific Contributions
Mitti ka Ittar — Kannauj, the Perfume Capital of India
The pleasant petrichor — earthy smell after the first rain — is captured in Kannauj (Uttar Pradesh) and turned into a natural perfume called Mitti ka Ittar. The traditional Deg-Bhapka method of distillation, passed down through generations, extracts fragrance from flowers, leaves and other plant parts. The Fragrance and Flavour Development Centre in Kannauj supports fragrance farmers and entrepreneurs. This ancient Indian distillation expertise is in demand worldwide.
Ready to Go Beyond — Petroleum Refinery
Fractional Distillation of Crude Petroleum
Crude oil extracted from Earth's crust is separated into fractions by fractional distillation in a petroleum refinery. The fractionating column separates: petroleum gas → petrol → aviation fuel (kerosene) → diesel → lubricating oil → bitumen (bottom). The gaseous fraction is pressurised into steel cylinders as LPG (Liquefied Petroleum Gas) — the domestic fuel used in kitchens across India.
5.3.3
🎨 Paper Chromatography
Used to separate coloured components of a mixture. Works by exploiting differences in the interactions of each component with the paper and the solvent — components that interact more strongly with the solvent travel faster up the paper.

The word chromatography comes from the Greek chroma (colour) + graphein (to write) — meaning "writing with colour." It was first used to separate coloured dyes and inks.

🎨 Activity 5.5 Separating Ink Components with Paper Chromatography
Investigate whether black sketch pen ink is a single colour or a mixture of pigments.
Step 1: Take a 3 cm wide strip of chromatography paper (or filter paper). Draw a pencil line 2 cm from the bottom.
Step 2: Mark a spot with black sketch pen at the centre of the pencil line.
Step 3: Place the strip vertically in a beaker/gas jar with enough water so the water level is BELOW the ink spot (critical!).
Step 4: As water rises up the paper, it carries different pigments at different speeds — the ink separates into coloured bands.
Inference: Black ink is NOT a single colour — it is a mixture of multiple pigments. Different pigments travel at different rates due to different interactions with paper and water.
Extensions: Try green food colour with 2% m/v salt solution as solvent. Try pigments from spinach leaves or flower petals. Some pigments need alcohol instead of water as solvent.

Section 5.4 · Heterogeneous Mixtures

Separating Heterogeneous Mixtures

Five techniques — each exploiting a different physical property

🫙
Separating Funnel — Immiscible Liquids
Oil and water don't mix — they form separate layers by density. Lower-density liquid (oil) sits on top; higher-density (water) at bottom. Open the stopcock to drain the lower layer, close, then drain the upper. Used for mustard oil + water mixtures.
🕯️
Sublimation — Camphor & Sand
Sublimation: solid → vapour without becoming liquid. On cooling: Deposition: vapour → solid. Camphor sublimes when heated; sand doesn't. Vapour collects on inverted funnel; sand stays in dish. Also works for naphthalene, dry ice.
🌀
Centrifugation — Suspensions
Spinning a mixture at high speed forces heavier particles outward to the bottom of the tube (centrifugal force). Lighter liquid remains on top. Used to separate blood components (red cells, plasma, platelets), cream from milk, and in labs.
🪨
Coagulation — Muddy Water
Adding a coagulant (like alum/fitkari) to muddy water causes fine suspended particles to clump together (coagulate). Larger clumps settle by gravity (sedimentation) and can be separated by decantation or filtration. Used in water treatment plants.
🧀
Coagulation in Food
Making paneer (cheese) from milk uses coagulation — lemon juice or vinegar acts as a coagulant that causes milk proteins to clump together. The solid protein clumps are then filtered out as paneer.
⚗️
Alloys — Cannot Be Separated
Alloys are homogeneous mixtures of metals (or metal + non-metal). Physical methods CANNOT separate them. Examples: brass (Cu 80% + Zn 20%), bronze (Cu 80% + Sn 20%), stainless steel (Fe + C + Cr + Ni + Mo). Made to be stronger, rigid, or corrosion-resistant.
🫙 Activity 5.6 Separating Mustard Oil from Water with a Separating Funnel
Pour 5 mL mustard oil + 20 mL water into a 50 mL separating funnel. Let stand undisturbed.
Two layers form: yellow mustard oil on top (lower density), water at the bottom (higher density).
Open stopcock slowly → water drains into a collection flask. Close when water is nearly all drained.
Discard the small mixed portion at the interface, then open the stopcock again to collect the oil separately.
🕯️ Activity 5.7 Sublimation of Camphor from Sand
Crushed camphor + sand mixture in a china dish. An inverted glass funnel (nozzle plugged with cotton) covers the dish on a tripod stand.
Heat gently with a burner. Camphor converts directly from solid to vapour (sublimation) below its melting point.
Vapour rises and deposits as white solid camphor on the cooler inner walls of the funnel (deposition).
Sand remains in the china dish — it does not sublime. Camphor is collected from the funnel. Clean separation!
Bridging Science and Society
🔬 The Paperfuge — Centrifugation Without Electricity
Inspired by a simple spinning toy, scientists developed the paperfuge — a hand-powered centrifuge made from cardboard and string. By spinning blood samples at very high speed, it separates heavier components from lighter ones, just like a lab centrifuge. This low-cost device can detect malaria and anaemia in remote areas without access to electricity — bringing life-saving diagnostics to underserved communities worldwide.
🛠️ Activity 5.8 Make a Model Centrifuge with Cardboard & Thread
Use a cardboard disc and thick thread to create a spinning centrifuge model. When spun, observe how objects placed on it move outward — demonstrating centrifugal force. Decide which mixture you would like to separate using your mini centrifuge!
🌊 Activity 5.4 Salt Manufacturing — From Seawater to Crystals
Observe the three steps in the industrial salt-making process and describe them in your own words.
Step 1 — Seawater: Seawater is collected in large shallow ponds (salt pans) near the sea.
Step 2 — Saturated Solution: Sun and wind evaporate the water, concentrating the salt until the solution is saturated.
Step 3 — Salt Crystals: Continued evaporation causes salt to crystallise out. Crystals are collected, washed and dried.
Sections 5.4.3 – 5.5 · Colloids & Light

Suspensions, Colloids and the Tyndall Effect

Understanding the three types of mixtures — and how light reveals the difference

5.4.3 — Suspensions
Heterogeneous mixtures where solid particles do not dissolve but remain suspended throughout a liquid medium. Particles are larger than 1000 nm, visible to the naked eye, and settle on standing. Examples: muddy water, chalk in water, sawdust in water, tea leaves in water. Can be separated by filtration — residue stays on filter paper.

💡 The Tyndall Effect
Named after scientist John Tyndall, who first explained the scattering of light by colloidal particles. When a beam of light passes through a colloid or suspension, the particles scatter the light — making the beam's path visible from the side. True solutions do NOT show this effect as particles are too small to scatter visible light.
Everyday Examples
• Sunlight through gaps in dense tree leaves
• Floodlights in a sports stadium (dust in air)
• Car headlights in fog
• Light entering a dark room through a small hole
Key Rule
Light scatters in colloid ✓
Light scatters in suspension ✓
Light does NOT scatter in true solution ✗

Use a laser pointer to tell them apart!
🩸
Blood — A Colloid
Blood is neither a solution nor a suspension — it is a colloid. Blood cells (dispersed phase) are suspended in plasma (dispersion medium). Components can be separated by centrifugation: plasma at top, platelets + white cells in middle, red cells at bottom.
🌊
Dispersed Phase & Medium
Every colloid has two components: the dispersed phase (solute-like particles, 1–1000 nm) and the dispersion medium (the continuous phase they're suspended in).
🧴
Emulsions
Colloids with liquid dispersed in liquid. Oil-in-water: milk, vanishing cream. Water-in-oil: butter, cold cream, body lotion. Stabilised by emulsifying agents (proteins in milk and butter).
🌫️
Threads of Curiosity
Why Do Cities with Smoke Look Hazy?
Smoke and dust in city air create a colloidal system. When light passes through it, particles scatter the light in all directions — this is the Tyndall Effect on a massive scale. The more particles in the air, the hazier the city looks. Clouds are also colloids — tiny water droplets (1–1000 nm) suspended in air, which is why they scatter light and look white!
Bridging Science and Society
🩸 Donate Blood — Science that Saves Lives
Donated blood is centrifuged to separate its components: plasma (top layer), platelets + white blood cells (middle), and red blood cells (bottom). These are stored in blood banks and supplied to patients in emergencies, surgeries or illness. The body naturally replaces donated blood within weeks. Do you know your blood group?

Meet a Scientist

Scientist Spotlight

The Indian scientist who revolutionised rehydration therapy

Meet a Scientist
Dilip Mahalanabis
Indian Paediatrician · Pioneer of Oral Rehydration Therapy
Dr Dilip Mahalanabis, an Indian paediatrician, was the first to develop and implement the treatment for severe dehydration caused by diseases like diarrhoea and cholera. He formulated Oral Rehydration Solution (ORS) — a precisely concentrated mixture of salt, sugar and water — and successfully used it during the 1971 Bangladesh refugee crisis when intravenous fluids were unavailable.

After the World Health Organization (WHO) popularised ORS worldwide, it has saved millions of lives. The solution demonstrates the profound real-world importance of concentration — the exact proportions of solute to solvent are what make ORS effective, not just any sweet salty drink.

Key Point: Not all sugary drinks prepared at home or sold commercially are ORS. Only the precisely formulated version works for dehydration treatment.
