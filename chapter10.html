Section 10.1 · Source of Sound

Production of Sound

All sound originates from vibration — no vibration, no sound

The Fundamental Fact: Sound = Vibration

Sound is a form of energy produced when an object vibrates — that is, undergoes periodic to-and-fro motion (oscillations). The moment vibration stops, so does the sound. The vibrating object is called the source of the sound. Sound can be produced by vibrating strings, membranes, air columns, and many other objects.

🔬 Activity 10.1 Rubber Band on Cardboard Box
A rubber band is stretched across the open side of a cardboard box. The band is plucked while it is vibrating and again after it stops.
Key Observation: Sound is produced only while the rubber band is vibrating. Once vibration stops, sound stops completely.
Tension Effect: Increasing tension (stretching more) changes the sound — the pitch becomes higher, indicating the frequency increases.
Box Role: The box amplifies the sound. Plucking the band without the box between two fingers produces much softer sound.
Conclusion: Vibration is necessary and sufficient to produce sound. The box acts as a resonator that amplifies the vibrating band's sound.
🎸
Strings & Membranes
Plucking a stretched string (guitar, sitar, rubber band) or striking a membrane (tabla, drum) causes vibration. The to-and-fro motion disturbs surrounding air particles, creating sound.
🎷
Air Columns
Blowing through a bansuri (flute) or any hollow pipe causes the air column inside to vibrate, producing sound. Musical wind instruments work on this principle.
🗣️
Vocal Cords — Human Voice
In humans, sound is produced by the vibration of vocal cords — tightly stretched muscular flaps in the voice box (larynx) in the throat. The tongue, lips, mouth and nasal cavity shape sound into speech.
10.1.1 Tuning Fork — The Sound Laboratory Tool
🔬 Activity 10.2 Tuning Fork — Vibration Made Visible
A U-shaped metal bar with a stem made of steel or aluminium. The two arms are called prongs or tines. A prong is struck against a soft rubber pad (never a hard surface — it may damage the fork) and brought near the ear or water surface.
Near the ear: Sound is heard as long as prongs vibrate. Sound stops when prongs stop vibrating.
Prong touching water: Waves form on the water surface — visual proof that the prong is vibrating even when you can't see it clearly.
Different orientations: Sound is heard regardless of how the tuning fork is oriented → sound propagates in multiple directions from the source.

10.2.1 Sound Cannot Travel Through Vacuum
🔬 Vacuum Bell Jar Experiment Definitive Proof — Sound Needs a Medium
An electric bell is placed inside a bell jar connected to a vacuum pump. The bell is switched on while the air is gradually removed.
With air: Bell is heard clearly.
As air is removed: Sound gradually becomes fainter, even though the bell can still be seen ringing.
Near-vacuum: Almost no sound is heard — even though the bell is clearly vibrating.
Air let back in: Sound gradually returns to original loudness.
Conclusion: Sound needs a material medium to propagate. In vacuum (no medium), sound cannot travel.

The material through which sound propagates is called a medium. A space with no medium (no matter) is called a vacuum. Since outer space is near-vacuum, astronauts on spacewalks cannot hear each other speak or hear metal clanking — they communicate through special radio devices fitted in their spacesuits. This is why space explosion scenes in movies with loud sound are physically incorrect!

Section 10.3 · Wave Nature

Sound Waves — Compressions and Rarefactions

How does sound actually move through a medium? It's all about density — not particle travel

🔬 Activity 10.5 The Slinky — A Perfect Sound Wave Analogy
A slinky (long flexible spring) is held stretched between two people. One person gives a sharp push-and-pull at their end, simulating a sound source vibrating.
Closer turns: Regions where the slinky's coils are compressed closer together — analogous to a compression in air (higher density region).
Spread-out turns: Regions where coils are more spread apart — analogous to a rarefaction in air (lower density region).
The marked turn: A marker on one coil shows it oscillates back and forth about its own position — it does NOT travel with the wave. Only the disturbance travels.
Continuous pushing/pulling: Produces a series of alternating compressions and rarefactions travelling along the slinky — exactly like a continuous sound wave.
🔵
Compression (C)
When the piston (or any vibrating surface) moves forward, it pushes nearby air particles together, creating a region of higher density than average. This high-density region is called a compression.
⚪
Rarefaction (R)
When the piston moves backward, nearby air particles spread out, creating a region of lower density than average. This low-density region is called a rarefaction.
〰️
Sound Wave = C + R Alternating
An oscillating source continuously creates alternating compressions and rarefactions. This series of disturbances propagating through the medium — without the actual flow of medium particles — is called a sound wave.

Section 10.4 · Energy Transfer

Energy of Sound Waves

Sound is a form of energy — it can move objects without touching them

🔬 Activity 10.6 Sound Moving Grains — Energy Proof
A cellophane or rubber sheet (from a balloon) is stretched tightly over a wide-mouthed container. Grains (rice, salt, chalk powder) are sprinkled on the sheet. A loud sound is produced near the bowl without touching it.
Observation: The grains on the sheet move or jump — even though the sound source never touches the sheet or container.
Why? Sound waves travel through air and set the sheet vibrating. The vibrating sheet's energy is transferred to the grains, causing them to move.
Louder sound: Grains jump higher — more energy is carried by louder sound.
Conclusion: Sound is a form of energy. When sound waves propagate, energy is transferred through the medium — NOT the particles themselves.
Bridging Science and Society
Microphones and Speakers — Sound ↔ Electricity
A microphone converts sound energy to electrical energy. When we speak into it, sound waves make a thin membrane (diaphragm) vibrate, which generates an electrical signal. A speaker does the reverse — an electrical signal makes a cone/diaphragm vibrate, producing sound that closely matches the original. This microphone–amplifier–speaker chain is the backbone of all audio technology: phones, concerts, hearing aids, and cinema.

Section 10.5 · Wave Graphs

Graphical Representation of a Sound Wave

Plotting density variation against distance — turning invisible waves into visible graphs

As a sound wave propagates, the density of the medium varies periodically with distance from the source at any given instant. Plotting density on the Y-axis against distance on the X-axis produces a wave-shaped graph. The horizontal dashed line represents the average density of the undisturbed medium.

🔝
Crest
The highest point on the density graph. Corresponds to a region of maximum density — i.e., a compression (C). The density here is highest above average.
🔻
Trough
The lowest point on the density graph. Corresponds to a region of minimum density — i.e., a rarefaction (R). The density here is lowest below average.
📊
Reading the Graph
Crests → Compressions (C). Troughs → Rarefactions (R). Average density line → undisturbed medium. Distance between consecutive crests or troughs = one wavelength (λ).

Section 10.6 · Wave Properties

Characteristics of a Sound Wave

Wavelength, frequency, time period, amplitude — the language of sound

Wavelength (λ)
λ in metres (m)
Distance between two consecutive crests (or two consecutive troughs). SI unit: metre (m). Symbol: λ (lambda)
Frequency (ν)
ν = oscillations / time
Number of complete density oscillations per unit time at a fixed point. SI unit: hertz (Hz) = per second (s⁻¹). Symbol: ν (nu)
Time Period (T)
T = 1 / ν
Time for one complete density oscillation at a fixed point. SI unit: second (s). Inversely related to frequency.
Amplitude
Max Δdensity from avg
Maximum change in density in a compression (or rarefaction) compared to average density. Larger amplitude = more energy = louder sound.
Intensity
Energy / (area × time)
Amount of sound energy passing through a unit area perpendicular to propagation direction per unit time. Decreases with distance from source.
Key Relation
ν = 1/T
Higher frequency = shorter time period. A whistle (high pitch) has higher frequency and shorter time period than a bass drum (low pitch).
🔬 Activity 10.7 Phyphox App — Measuring Musical Note Frequencies
Using the Phyphox app's 'Audio Spectrum' feature, sing the musical notes Sa, Re, Ga, Ma, Pa, Dha, Ni, Sa and observe their frequencies. The 'Audio Scope' feature when whistling carefully produces a graph that looks exactly like a single-frequency wave — proving whistling is nearly a pure tone.
Key finding: Frequency is lowest for 'Sa' and gradually increases for higher notes. Each musical note has a distinct, specific frequency.
Ratio pattern: Musical notes follow mathematical ratios — the frequency of each note relates to 'Sa' in a specific ratio, giving music its mathematical beauty.
Example 10.1 🎵 Frequency and Time Period from Oscillations
If there are 10 density oscillations in 2 seconds at a given position, calculate: (i) frequency of the sound wave, and (ii) its time period.
Solution
(i) Frequency = number of oscillations ÷ time taken = 10 ÷ 2 s = 5 Hz

(ii) Time period T = 1/ν = 1/5 = 0.2 s
(Or: T = total time ÷ number of oscillations = 2 s ÷ 10 = 0.2 s)
10.6.2 Amplitude and Intensity
🔉
Low Amplitude → Soft Sound
Small change in density (small crests and troughs in graph). Less energy transferred. The sheet in Activity 10.6 vibrates gently, grains barely move.
🔊
High Amplitude → Loud Sound
Large change in density (tall crests and deep troughs in graph). More energy transferred. Grains jump high. Sounds can travel farther before becoming inaudible.
📡
Intensity Decreases with Distance
As a sound wave spreads from its source, the same energy covers an ever-larger spherical area → intensity (energy per unit area per unit time) decreases with distance.

Section 10.6.3 · Speed

Speed of Sound

v = λν — how far the disturbance travels per unit time, and how medium, temperature and humidity matter

The Wave Speed Equation
v = λ × ν
speed = wavelength × frequency

ν

Derivation: In one time period T, the wave travels exactly one wavelength λ. So v = λ/T. Since ν = 1/T, we get v = λν.

🌡️
Effect of Temperature
Speed of sound increases as temperature increases. In dry air: 331 m s⁻¹ at 0°C → 344 m s⁻¹ at 22°C. Warmer air particles move faster, transmitting vibrations more quickly.
💧
Effect of Humidity
Speed of sound increases as humidity increases. Humid air (water vapour) is less dense than dry air, so the disturbance travels faster through it.
🔒
Speed is Constant for a Given Medium
In air, the speed depends only on the medium conditions (temp, humidity) — not on the source's frequency. If frequency changes, wavelength adjusts while speed stays constant.
Example 10.2 👂 Human Hearing Range — Wavelengths in Air
Human hearing spans 20 Hz to 20 kHz. What are the corresponding wavelengths in air? (Speed of sound = 344 m s⁻¹)
Solution
λ = v / ν

(i) For ν = 20 Hz: λ = 344 / 20 = 17.2 m
(ii) For ν = 20,000 Hz: λ = 344 / 20000 = 0.0172 m = 1.72 cm

Human audible wavelengths range from 1.72 cm to 17.2 m.
Example 10.3 ⚡ Lightning and Thunder — Distance Calculation
Lightning is seen 5 s before thunder is heard. If speed of sound = 340 m s⁻¹, how far away did lightning strike? (Light reaches almost instantaneously.)
Solution
Distance = v × t = 340 m s⁻¹ × 5 s = 1700 m = 1.7 km
The lightning struck about 1.7 km away. This is how the "5-second rule" for estimating storm distance works!
Example 10.4 🔩 Sound Wave in Steel — λ, ν, T from Graph
A sound wave in steel has wavelength λ = 50 m (from graph). Speed of sound in steel = 5000 m s⁻¹. Find frequency and time period.
Solution
ν = v / λ = 5000 / 50 = 100 Hz
T = 1 / ν = 1 / 100 = 0.01 s

Section 10.6.4 · Perception

Human Perception of Sound

Pitch, loudness, timbre — how we subjectively experience the objective properties of sound

Infrasound
< 20 Hz
Humans cannot hear. Detected by elephants. Used to detect earthquakes, volcanic eruptions, and severe storms (travels great distances).
Audible Range
20 Hz – 20 kHz
Human hearing range. Varies person to person; decreases with age. Normal conversation ≈ 1–4 kHz. Musical range ≈ 20 Hz–20 kHz.
Ultrasound
> 20 kHz
Humans cannot hear. Detected by dogs, cats, bats, dolphins. Used in ultrasonography, kidney stone breaking, industrial cleaning, and SONAR.
🎶
Pitch — Perception of Frequency
How humans perceive frequency is called pitch. High-frequency sounds are perceived as shrill (high pitch — whistle, siren). Low-frequency sounds are deep (low pitch — thunder, bass). A "shriller" sound has higher frequency.
🔊
Loudness — Perception of Amplitude
How humans perceive amplitude is called loudness. Larger amplitude = louder sound. Loudness decreases with distance from source. Measured in decibels (dB). Rustling leaves ≈ few dB, conversation ≈ 60 dB, firecrackers > 100 dB.
🎼
Timbre — The Quality of Sound
Even at same pitch and loudness, a flute sounds different from a tabla. This unique quality is called timbre — determined by the pattern and intensity of overtones, which depend on shape, material, and construction of the instrument.
🗣️
Threads of Curiosity
Why Every Voice Sounds Unique
Everyone's voice is unique — you can instantly recognise your teacher or friend. Voices differ not just in frequency but in how sound is shaped by the throat, mouth, and nasal cavities. During adolescence, boys' vocal cords lengthen and thicken, vibrating less frequently — this "deepens" their voice. Male, female, and children's voices also differ in timbre, making each person's voice an acoustic fingerprint.
🎵
Threads of Curiosity
Tone, Note, Overtone and Octave
A tone is a single-frequency sound (tuning fork, careful whistling). A musical note is a combination of a fundamental frequency and higher overtones — creating a rich, pleasant sound. Indian string instruments like the sarangi, sitar, and veena use extra strings to enrich this combination. An octave is the interval between two notes where one has double the frequency of the other (e.g., 200 Hz and 400 Hz). The tabla and mridangam have a black patch called the syaahi at the centre of their membrane, giving them extraordinary tonal control.
🔬 Activity 10.8 Listening to Sounds at Different Frequencies
Using a sound-generating app, set frequencies from 100 Hz to 1000 Hz in steps of 100 Hz and listen carefully. Then decrease from 50 Hz toward 20 Hz.
As frequency increases from 100 Hz to 1000 Hz, sound becomes progressively shriller (higher pitch).

Bridging Science and Society
Noise Pollution and Hearing Health
Unwanted or harmful sound is called noise. Noise pollution is a severe public health problem. Prolonged exposure to sounds above recommended levels (especially above 85 dB) can cause permanent hearing loss, which can be tested using audiograms. People with hearing loss may use hearing aids — devices with a microphone, amplifier, and speaker. The human ear's eardrum vibrates when sound enters; tiny bones amplify these vibrations, and the cochlea converts them to electrical signals sent to the brain. Two ears help the brain pinpoint the direction of a sound by comparing the tiny time gap (often < 1 ms) between when each ear receives the sound.

Section 10.7 · Reflection

Reflection of Sound — Echo and Reverberation

Sound bounces off hard surfaces just like light — and the results range from echoes in mountains to perfect concert hall acoustics

Sound follows the same laws of reflection as light: the angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection, and both lie in the same plane as the normal. When sound reflects off a distant hard surface and returns to the listener, it is called an echo.

Echo
🏔️
Clear Distinct Repeat
Time gap between original sound and reflected sound ≥ 0.1 s. Brain can distinguish them as separate sounds. Requires minimum distance of 17 m from reflecting surface.
Shouting near a mountain, cliff, or long empty corridor
Reverberation
🎭
Multiple Rapid Reflections
Multiple reflections arrive with time difference < 0.05 s. Cannot be distinguished individually — sound appears to persist after source stops. Common in large halls.
Auditoriums, concert halls, cathedrals
No Echo
🛋️
Absorbed / Scattered
Soft surfaces (curtains, upholstered chairs) absorb sound. Rough surfaces scatter it in many directions. Small rooms: reflections arrive too quickly to separate from original.
Carpeted rooms, recording studios with acoustic foam

Example 10.5 👏 Echo — Distance to Wall
You clap in an empty corridor and hear an echo after 0.5 s. Speed of sound = 340 m s⁻¹. How far is the wall?
Solution
Sound travels to wall AND back in 0.5 s
Distance to wall = (v × t) / 2 = (340 × 0.5) / 2 = 170 / 2 = 85 m

Section 10.8 · Applications

Ultrasonic and Infrasonic Waves & Their Applications

Beyond human hearing lies a world of extraordinary technology — from medical imaging to ocean depth mapping

Applications of Sound Outside the Audible Range
🌋
Infrasound — Natural Disaster Detection
Infrasonic waves (<20 Hz) travel very long distances through air and the Earth. Used to detect earthquakes and volcanic eruptions early, and to track severe storms. Elephants use infrasound to communicate over tens of kilometres.
🏥
Ultrasound — Medical Imaging
Ultrasonography uses ultrasonic waves to image internal organs without surgery. High-frequency sound reflects off different tissue boundaries, building up an image. Used for monitoring fetal development, detecting tumours and organ abnormalities.
🪨
Kidney Stone Breaking
Focused ultrasonic waves can break kidney stones into smaller fragments (lithotripsy) that the body can then pass out naturally through urine — avoiding surgery completely.
🔧
Industrial Cleaning
Delicate machine parts (watches, surgical instruments, PCBs) are placed in a liquid and exposed to ultrasonic waves. The rapid pressure variations clean even tiny crevices without mechanical scrubbing — ultrasonic cleaning.
🔍
Defect Detection in Metals
Ultrasound is used to detect internal cracks or defects in metal components during construction and industrial testing — without breaking or cutting the material. Non-destructive testing (NDT).
🦇
Echolocation & SONAR
Using reflected ultrasonic waves to locate objects. Used by bats, dolphins, and whales in nature. Adapted by humans as SONAR (Sound Navigation and Ranging) for underwater exploration.
10.8.1 Echolocation — Nature's SONAR
🦇 Echolocation How Bats Navigate in the Dark
Most bats are nocturnal — they hunt insects in complete darkness without colliding with objects. They emit short bursts of ultrasonic waves. When these waves reflect off nearby objects or prey, the bat's sensitive ears pick up the echoes. By analysing the time delay and direction of echoes, bats determine the position, distance, and movement of prey.
Principle: Time between emitted pulse and received echo → distance to object. Direction of echo → direction of object.
Other echolocators: Dolphins, whales, some birds (oilbirds, cave swiftlets) also use echolocation for navigation and hunting.
Human application: The same principle is used in SONAR, ultrasonic parking sensors in vehicles, and medical ultrasound imaging.
SONAR — Sound Navigation and Ranging

Ultrasonic waves are emitted into water from a ship. They travel to the sea floor or underwater objects and reflect back. By measuring the time between emission and reception, the distance, direction, and speed of underwater objects (submarines, shipwrecks, schools of fish, ocean floor) can be determined.

This method — audio surveillance — helps monitor airspace for safety and security without requiring visual confirmation. It's a real-world application of frequency analysis and sound detection systems.

Meet the Scientist

Sir C. V. Raman — India's First Science Nobel Laureate

Nobel Prize in Physics · 1930
Sir C. V. Raman
Indian Physicist · Chandrasekhar Venkata Raman · 1888–1970
Sir C. V. Raman won India's first Nobel Prize in Science for discovering the Raman Effect in light — a phenomenon of inelastic scattering of photons when light interacts with molecules. Beyond optics, Raman made important contributions to acoustics by scientifically studying Indian percussion instruments. He analysed the tabla and mridangam to understand how their construction — particularly the syaahi (the black patch at the centre of the drumhead membrane) — allows them to produce extraordinarily rich, nuanced, and tonally precise sounds. His work showed that these ancient Indian instruments achieved acoustic sophistication that rivalled (and in some ways exceeded) Western instruments. Raman's interdisciplinary genius bridged pure physics and the classical arts of India.
🥁
Threads of Curiosity
The Science of the Tabla — The Syaahi
Indian drums like the tabla and mridangam have a unique black patch at the centre of their membrane called the syaahi — a mixture of rice paste, iron powder, and other materials applied in layers. This patch alters the vibration of the membrane in a special way: it allows these instruments to produce a rich variety of distinct musical tones and gives a level of tonal control rarely found in any other drum worldwide. C. V. Raman scientifically explained why the tabla sounds so uniquely musical while most other drums do not.

Sound is produced by vibrating objects. Vibration stops → sound stops. Sources include strings, membranes, air columns, and vocal cords. The object producing sound is the source.
〰️
Longitudinal Mechanical Wave
Sound is a longitudinal wave (particles vibrate parallel to propagation direction) and a mechanical wave (needs a medium). Cannot travel through vacuum. Travels as alternating compressions (C) and rarefactions (R).
📐
Wave Equations
ν = 1/T (frequency and time period). v = λν (speed = wavelength × frequency). Wavelength = distance between two consecutive crests or troughs. SI units: λ in m, ν in Hz, T in s, v in m s⁻¹.
🎚️
Amplitude & Intensity
Amplitude = max density change from average. Larger amplitude = more energy = louder sound. Intensity = energy per unit area per unit time. Intensity decreases with distance from source.
🔊
Echo & Reverberation
Echo: reflection heard as separate sound (gap ≥ 0.1 s, distance ≥ 17 m). Reverberation: multiple rapid reflections in large halls (gap < 0.05 s). Hard smooth surfaces reflect better; soft porous materials absorb sound.
🦇
Ultrasound Applications
Ultrasound (>20 kHz): medical imaging, kidney stone breaking, industrial cleaning, defect detection. Echolocation: bats, dolphins. SONAR: distance = (v × t) / 2. Infrasound (<20 Hz): earthquake and storm detection.
