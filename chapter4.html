Section 4.1 · Overview

Motion in Nature — From Simple to Complex

Why scientists start with the simplest, idealised form

Nature presents motion in extraordinary variety — butterflies, snakes, hares, horses, ocean tides, gathering clouds, dancing dust in a sunbeam. To study such complexity, scientists first work with idealised simplified forms. The three fundamental types are linear (straight line), circular, and oscillatory. This chapter focuses on linear motion and uniform circular motion.

➡️
Linear Motion
Motion in a straight line — the simplest kind. Examples: children in a swimming race, a ball falling vertically, a car on a straight highway, a train on a straight track. Also called motion in one dimension.
🔄
Circular Motion
Motion along a circular path — a child on a merry-go-round, a satellite orbiting Earth, a vehicle turning. A special case: uniform circular motion where speed is constant but direction keeps changing.
〰️
Oscillatory Motion
Back-and-forth motion about a central point — a pendulum, a vibrating string, a spring. You will study this in later chapters. All three types of motion serve as foundations for understanding more complex real-world motion.
Section 4.1.1 · Describing Position

Position, Reference Point, Motion and Rest

Everything begins with knowing where something is

To describe position, you first need a reference point (also called the origin). The position of an object is described by its distance AND direction from that reference point at any instant of time. Direction is represented using + and − signs: positions to the right of O are positive; to the left are negative.

📍
In Motion
An object is in motion if its position with respect to the reference point changes with time. Example: athlete running down a track — her position changes each second.
🛑
At Rest
An object is at rest if its position with respect to the reference point does not change with time. Note: "rest" and "motion" are always relative to a chosen reference point — not absolute.
⏱️
Instant vs Interval
An instant of time is a single reading of a clock at a given moment. A time interval is the duration between two instants — i.e., between two clock readings. These are not the same thing.
Worked Example 🏃 Athlete on a Straight Track — Describing Position
An athlete starts at origin O (reference point = 0 m). She runs to point A at 100 m, then back to B at 40 m. Positions to the right of O are positive (+). Her position at A is +100 m; at B is +40 m; at O is 0 m. A position to the left of O would be negative, e.g., −20 m.
Note: Once you choose your origin and positive direction, do not change them while solving a problem. You may choose any convenient reference point — just be consistent.
Section 4.1.2 · Key Quantities

Distance vs Displacement

The same journey — two very different measurements

Distance Travelled
Total Path Length
The total length of path covered by the object, regardless of direction. It is a scalar quantity — it has only a numerical value, no direction required.
SI Unit: metre (m)
Always positive or zero; never negative
Fuel consumption depends on distance, not displacement
Displacement
Net Change in Position
The net change in position between two instants of time. It is a vector quantity — requires both magnitude (how far) and direction (which way).
SI Unit: metre (m)
Can be positive, negative, or zero
Magnitude ≤ total distance travelled (always)
FeatureDistanceDisplacement
TypeScalar (magnitude only)Vector (magnitude + direction)
Path dependenceDepends on actual path takenDepends only on start and end positions
Can be zero?Only if object doesn't moveYes — if object returns to start
Can be negative?No — always ≥ 0Yes — depends on chosen direction
Equal when?Equal when object moves in one direction only without turning back
Example 🏃 Athlete Back-and-Forth — Distance vs Displacement
Athlete: O (t=0s) → A at 100m (t=10s) → back to B at 40m (t=16s). Find distance and displacement.
Solution
Total distance = OA + AB = 100 m + 60 m = 160 m
Displacement = OB = 40 m in the positive direction

The distance (160 m) and the magnitude of displacement (40 m) are not equal — because the athlete turned back. They would be equal only if she had moved in one direction without turning.
⚾ Activity 4.1 Ball Thrown Vertically Upward — Analyse Distance & Displacement
A ball is thrown vertically upward from O. It rises to B (140 cm), passing through A (40 cm) and C (100 cm) on the way up. Fill in distance and displacement at each position.
Position O: Distance = 0 cm · Displacement = 0 cm
Position A (going up): Distance = 40 cm · Displacement = 40 cm upward
Position B (top): Distance = 140 cm · Displacement = 140 cm upward
Position C (coming down): Distance = 140 + 40 = 180 cm · Displacement = 100 cm upward
Back at O: Distance = 280 cm · Displacement = 0 cm (returned to start)
Conclusion: Displacement magnitude ≤ total distance always. It can be zero even when distance is non-zero.
Next Level Up — Scalars and Vectors
Scalar vs Vector Quantities
Physical quantities specified by just their numerical value (magnitude) alone are called scalars — distance, speed, time, mass. Physical quantities that require specifying both magnitude and direction are called vectors — displacement, velocity, acceleration, force. You will study vector algebra in higher grades.

Section 4.1.3 · Rates of Change

Average Speed and Average Velocity

How fast an object moves — and in which direction

Average Speed
speed = distance / time
Total distance travelled ÷ time interval. Scalar — magnitude only, no direction. SI unit: m s⁻¹ (metres per second).
Average Velocity
vav = s / t
Displacement ÷ time interval. Vector — has both magnitude and direction. Direction = direction of displacement. SI unit: m s⁻¹.
⚡
Uniform Motion
If an object travels equal distances in equal time intervals (for all choices of interval), it is in uniform motion. It moves at a constant speed. Position–time graph: straight line.
📊
Non-Uniform Motion
If an object travels unequal distances in equal time intervals, it is in non-uniform motion. Its speed is changing — increasing, decreasing, or both. Position–time graph: curved line.
🧭
Rate of Change
The ratio of change in one quantity to the corresponding change in time is the rate of change. Average velocity = average rate of change of position. This concept is central to all of motion physics.
Example 4.2 🏊 Sarang Swimming — Speed ≠ Velocity
Sarang swims from one end to the other and back in a 25 m pool in 50 s. Find average speed and average velocity.
Solution
Total distance = 50 m · Displacement = 0 m (returned to start)
Average speed = 50 m ÷ 50 s = 1 m s⁻¹
Average velocity = 0 m ÷ 50 s = 0 m s⁻¹

Same time interval, very different values — because speed uses distance while velocity uses displacement.
🇮🇳
India's Scientific Contributions
Aryabhatiya (5th century CE) & Ganitakaumudi (14th century CE)
The concept that speed = distance ÷ time is well-established in ancient India. The Aryabhatiya (5th century CE) by Aryabhata formalised this relationship. The Ganitakaumudi (14th century CE) contains the following classic problem:
Classic Problem
Two postmen start walking toward each other from a distance of 210 yojanas. One travels 9 yojanas/day, the other 5 yojanas/day. In how many days do they meet?

Solution: Together they cover 9 + 5 = 14 yojanas/day. Time = 210 ÷ 14 = 15 days. (First postman: 135 yojanas; second: 75 yojanas.)

Section 4.1.4 · Changing Velocity

Average Acceleration

The rate at which velocity changes — direction matters

When a vehicle suddenly starts moving from rest, you feel a jolt. When it stops suddenly, you feel another jolt. These are your body's experience of acceleration — the rate of change of velocity. Acceleration depends not on how fast something moves, but on how quickly its velocity is changing.

Average Acceleration
a = (v − u) / (t₂ − t₁)
Where u = initial velocity, v = final velocity, t₂ − t₁ = time interval. SI unit: m s⁻². Like velocity, acceleration is a vector — it has direction.
🚀
Acceleration in Direction of Motion
When the magnitude of velocity is increasing, the average acceleration is in the same direction as velocity. The object is speeding up. Example: pressing the accelerator.
🛑
Acceleration Opposite to Motion
When the magnitude of velocity is decreasing, the average acceleration is opposite to velocity (shown by a minus sign). The object is slowing down. Example: pressing the brakes.
🌀
Zero Acceleration
An object moving at constant velocity has zero acceleration — even if its speed is very high. Acceleration depends on change in velocity, not velocity itself. A bus at constant 90 km/h has zero acceleration.
Example 4.3 🚌 Bus on Highway — Acceleration and Braking
A bus moves at 36 km h⁻¹. Driver accelerates for 10 s → velocity reaches 54 km h⁻¹. Then brakes and stops in 5 s. Find acceleration in each phase.
Solution
Convert: 36 km h⁻¹ = 10 m s⁻¹; 54 km h⁻¹ = 15 m s⁻¹

(i) Accelerating: a = (15 − 10) / 10 = 0.5 m s⁻² (positive — same direction as velocity — speeding up)

(ii) Braking: a = (0 − 15) / 5 = −3 m s⁻² (negative — opposite to velocity — slowing down)
Example 4.4 🍎 Object Dropped from Height — Constant Acceleration Due to Gravity
An object dropped from rest. Velocities: t=0s: 0 m/s, t=1s: 9.8 m/s, t=2s: 19.6 m/s, t=3s: 29.4 m/s, t=4s: 39.2 m/s. Find average acceleration each second.
Solution
Each interval: a = (9.8 − 0)/1 = (19.6 − 9.8)/1 = (29.4 − 19.6)/1 = (39.2 − 29.4)/1 = 9.8 m s⁻²

The acceleration is constant at 9.8 m s⁻² in the downward direction — this is the acceleration due to gravitational force (g). It is the same for all falling objects regardless of mass.
Important: In this chapter, we consider only cases where acceleration is constant. The chapter notes that instantaneous velocity and instantaneous acceleration are more advanced concepts you will study in higher grades.
Section 4.2 · Visual Description

Graphical Representation of Motion

Seeing motion — what graphs reveal that equations cannot

A graph is a powerful visual representation of how physical quantities change with time. Motion graphs help: compare the motion of two objects; calculate physical quantities directly from shape, slope, and area; and identify instantly whether motion is uniform or non-uniform. Remember: a graph is not a route map — it does not show the path, only how position (or velocity) changes with time.

📈
Position–Time Graph
Y-axis: position (m). X-axis: time (s). Shows how position changes over time. Slope of the graph = velocity. Straight line → constant velocity. Curve → changing velocity (acceleration).
📉
Velocity–Time Graph
Y-axis: velocity (m/s). X-axis: time (s). Shows how velocity changes over time. Slope = acceleration. Area under graph = displacement. Flat line → constant velocity (a=0).
📐
How to Plot a Graph
Draw X and Y axes. Choose suitable scales for both axes to use the available space effectively. Mark values, plot points, and connect them. Choose scale so data fits well — scale affects the appearance, not the physics.
Section 4.2.2 · Graph Interpretation

Position–Time Graphs — Reading Motion

Shape, slope, and steepness all carry meaning

📏
Straight Line (through origin)
Constant Velocity
Equal displacements in equal time intervals → constant velocity. Slope of the straight line = velocity = (change in position)/(change in time). A steeper line means higher velocity.
Slope = BC/CA = (s₂−s₁)/(t₂−t₁) = velocity
📈
Curved Line
Changing Velocity (Accelerated)
Unequal displacements in equal time intervals → velocity is changing → object is accelerating. Concave up = speeding up. The curve is not the path — it's the changing rate of position.
Increasing slope → increasing velocity → acceleration
➡️
Horizontal Line
Object at Rest
Position does not change with time → object is stationary. Slope = 0 = velocity. A flat position–time line always means at rest at that position.
Slope = 0 → velocity = 0 → at rest
📊
Steeper Slope vs Gentler Slope
Comparing Velocities
On the same position–time graph, the object with the steeper slope has a higher velocity. In the same time interval, a steeper line covers more displacement.
Steeper = faster; gentler = slower
📐 Activity 4.4 Calculating Velocity from a Position–Time Graph
From the graph (Fig. 4.14), select two points A and B on the straight-line segment. Draw lines parallel to both axes to form triangle ABC.
BC = change in position = s₂ − s₁ (vertical side of triangle)
CA = change in time = t₂ − t₁ (horizontal side of triangle)
Velocity = BC/CA = (80 − 40)/(4 − 2) = 40/2 = 20 m s⁻¹
BC/CA is called the slope of line AB — the steepness of the line. Slope of position–time graph = velocity.

Section 4.2.3 · Velocity–Time Graphs

Velocity–Time Graphs — Slope and Area

Two powerful tools: slope gives acceleration, area gives displacement

➡️
Horizontal Line
Constant Velocity
Velocity does not change with time → zero acceleration. Graph is a straight line parallel to the time axis. The car at 20 m s⁻¹ maintains this value throughout.
Slope = 0 → acceleration = 0
📈
Rising Straight Line
Constant Positive Acceleration
Velocity increases by equal amounts in equal time intervals → constant acceleration in direction of velocity. The slope (BC/CA) gives the magnitude and direction of acceleration.
a = (v−u)/(t₂−t₁) = slope of line
📉
Falling Straight Line
Constant Deceleration
Velocity decreases by equal amounts → constant acceleration opposite to velocity. Slope is negative (−0.5 m s⁻²). The velocity eventually reaches zero — object stops.
Negative slope → acceleration opposes velocity
▭
Area Under Graph
= Displacement
The area between the velocity–time graph line and the time axis equals the displacement in that time interval. For a rectangle: area = velocity × time = displacement. For a triangle: ½ × base × height.
Area of trapezoid = displacement between two times
Area Calculation 📐 Displacement from Velocity–Time Graph Area
Constant velocity of 20 m s⁻¹ from t=0 to t=6s. Find displacement using graph area.
Solution
Area = rectangle OABC = velocity × time = 20 m s⁻¹ × 6 s = 120 m

For accelerating car (5 m s⁻¹ to 10 m s⁻¹ over 10–20 s): Displacement = area of rectangle + area of triangle = (5 m s⁻¹ × 10 s) + (½ × 10 s × 5 m s⁻¹) = 50 + 25 = 75 m

Key Summary — What You Can Find from Each Graph:
From a position–time graph: position at any time; velocity (= slope of line).
From a velocity–time graph: velocity at any time; acceleration (= slope of line); displacement (= area under line and time axis).

Section 4.3 · The Three Equations

Kinematic Equations for Constant Acceleration

Three equations — five quantities — unlimited problem-solving power

For motion in a straight line with constant acceleration, the five physical quantities — displacement (s), time (t), initial velocity (u), final velocity (v), and acceleration (a) — are linked by three equations. If you know any three, you can find the other two. These equations are valid only when acceleration is constant.

The Three Kinematic Equations
Equation 4.4a
v = u + at
Links velocity, initial velocity, acceleration, and time. Doesn't involve displacement.
Equation 4.4b
s = ut + ½at²
Links displacement, initial velocity, time, and acceleration. Doesn't involve final velocity.
Equation 4.4c
v² = u² + 2as
Links velocity, initial velocity, acceleration, and displacement. Doesn't involve time.
Variable Key
u = initial velocity (m/s) v = final velocity (m/s) a = acceleration (m/s²) s = displacement (m) t = time interval (s)

Derivation idea: Equation (4.4a) comes from the definition of acceleration: a = (v−u)/t → v = u + at. Equation (4.4b) comes from the area under the velocity–time graph (area of rectangle + area of triangle). Equation (4.4c) is derived by eliminating t between (4.4a) and (4.4b).

Example 4.8 🚗 Stopping Distance — Double the Speed, Quadruple the Distance!
Car brakes with a = −4 m s⁻². How far before stopping if initial speed was (i) 54 km h⁻¹ and (ii) 108 km h⁻¹?
Solution — Using v² = u² + 2as with v = 0
s = u²/(2×4) = u²/8

(i) u = 54 km h⁻¹ = 15 m s⁻¹ → s = 225/8 = 28.1 m
(ii) u = 108 km h⁻¹ = 30 m s⁻¹ → s = 900/8 = 112.5 m

Double the speed → four times the stopping distance! This is why speed limits exist and why maintaining safe following distance is critical.
Bridging Science and Society
🚦 Road Safety and Stopping Distance
When brakes are applied, stopping distance depends on: (1) initial velocity (higher speed → dramatically more distance); (2) road surface (wet/dry); (3) braking capacity (negative acceleration); (4) driver reaction time (vehicle still moves at full speed during this time). This is why "keep a safe distance" signs appear on highways. There is also Vehicle-to-Vehicle (V2V) communication technology, now being developed in many countries including India, which allows vehicles to exchange signals and warns drivers of possible collisions.

Section 4.4 · Motion in a Plane

Uniform Circular Motion

Constant speed — but always accelerating. Here's why.

Motion in a plane (two dimensions) includes: a vehicle overtaking another, a kicked ball's trajectory, a satellite in orbit. The simplest case of 2D motion is uniform circular motion — where an object moves in a circle at constant speed. But even though speed is constant, velocity is not constant — because direction keeps changing. Changing velocity means non-zero acceleration.

Speed
⚡
Constant Speed
In uniform circular motion, the object moves at the same speed at every point on the circular path. The speedometer reading doesn't change.
vav = 2πR/T — speed for one revolution
Direction
🔄
Changing Direction
The direction of velocity changes continuously — always along the tangent to the circle at that point. The velocity vector rotates as the object moves.
Velocity is always tangent to the circular path
Acceleration
⚙️
Non-Zero Acceleration
Because velocity's direction changes, the acceleration is non-zero — even though speed is constant. Acceleration can arise from change in direction alone, not just speed.
This acceleration points toward the centre of the circle
Key Concept 🎠 Merry-Go-Round — One Revolution
A child on a merry-go-round (radius R) makes one complete revolution.

Distance travelled = circumference = 2πR (the full curved path)
Displacement = 0 (returned to starting point)
Average velocity = 0/T = 0 (displacement = 0 over one revolution)
Average speed = 2πR/T (distance/time — this is non-zero)

Building intuition — from polygon to circle: Imagine an athlete running around a rectangular track (4 direction changes per lap), then a hexagonal track (6 changes), then an octagon (8 changes)… As the number of sides increases toward infinity, the track approaches a circle, and the direction of motion changes continuously at every instant. This is what makes circular motion fundamentally different from straight-line motion.

🪐 Activity 4.5 Marble in a Ring — What Happens When You Lift the Ring?
Set a ring flat on a smooth surface. Spin a marble inside it so it travels along the inner boundary. After a couple of revolutions, lift the ring without disturbing the marble. What happens?
The marble immediately moves in a straight line — tangent to the circle at the point the ring was lifted.
The ring was providing the inward force that kept the marble on a circular path. When removed, no force acts on the marble to bend its path.
The marble continues in the direction it was moving at the instant the ring was removed — along the tangent to the circle.
This demonstrates that circular motion requires a continuous inward force — without it, the object moves in a straight line. (You will learn about this force in a later chapter.)
Ready to Go Beyond · Tangent to a Circle
Direction of Velocity in Circular Motion
The velocity at any point in circular motion is along the tangent to the circle at that point. A tangent is a straight line that meets the circle at exactly one point. When the ring was lifted from the marble, the marble moved along this tangent — which is the direction of velocity at that instant. Motion in three dimensions (car on mountain road, bird in sky, aircraft in air) will be studied in higher grades.
Important misconception: In everyday life, we say a vehicle is "accelerating" only when its speed increases. But in physics, acceleration can arise from change in direction alone — even at constant speed. Uniform circular motion is always accelerated motion, even though the speedometer reading doesn't change.
Uniform circular motion as a model: In real life, truly constant speed on a circular path is rare. But uniform circular motion is an idealised model — like all scientific models — that serves as the foundation for understanding orbits, turning vehicles, rotating machinery, and much more.
India's Scientific Contributions

Motion in Indian Scientific History

Ancient India's mathematical foundations of speed and motion

📜
Aryabhatiya · 5th Century CE
Aryabhata and the Concept of Speed
The concept that speed = distance ÷ time — the most fundamental relationship in motion physics — was established in ancient India as early as the 5th century CE. The Aryabhatiya by Aryabhata formalised this relationship in the context of astronomy and mathematics. This was not merely a statement but was used for calculating positions of planets, predicting eclipses, and understanding celestial motion.
📐
Ganitakaumudi · 14th Century CE · Narayana Pandita
A Speed Problem from 700 Years Ago
The Ganitakaumudi (14th century CE) is a comprehensive mathematical text by Narayana Pandita. It contains rich problems in arithmetic, algebra, and combinatorics — including classic speed and distance problems identical in structure to what we solve today. The two-postmen problem (210 yojanas apart, walking toward each other at 9 and 5 yojanas/day) is a direct application of the average speed formula. The solution is 15 days. The fact that such problems appear in a 14th-century Indian text shows the sophisticated mathematical tradition India contributed to global science.

🏎️
Threads of Curiosity
The Speedometer — Instantaneous Velocity in Your Car
The reading of a vehicle's speedometer is nearly (but not exactly) the magnitude of the instantaneous velocity — the velocity at a single instant of time. As the time interval around an instant is made progressively smaller, the average velocity over that shrinking interval approaches a fixed value called the instantaneous velocity. The direction of velocity at any instant is given by the direction the tyres are pointing. This is why a speedometer at constant 60 km/h and a constant direction means zero acceleration — even though the vehicle is moving fast.
📱
Threads of Curiosity · Technology
Accelerometer in Your Smartphone — Detecting Tiny Accelerations
Many smartphones contain a built-in accelerometer — a device that measures acceleration in all three directions. Apps like Phyphox (phyphox.org) can display these readings in real time. Try comparing the reading when your phone is on an outstretched palm versus placed flat on the floor. The differences reveal that even a stationary phone on your palm experiences subtle motions — involuntary tremors, vibrations from the environment. Such tiny, involuntary movements are studied in medical research — for example, in monitoring movement disorders like Parkinson's disease.
Ready to Go Beyond
🌍 Is Everything in the Universe Really in Motion?
A student said "The Earth moves around the Sun." In this context, can an object kept on Earth be considered truly at rest? It depends on the reference point chosen. Relative to the ground, a book on a table is at rest. Relative to the Sun, it moves at ~30 km/s with Earth. Relative to the Milky Way's centre, Earth moves at ~220 km/s. Motion and rest are not absolute — they are always relative to a chosen reference point. This idea was formalised by Einstein in the Special Theory of Relativity.
Ready to Go Beyond
📊 The Spinning Disc — Speed and Circular Motion
Take a cardboard disc (radius ~8 cm). Write numbers 1–12 on the outer ring (7 cm from centre) and letters A–F on the inner ring (4 cm from centre), using the same font size. Spin the disc slowly, then faster. The outer numbers fade or disappear while the inner letters remain visible — because the outer points travel a longer arc in the same time (higher speed = more blur). In one rotation, every point completes a full circle — but outer points travel a longer circumference (2πR) in the same time T, so their speed (2πR/T) is greater than inner points. This directly demonstrates that in circular motion, objects at greater radius have higher speed even if they complete the same number of rotations per second.
Bridging Science and Society
🚗 V2V Technology — The Future of Road Safety
Vehicle-to-Vehicle (V2V) communication technology allows vehicles to exchange data wirelessly and warn drivers of possible collisions before they happen — going beyond human reaction time. Currently being developed in India and many countries. V2V systems can communicate: current speed, position (GPS), direction, and braking status to surrounding vehicles. Combined with kinematic equations (braking distance = u²/2a), autonomous systems can predict collisions up to seconds in advance and automatically apply brakes. This is one of the most direct applications of motion physics in saving lives on Indian roads.
Journey Beyond — Derive These Yourself
Two More Kinematic Equations to Derive
From the two primary equations v = u + at and s = ut + ½at², three more can be derived. We derived one (v² = u² + 2as). The remaining two are:

s = vt − ½at² (eliminate u using v = u + at)
s = ½(u + v)t (area of trapezoid on velocity–time graph — use the trapezoid area formula from mathematics)

These equations are particularly useful when initial velocity is unknown or when mean velocity is easier to work with.
