Scientific Models

Simplifying the complex to find the essential

🔭
What is a Scientific Model?
A model is a simplified way of looking at a real system that focuses only on what is most important for a given question. It deliberately ignores some details on purpose.
⚙️
Why Simplify?
The natural world is too complex to study in full detail. Ignoring certain details is not a mistake — it is done purposefully to keep things manageable while still finding the answer we need.
🧩
More Complexity = More Accuracy
As we build more complex models, we add extra details for greater accuracy. Simple models come first — complexity is added only when needed.

Section 3

Simplification in Science

Purposeful choices, not careless omissions

Key Insight: When scientists simplify, they are making deliberate, purposeful choices. Ignoring air resistance when studying gravity is not laziness — it allows us to understand the basic effect clearly before adding complexity later. These choices are not mistakes.

🏋️
Assumptions
Every model makes assumptions — statements about what is true for the model. Example: "Assume the ball is a perfect sphere." The model only works within these assumptions.
🎯
Focus on the Question
The right model depends on the question. Different questions about the same object may require different models with different included/ignored details.
📈
Building Up Complexity
Start simple. Once the simple model works, add more details to improve accuracy. This is a standard scientific strategy.
Real-World Example of Simplification
Meghnad Saha and the Stars
When physicist Meghnad Saha studied light from stars, he did not try to model every atom, reaction, or movement inside a star. Instead, he treated star matter as a hot gas, ignored many complex processes, and focused only on temperature, pressure, and how atoms formed ions. This simplification allowed him to explain why star colour depends on star temperature. → See full Scientist Profile
Section 4

Language of Science

Precise, shared, and unambiguous

Science uses language in a very careful and precise way. Many everyday words — like "force", "work", "cell", or "reaction" — have specific scientific meanings that may differ from everyday usage. This precision allows scientists across the world to communicate clearly and without confusion.

Same Word, Different Meanings
Force
Science: A push or pull that changes motion (measured in Newtons)
Everyday: Effort, strength ("I forced open the door")
Work
Science: Force × displacement in the direction of force (measured in Joules)
Everyday: Any activity or effort
Cell
Science: The basic unit of life in biology OR a unit in an electrochemical battery
Everyday: A room, a phone, a prison cell
Reaction
Science: A chemical process where substances combine/break down; or Newton's 3rd Law force pair
Everyday: Response, reply

Why does this matter? If two scientists use the same word to mean different things, their results cannot be compared, their ideas cannot be shared, and mistakes happen. Precise scientific language is what makes global scientific collaboration possible.

Symbols and Units

The shared shorthand of science worldwide

🔤
Why Symbols?
Quantities like mass, velocity, force are represented by symbols like m, v, F, I. Symbols make equations compact, universal, and quick to write and communicate.
📏
SI Units — The Global Standard
The International System of Units (SI) ensures that a kilogram in India is the same as a kilogram in France. Standard units allow results to be compared and ensure fairness in daily life and trade.

Threads of Curiosity
Why is the speed of light denoted by 'c'?
Scientific symbols often come from history and international agreements — not just abbreviations of convenience. The symbol c for the speed of light comes from the Latin word celeritas, meaning speed. Today, the speed of light is a precisely defined physical constant: exactly 299,792,458 m/s. Many other symbols also have historical origins — understanding them connects us to centuries of scientific tradition.
⚖️
Threads of Curiosity
Why is a kilogram used everywhere?
When you buy rice or vegetables, you expect a kilogram to mean the same amount everywhere. Imagine the confusion if different weights were used in different cities! Thankfully, measurements are based on agreed international standards, not local objects or opinions. Standard units allow scientific results to be compared and ensure fairness in daily life and trade worldwide.
Ready to Go Beyond
⚠️ Airplane Fuel Miscalculation — A Real Disaster Caused by Wrong Units
A passenger aircraft once ran out of fuel mid-flight due to a unit mix-up. The flight needed 22,300 kg of fuel, but the ground crew used the density of fuel in pounds (lb) per litre instead of kilograms (kg) per litre. The aircraft was about 15,000 litres short of fuel. Luckily, it could glide to an emergency landing — the aircraft was damaged but there were no casualties. Pounds and kilograms are very different units. This real-life incident shows exactly why using standard (SI) units everywhere avoids dangerous errors.

Mathematics in Science

A language for thinking, not just calculating

🔢
Mathematics as a Language
Mathematics in science is not meant to be a hurdle. It is a language that helps us think more clearly. An equation is a compact statement about how certain things are related.
📊
Equations Tell Stories
Describing motion using distance, time, velocity lets us predict where an object will be later. Chemical rate equations describe reaction speeds. Population growth follows mathematical patterns.
🧠
How to Approach Math in Science
Understand the situation first → Identify quantities involved → Apply relationships carefully. This approach makes equations feel like helpful guides, not obstacles.

Important: Learning mathematics in science does not mean memorising equations. It means understanding the situation first, identifying the relevant quantities, and then using mathematical relationships to reason carefully. Equations are like helpful guides — not obstacles — in your exploration of science.

🏃
Example: Motion
Using distance, time, and velocity equations, we can predict where an object will be at any future moment — before doing any experiment.
⚗️
Example: Chemical Reactions
Mathematical expressions describe rates of chemical reactions — how fast products form, how quickly reactants are used up.
🌱
Example: Population Growth
Patterns of population growth — in bacteria, plants, animals, or humans — follow mathematical relationships that can be modelled and predicted.
Section 7

Laws, Theories and Principles

Three types of scientific ideas — each plays a different role

Scientific Law
📜
Law
Describes a regular pattern observed in nature. Often expressed in words or as a mathematical relationship. Laws tell us what happens.
Newton's Laws of Motion explain the jerk felt when a bus stops suddenly.
Scientific Theory
💡
Theory
Provides an explanation of why patterns occur. Based on evidence gathered and tested over time. A theory is NOT a guess — it is a well-tested explanation.
Atomic theory explains how molecules are formed from atoms.
Principle
⚖️
Principle
A broad idea that helps make sense of a situation. Guides reasoning and analysis across many different contexts.
Principle of conservation of energy applies when climbing stairs.
🔬
Critical Understanding
A Theory is NOT a Guess!
In everyday life, "theory" sometimes means a guess. In science, a theory is the opposite — it is an explanation based on careful testing and critical examination over time. Scientific theories are always open to improvement and change as new evidence emerges. This openness to revision is not a weakness — it is what makes science reliable and trustworthy.

Scientific Questions to Ask
Good scientific questions look for measurable evidence and past patterns. Simple yes/no questions are usually not enough.
What was the sky condition the last time it rained?
What is the humidity today? Was it above 80% the last time it rained?
What is today's wind speed and direction?
Is the temperature dropping like it did before recent rains?
💡 These questions ask about measurable data and past patterns — going well beyond "clouds look dark."

🌦️ Why Do Weather Forecasts Sometimes Go Wrong?
Weather depends on many changing factors: temperature, pressure, humidity, and wind. Forecasts use measurements and models, but very tiny differences in initial conditions can grow over time and lead to completely different outcomes. This is why forecasts are usually reliable for a few hours or a few days, but less certain further into the future. This sensitivity to initial conditions is a fundamental feature of complex systems.
Section 9

Scientific Evidence and Scientific Thinking

What makes science different from opinion

📋
Theories Have Limits
Even the most successful scientific theories may fail when new conditions are explored or measurements become more precise. This is not a weakness — it is how science grows.
🔍
Evidence Over Opinion
When predictions don't match observations, scientists don't reject ideas based on opinion or belief — only on evidence. No scientific theory is ever final or beyond question.
♻️
Open to Correction
Science's greatest strength is its openness to being corrected by nature itself. This is what has allowed science to help us understand the world we live in.
🌑
Threads of Curiosity — Checking 'Viral' Claims
Is Eating Food Harmful During an Eclipse?
A commonly circulated claim: "Food should not be eaten during an eclipse because it becomes harmful." Scientific thinking helps us test this. An eclipse is simply a play of shadows — the Moon passing between Earth and Sun. Ask: What physical change occurs during an eclipse? Does temperature change significantly? Does food go bad if left in a shadow? No physical, chemical, or biological mechanism supports this claim. Scientific thinking applied to everyday myths is a powerful life skill.
Section 10 & 11

Scientific Thinking & Estimation

Approximate reasoning as a powerful scientific skill

A helpful strategy in science: first understand the situation, then identify quantities that matter, then make a rough estimate to check whether an answer makes sense. Exact values are not always necessary — approximate estimates often tell us whether a result is reasonable or impossible. Science values careful reasoning more than accurate calculations!

Example 1.3 💨 How Many Litres of Air Do You Breathe Per Day?
Estimate how many litres of air you breathe in one day. Start by estimating breaths per minute and volume of one breath. Aim for a reasonable estimate, not an exact answer.
1
Breaths per minute at rest
~12 – 15 breaths/min → use 13
2
Minutes in a day
60 × 24 = 1,440 minutes/day
3
Total breaths per day
13 × 1,440 ≈ 18,000 – 22,000 breaths/day
4
Volume per breath (balloon estimate)
~4–5 breaths fill a 2-litre balloon → ~0.5 litre per breath
✓
Final Estimate
~20,000 × 0.5 L = 10,000 litres of air per day!
💡 Cross-check: If you filled 3 balloons/min × 2 litres × 1,440 min = ~8,640 litres — close enough! Both approaches give the same order of magnitude, so the estimate is reasonable.
Ready to Go Beyond
🍚 How Much Rice Would Feed a Family of Four for a Month?
To estimate: assume all calorie needs come from rice alone. An average adult needs about 2000–2500 kcal per day. Find how many calories 100g of uncooked rice provides when cooked. Use this to estimate daily rice for the family, then multiply by 30 days. The aim is not an exact number — but to check if the answer is reasonable: 100g/month is clearly too little, while a few tonnes is far too much. Such estimation connects science to real everyday questions.

Section 12 & 13

Interdisciplinary Nature of Science & Science in Everyday Life

Science has no boundaries in the real world

After Grade 10, science divides into branches — physics, chemistry, biology, earth science. But the natural world has no such boundaries. These divisions exist only to help organise knowledge. Real-world problems like climate change, medicine, and sustainable technology require ideas from several disciplines together. Science also connects with mathematics, technology, arts, and social sciences.

Ready to Go Beyond
😷 How Does a Mask Really Work? — A Study in Interdisciplinary Science
During the COVID-19 pandemic, everyone used masks for safety. Understanding how a mask works requires knowledge from multiple branches of science:
Physics
Particle motion and electrostatic attraction — how particles are drawn to and trapped by mask fibres.
Chemistry
Properties of polymer fibres — what makes the mask material effective as a filter.
Biology
Size and behaviour of viruses — how large viral particles are and how they travel in droplets.
Mathematics
Modelling airflow and filtration efficiency — quantifying how effective the mask is at different particle sizes.

Meet a Scientist

Meghnad Saha — Simplifying the Stars

🌟
Indian Physicist 1893 – 1956
Meet a Scientist
Meghnad Saha
1893 – 1956 · Astrophysicist · India
When physicist Meghnad Saha studied light from stars, he did not try to model every atom, every reaction, or every movement inside a star. Instead, he treated the matter in the star as a hot gas, ignored many complex processes, and focused only on three key quantities: temperature, pressure, and how atoms formed ions.
This elegant simplification — a perfect example of scientific model-building — allowed him to explain how the colour of stars is deeply connected to their temperature. His Saha Ionisation Equation became a cornerstone of astrophysics.
Meghnad Saha is honoured on an Indian postage stamp, recognising his foundational contribution to the understanding of stars and stellar spectra.
Key Takeaway for Students
You don't need to model everything to understand something important. Choose the right simplification for the right question. Saha showed that a simple model of a star as a hot gas was enough to unlock one of astronomy's great mysteries: why different stars have different colours.

💫
Threads of Curiosity
Why is the speed of light denoted by 'c'?
Scientific symbols often come from history, based on international agreements rather than convenience. The symbol c comes from the Latin word celeritas, meaning "speed." Today, the speed of light is a physical constant, defined to be exactly 299,792,458 m/s. This is not an experimental measurement — it is a definition. Other historical symbol origins: F for force (Latin: Fortia), G for gravitational constant, and E for energy in Einstein's famous E=mc².
⚖️
Threads of Curiosity
Why is a kilogram used everywhere in the world?
When you buy rice, vegetables, or gold, you expect a kilogram to mean exactly the same amount everywhere. Without international standards, trade would be chaotic, scientific results couldn't be compared, and daily life would be confusing. The kilogram (and all SI units) are based on agreed international standards — not local objects or customs. Since 2019, the kilogram is defined using Planck's constant, a fundamental physical constant, making it even more precise and universal.
🌑
Threads of Curiosity — Myth Busting
Checking 'Viral' Claims: Is Eating Food Harmful During an Eclipse?
The claim: "Food becomes harmful during an eclipse." Let's apply scientific thinking. An eclipse is just a play of shadows — the Moon blocks sunlight temporarily. Ask yourself: What physical change actually happens during an eclipse? Does temperature change significantly enough to spoil food? Would food go bad if you simply put it in a shadow for a few minutes? No physical, chemical, or biological mechanism supports this claim. Scientific thinking — asking precise questions and demanding evidence — is your most powerful tool for evaluating everyday claims.
Ready to Go Beyond
✈️ The Airplane Fuel Disaster — A Unit Conversion Error with Real Consequences
In a well-known aviation incident, a passenger aircraft ran out of fuel mid-flight. The flight needed 22,300 kg of fuel in total. The ground crew used the density of fuel in pounds (lb) per litre instead of kilograms (kg) per litre. Since 1 kg ≈ 2.2 lb, the error meant the plane received far less fuel by mass than needed. The aircraft was about 15,000 litres short and had to glide to an emergency landing — damaging the plane, though with no casualties. This real incident is studied in aviation safety training worldwide and powerfully illustrates why using standard SI units everywhere is not just academic — it can save lives.
Ready to Go Beyond
🌦️ Why Do Weather Forecasts Sometimes Go Wrong?
Weather systems depend on an enormous number of changing factors: temperature, pressure, humidity, wind speed and direction, ocean temperatures, and more. Forecasts use complex measurements and mathematical models, but there is a fundamental problem: very tiny differences in initial conditions can grow over time and lead to completely different outcomes. This is called "sensitive dependence on initial conditions" and is related to what scientists call chaos theory. This is why weather forecasts are reasonably reliable for a few hours or a few days — but become much less certain a week or more into the future.
Ready to Go Beyond
🍚 How Much Rice Would Feed a Family of Four for a Month?
An estimation challenge! Assume all calorie needs come from rice. An average adult needs about 2000–2500 kcal per day. A family of four = approximately 4 adults. Total daily needs: ~8,000–10,000 kcal. Research: 100g of uncooked rice ≈ 360 kcal when dry, yielding more when cooked. Estimate daily rice: ~2.2–2.8 kg uncooked per day for the family. Over 30 days: approximately 66–84 kg of uncooked rice for a month. Does this seem reasonable? A 50 kg bag of rice is a common market size — two such bags roughly fits our estimate. Not too little, not too much. Estimation works!
Ready to Go Beyond
😷 How Does a Mask Really Work? — Interdisciplinary Science in Action
During COVID-19, masks became essential. But understanding why they work needs knowledge from every branch of science: Physics — tiny particles move randomly (Brownian motion) and are attracted to charged fibres by electrostatic forces. Chemistry — mask fibres are made from polymers (like polypropylene) with specific material properties. Biology — viruses travel in respiratory droplets; knowing their size (~0.1 μm) helps design the right filter mesh. Mathematics — airflow models and filtration efficiency equations quantify mask effectiveness. This single everyday object is a perfect example of how real-world solutions require interdisciplinary science.
