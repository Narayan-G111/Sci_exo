Opening Concept

What is Reproduction?

Every organism is born, grows, matures, reproduces and eventually dies — reproduction is what keeps the chain of life unbroken

A Mango Tree Dies. Its Seeds Don't.

Reproduction is the biological process by which living beings produce new individuals of their own kind, ensuring life continues even after an individual organism dies. A mango tree eventually grows old and dies, but the seeds it dropped years earlier are already growing into new mango trees. Cows give birth to calves, dogs to puppies, cats to kittens, and humans to children — each generation handing the baton of life to the next.

🧬
Asexual Reproduction
A single parent produces offspring that are almost exact copies of itself. No mixing of genetic material — the new individual is essentially a clone.
🌸
Sexual Reproduction
Two parents contribute genetic material, and the offspring inherits a mix of characteristics from both — producing small but important differences from each parent.
🌍
Why Variation Matters
Differences between parent and offspring, accumulated over many generations, help species adapt to changing environments and sometimes give rise to entirely new species over time.

Section 11.1 · One Parent, Identical Copies

Asexual Reproduction & Vegetative Propagation

Seen in bacteria, amoeba, yeast, hydra, sponge — and in many plants that sprout new shoots and roots from existing parts

Many plants sprout new shoots and roots from their existing vegetative parts — without ever producing seeds. Potato and ginger have fleshy underground stems that sprout new plants. Money plant and sugarcane stem cuttings grow into new plants. A Bryophyllum leaf sprouts tiny plantlets along its edge that eventually grow into independent plants. This is called vegetative propagation: new plants arising from the growing (vegetative) parts of a parent plant. Because only one parent is involved, the offspring are genetically identical to it.

What to record: Average length of cuttings, number of nodes/internodes, and how different plants have different growing seasons for taking cuttings.

Bridging Science and Society
Krishi Vigyan Kendras — Taking Grafting to Farmers
Various Krishi Vigyan Kendras (KVKs) under the Indian Council of Agricultural Research (ICAR) train farmers in vocational grafting skills, helping them grow high-yield fruit varieties and boost their income through better marketing strategies and government schemes.
Bridging Science and Society
Tissue Culture — Revolutionising Banana Farming
Scientists can mass-produce healthy plantlets from just the shoot tip (apical meristem) of a plant using the tissue culture technique. This is another form of asexual reproduction — it eliminates virus-infected stock and ensures high, uniform yields, and has transformed banana farming across India.
Key point: All vegetative propagation methods — cutting, grafting, layering, and tissue culture — produce genetically identical individuals because only one parent's vegetative tissue is involved. These methods let farmers reliably reproduce a desirable variety on a large scale.
Section 11.1 continued · Other Asexual Methods

Budding, Spore Formation & the Power of Mitosis

Yeast buds, hydra buds, and bread mould releases millions of spores — all powered by the same cell-division process

🔬 Activity 11.2 Watching Yeast Bud Under a Microscope
A pinch of yeast granules is added to 20 mL of warm sugar solution in a test tube, plugged with cotton, and left undisturbed in a warm place. After 1–2 hours, a drop is mounted on a slide and observed under a compound microscope.
Observation: Small, round outgrowths called buds appear on the parent yeast cells — visible proof that the yeast is duplicating itself.
In hydra: Repeated cell division at a specific site on the body produces a small outgrowth (a bud), which enlarges and eventually separates to live independently. Several buds can often be seen growing on one hydra at once.

Budding is asexual reproduction where a new individual develops as an outgrowth from the parent's body and later detaches to live on its own — seen in single-celled yeast and the multicellular hydra alike.

🔬
Threads of Curiosity
Louis Pasteur — Life Only Comes from Life
For centuries people believed living things could arise spontaneously from non-living matter. Louis Pasteur's experiments disproved this once and for all, proving new life always comes from pre-existing life. His work also established the germ theory of disease, strengthened the cell theory (all cells arise from pre-existing cells), and led directly to practices like food and instrument sterilisation that we depend on today.
🔬 Activity 11.3 Growing Mould on Bread — Where Do Microbes Come From?
A moistened slice of bread or roti is placed in a warm, dark, moist chamber (a box lined with damp cotton and tissue paper) for three days, then examined with a magnifying glass and, later, under a microscope using cotton blue stain.
Observation: Fuzzy, thread-like structures (hyphae) appear, each topped with a round sac containing tiny spores.
Where did it come from? The mould was not present on the fresh bread — it grew from spores already floating in the air that settled on the moist bread and began reproducing rapidly once conditions were favourable.
Fungal structures seen: Rhizopus forms spores in a single sac-like structure; Aspergillus forms them on a swollen vesicle at the tip of a long fungal strand (hypha).
🍄
Spore Formation
Fungi produce millions of lightweight, single-celled spores from one colony alone. Spores float easily on air currents and germinate rapidly into a new individual once they land on moisture and nutrients.
❄️
Why We Refrigerate Food
Mould spores need warmth (25–35°C) and moisture to grow. Lower temperatures slow or stop their reproduction — which is exactly why refrigeration keeps food fresh far longer than was possible a century ago.
🔁
Mitosis — The Engine of Asexual Reproduction
In every organism studied so far, asexual reproduction is powered by mitosis — cell division producing two daughter cells with the same chromosome number as the parent. The result: genetically identical offspring called clones.
Bridging Science and Society
Fungi: Unpleasant on Bread, Invaluable to Society
Mould on bread looks unappealing, but fungi benefit society enormously. Growing rapidly through spore formation, fungi degrade organic waste and pollutants and play a role in removing heavy metals from industrial waste. Many life-saving antibiotics — including penicillin and amoxicillin — are derived from fungi, saving countless lives from bacterial infections. Some fungi can even degrade plastic!
Speed advantage: Because asexual reproduction via mitosis is fast and needs no partner, organisms can rapidly increase their population whenever environmental conditions are favourable.
Section 11.2 · Two Parents, New Combinations

Sexual Reproduction & How Meiosis Creates Variation

Two parents, half the chromosomes each — and millions of possible combinations in every child

Solving the Chromosome-Doubling Problem

Sexual reproduction involves two parents, both contributing genetic material to the offspring. But if every generation received the full set of chromosomes from both parents, the chromosome number would double every generation. Nature solves this with meiosis — a special type of cell division that halves the chromosome number when forming gametes (sex cells), so that when two gametes fuse, the original number is restored.

Chromosomes
Humans: 23 pairs
Thread-like structures in the nucleus carrying genetic information. Each pair has one chromosome from each parent — 46 in total per cell.
Diploid Parent Cell
2n → n
Meiosis reduces the chromosome number of a parent (diploid) cell to half in the daughter cells, which are haploid.
Gametes
Sperm + Egg
Haploid cells used only for reproduction. In animals: sperm (male) and eggs (female). In plants: pollen grain (male) and ovule's egg cell (female).
🔬 Activity 11.4 Bead Combinations — Modelling Meiosis
Take three pairs of contrasting-coloured beads, each pair representing two versions of a character on different chromatids of a chromosome — e.g. light/dark green for hair colour, light/dark blue for hair texture, light/dark red for eye colour. Randomly pick one bead from each pair to form a combination.
Result: With just 3 pairs of contrasting characters, 8 different combinations are possible (2 × 2 × 2).
Scaling up: Now imagine the possibilities with all 23 pairs of human chromosomes, each carrying information for many characters — the number of possible combinations becomes astronomically large.
Conclusion: During meiosis, chromosomes of each pair separate so every gamete gets only one chromosome from each pair. This random mixing is why children are genetically different from their parents — and from each other.

This variation among individuals is essential for the survival of a species — it helps some individuals adapt better to changing environments and, over long timescales, contributes to evolution. For example, some people can tolerate low oxygen at high altitudes, or digest milk into adulthood — traits that arose from this constant genetic shuffling.

Section 11.2.2 · Reproductive Organs of Plants

Sexual Reproduction in Flowering Plants

Flowering plants (angiosperms) are Earth's most diverse plant group — and the flower is their reproductive organ

A flower bud is wrapped in a thin, flat, green outer covering called the sepal, which protects the flower in the bud stage. As the bud opens, the colourful inner projections — the petals — are revealed, which attract pollinators and enhance the flower's beauty and fragrance. Together, a complete flower has four parts: sepals, petals, stamens, and pistil (in some flowers, sepals and petals are fused).

🔬 Activity 11.5 Dissecting Flowers — Recording the Floral Parts
Collect different types of flowers and observe each part from the outer whorl inward. Record the presence of sepal, petal, stamen and pistil for each flower in a table, then cut a transverse and longitudinal section of the ovary and observe it under a dissecting microscope.
🌸
Sepal & Petal
The sepal is the outermost green whorl that protects the flower bud. The petal is the colourful, often fragrant inner whorl that attracts pollinators.
♂️
Stamen — Male Part
Consists of a filament (stalk) and an anther at its tip, which produces pollen grains containing the male gametes.
♀️
Pistil — Female Part
Made of three sub-parts: the stigma (sticky tip that receives pollen), style (the connecting tube), and ovary (containing ovules, each with an egg cell).

Section 11.2.3–11.2.4 · Pollen on the Move

Pollination — Strategies for Reproductive Success

Without pollen reaching the stigma, no fruit and no seed can ever form

🔬 Activity 11.6 Investigating Pollination in Garden Pea
On the same pea plant, stamens are removed from one flower bud and one open flower. Muslin cloth bags are wrapped around: a bud, the stamen-removed bud, the stamen-removed flower, and an intact flower — while one intact flower is left completely uncovered.
Result: Fruits form in every treatment except the flower bud whose stamens were removed before it could mature.
Inference: The transfer of pollen grains from anther to stigma is essential for fruit formation. This transfer is called pollination.
Self-Pollination
🔄
Same Flower or Plant
Pollen is transferred to the stigma of the same flower, or to another flower on the same plant. Offspring are genetically very similar to the parent plant.
Cross-Pollination
🦋
Between Two Plants
Pollen from the anther of one plant's flower reaches the stigma of a different plant of the same species — introducing new genetic combinations and greater variation.
11.2.4 Pollination Strategies — Who Carries the Pollen?
💨
Wind Pollination
Seen in wheat, maize and rice. Pollen grains are light, small, and produced in huge numbers; the stigma is long and feathery to trap drifting pollen.
💧
Water Pollination
Aquatic plants like Vallisneria and Hydrilla rely on water currents to carry pollen from one flower to another.
🐝
Insect Pollination
Sunflower, hibiscus and marigold are bright, nectar-producing and fragrant to attract bees and butterflies. Pollen is large, sticky or spiny so it clings to insect bodies, and the stigma is sticky to receive it.
🐦
Bird Pollination
Coral tree and hibiscus flowers are pollinated by birds such as the Indian white-eye and sunbirds, which feed on their nectar while incidentally transferring pollen.
🔬 Activity 11.7 Comparing Wind vs Insect Pollination Efficiency
Data comparing pollen released per flower against the average seeds formed reveals a striking efficiency gap between strategies.

Why so much wasted pollen? Wind pollination is highly inefficient at a per-grain level — most pollen never reaches a stigma at all. Producing enormous quantities compensates for this low hit-rate, making it a successful strategy despite poor odds for any single grain. Insect pollination is targeted and far more efficient per grain, so far fewer grains are needed.
Bridging Science and Society
Sexual Reproduction in Plant Breeding
Understanding pollination has applied importance in plant breeding. In selective breeding, farmers choose plants with desirable characters to reproduce. Artificial hybridisation involves removing stamens, bagging the flower to prevent self-pollination, then manually transferring pollen with desired characters. Genetic engineering goes further, inserting genetic material directly into a plant's DNA — together, these methods have produced high-yielding and disease-resistant crop varieties that have revolutionised agriculture.

Section 11.5.1–11.5.3 · Reproduction in Human Beings

The Male & Female Reproductive Systems

As a child grows into an adult, reproductive organs mature and begin producing gametes

During adolescence, reproductive organs mature and begin producing gametes — sperm in males and eggs in females. When a sperm meets an egg inside the female body, they form a zygote that develops first into an embryo and eventually into a foetus within the uterus.

11.5.2 The Male Reproductive System
🫘
Testes & Scrotum
Sperm are produced in two oval organs called testes, housed in a skin pouch called the scrotum, which keeps them slightly cooler than body temperature — necessary for sperm formation. Testes also produce a hormone controlling sperm production and puberty changes.
〰️
Vas Deferens & Urethra
Sperm travel from the testes through a long tube, the vas deferens, which opens into the urethra — a shared passage for both urine and sperm.
💧
Seminal Vesicle & Prostate
These glands add fluids that nourish the sperm and help them stay active and motile. Each sperm has a head with genetic material and a long tail for swimming.
11.5.3 The Female Reproductive System
🥚
Ovaries
A pair of ovaries produces the female germ cells (eggs) and releases hormones that bring about changes during puberty.
➡️
Oviducts (Fallopian Tubes)
These tubes connect each ovary to the uterus, providing the pathway along which the released egg travels — and the usual site of fertilisation.
🏠
Uterus, Cervix & Vagina
The uterus is a bag-like structure where a foetus develops. It opens into the vagina through a narrow passage called the cervix.
Asymmetric design: Notice how the male system is built for producing and delivering huge numbers of mobile sperm, while the female system is built for producing far fewer eggs and providing a protected, nourishing space for a developing embryo — a pattern seen across most animal species.
Section 11.5.4–11.5.5 · Making & Meeting Gametes

Gametogenesis — and What Happens When Sperm Meets Egg

46 chromosomes become 23 in every gamete — so that fusion restores the full set

The formation of gametes is called gametogenesis, occurring in the testes and ovaries through meiosis, which halves the chromosome number. Human cells have 46 chromosomes, but sperm and eggs have only 23 each — so that when they combine, the resulting zygote has the correct number of 46, just like both parents.

Section 11.5.6 · The Monthly Cycle

What Happens When an Egg is Not Fertilised?

Menstruation, explained as part of a repeating ~28-day cycle of preparation and renewal

If an egg is not fertilised, it remains viable for about a day before degenerating. The thickened, blood-rich uterine lining that had prepared to nourish a possible zygote is no longer needed, so it sheds — leaving the body through the vagina along with some blood. This process is called menstruation (a period), and usually lasts 3 to 7 days.

Threads of Curiosity
What Determines a Baby's Biological Sex?
Every person has two sex chromosomes — females have XX and males have XY. The mother always contributes an X chromosome to her child, while the father contributes either an X (resulting in a female, XX) or a Y (resulting in a male, XY). Since only the father's gamete carries this variable chromosome, it is the father's contribution that determines the biological sex of the baby — not the mother's.
Bridging Science and Society · Period is Your Pride
Healthy Hygiene Practices During Menstruation
Menstruation is a sign of a healthy reproductive system, not something to feel ashamed of. Good practices include: using menstrual products such as sanitary pads, tampons or menstrual cups (preferably biodegradable); washing the genital area with water only (soap can disrupt natural bacterial balance); always washing hands before and after changing a pad; wrapping used pads before disposal and never flushing them; properly cleaning and drying reusable pads; and changing menstrual products every 4–6 hours, or more often during heavy flow.
Section 11.5.7–11.5.8 · Nine Months of Growth

Pregnancy, Childbirth & a Mother's Health

Three trimesters of transformation, followed by careful care for both mother and newborn

1st Trimester
🌱
Months 1–3
The fertilised egg develops into an embryo and major organs begin forming. From about the ninth week, the developing embryo is called a foetus.
2nd Trimester
🤰
Months 4–6
The foetus grows bigger and stronger, and the mother can usually start to feel its movements.
3rd Trimester
👶
Months 7–9
The baby grows rapidly and prepares for life outside the womb. The uterus protects and nourishes the baby throughout.

During childbirth, strong contractions of the uterine muscles push the foetus out through the birth canal. If a normal vaginal birth is not possible or safe for mother or baby, doctors may use medical or surgical procedures to deliver the baby safely.

🍼
Newborn Care
Breastfeeding provides complete nutrition and protects against many diseases. Newborns must be kept warm, vaccinated on time, and handled gently.
🥗
Mother's Health During Pregnancy
A balanced diet rich in proteins, vitamins and minerals, regular medical check-ups, light exercise, adequate rest, and emotional support from family all matter greatly.
🚫
Substances to Avoid
It is strongly recommended for a pregnant woman to avoid smoking, alcohol, and any medicines taken without medical advice, since the health of mother and baby are closely connected.
Bridging Science and Society
ASHA Workers — India's Community Health Heroes
More than 10 lakh Accredited Social Health Activist (ASHA) workers serve across India. These trained community health workers — predominantly women — promote hygiene, immunisation and family planning under the National Health Mission, especially in rural areas, offering advice on maternal care, safe deliveries and contraceptive methods.

Post-Partum Depression — A Treatable Condition
Some mothers experience a form of anxiety and fatigue after childbirth, known as post-partum depression. This is a recognised, treatable condition — mothers should be encouraged to talk to a healthcare worker (doctor, nurse, or ASHA worker) if they feel low or overwhelmed, so they can get the proper help and support they deserve.

Section 11.5.9–11.5.10 · Growing Up Responsibly

Sexual Maturity, Safe Choices & Reproductive Health

Physical readiness comes first — emotional and social maturity take longer to develop

Sexual maturation means the body becomes capable of reproduction — sperm production in boys and menstrual cycles in girls happen gradually as part of overall growth. But this physical change does not mean someone is ready for adult responsibilities. Emotional maturity — handling feelings, communicating clearly, and making thoughtful decisions — develops on its own, longer timeline. Being ready for sexual activity is about far more than physical change; it requires the ability to make responsible, informed decisions.

Sexually Transmitted Infections (STIs)
What are STIs? Since sexual activity involves close physical contact, certain infections can pass from an infected person to an uninfected one. These are called Sexually Transmitted Infections and include gonorrhoea, herpes, syphilis, genital warts, and HIV (which can eventually lead to AIDS). Some of these are not yet curable. Using condoms can help prevent both their transmission and unwanted pregnancy.
Preventing Unwanted Pregnancy — Contraceptive Methods
🛡️
Barrier Methods
Condoms or vaginal covers physically stop sperm from reaching the egg — and condoms uniquely also reduce STI transmission.
💊
Hormonal Methods
Oral contraceptive pills change hormone levels to alter egg release, preventing pregnancy — though they may carry some side effects.
🌀
Intra-Uterine Devices (IUDs)
Devices like the Copper-T are placed inside the uterus to prevent pregnancy, though they may sometimes cause irritation.
⚕️
Surgical Methods
Permanently blocking the vas deferens (in males) or the fallopian tubes (in females) prevents sperm and egg from ever meeting.
Abortion: In some cases, an unwanted pregnancy can be ended by surgery — generally only within the first trimester, when the embryo is very small. The practice of self-selective abortion driven by gender preference is a serious concern, as it can severely imbalance a society's sex ratio. Prenatal sex determination is strictly prohibited by law in India to protect a healthy, forward-looking society.
Bridging Science and Society
India's Indigenous Contraceptive Breakthrough
Indian scientists at the Central Drug Research Institute (CDRI), Lucknow, developed the world's first non-steroidal and non-hormonal oral contraceptive pill. Taken just once weekly, it avoids common side effects like weight gain, nausea, or headaches. It is safe, convenient and effective, and is provided free through the National Family Planning Programme.

From plant embryos in a test tube to India's first test-tube baby

Father of Indian Embryology
P. Maheshwari
Indian Botanist · Pioneer of Plant Embryology
P. Maheshwari led the field of embryology — the study of plant reproductive organs. He developed the technique of in-vitro fertilisation in flowering plants, successfully fusing an egg and male gamete in a test tube to create new hybrid plants, and was among the first scientists to grow plant embryos on artificial nutrient media. His book, An Introduction to the Embryology of Angiosperms (1950), remains a classic reference for scientists worldwide.
Pioneer of Indian IVF · 1978
Subhash Mukhopadhyay
Kolkata, India
India holds a significant place in the history of In-vitro Fertilisation (IVF) — a technique where an egg and sperm are combined outside the body, usually in a laboratory dish, and the resulting fertilised egg is implanted in the uterus to begin a possible pregnancy. In 1978, Subhash Mukhopadhyay of Kolkata pioneered India's first test-tube baby, Kanupriya Agarwal (nicknamed Durga), through his own experimental IVF work — placing India among the earliest nations in the world to achieve this medical milestone.

Trimesters of Pregnancy
🌿
Asexual Reproduction
One parent, powered by mitosis, produces genetically identical clones. Seen as budding (yeast, hydra), spore formation (fungi), and vegetative propagation (cutting, grafting, layering, tissue culture).
🧬
Sexual Reproduction & Meiosis
Two parents contribute gametes formed by meiosis, which halves chromosome number. Random mixing of characters creates variation — vital for adaptation and survival of a species.
🌸
Flower Structure & Pollination
Sepal, petal, stamen (male) and pistil (female) make up a complete flower. Pollination — self or cross — transfers pollen from anther to stigma via wind, water, insects or birds.
🌰
Fertilisation & Seed Formation
The pollen tube delivers the male gamete to the ovule, forming a zygote. The ovary becomes the fruit; ovules become seeds, ready for dispersal and germination.
🐟
Animal Reproduction
External fertilisation (fish, frogs) needs many eggs since survival is low; internal fertilisation (reptiles, birds, mammals) protects the embryo, needing far fewer eggs.
👶
Human Reproduction
Testes and ovaries produce gametes via gametogenesis. Ovulation, fertilisation, implantation, and a nine-month pregnancy across three trimesters bring a new human into the world.
