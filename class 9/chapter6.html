Section 6.1 · Foundations

The Concept of Force

Force is the cause behind every change in motion — and it always has both a magnitude and a direction

What a Force Can Do
⚽
Set an object in motion
A ball at rest starts moving when you kick it — force changes it from zero to non-zero velocity
🏏
Change speed or direction
A cricket bat's force on the ball changes its speed and direction of motion simultaneously
🍋
Change the shape
Squeezing a lemon with your fingers applies force that deforms its shape
📐
Force is a Vector Quantity
Like displacement, velocity and acceleration, force requires both magnitude (how strong) and direction (which way). Changing either the magnitude or the direction — or both — changes the effect of the force on the object.
⚖️
SI Unit: The Newton (N)
The SI unit of force is the newton — written in small case (newton, not Newton) but symbolised with capital N. One newton is the force that produces 1 m s⁻² acceleration on a 1 kg object.
🌡️
Measuring Force
A spring balance measures force magnitude. It measures not just weight (gravitational force) but any force you apply to the spring. The stretch of the spring indicates the force.
🔬
Threads of Curiosity
The Scale of Forces in Nature
In everyday life, the smallest force we can directly feel is around millinewtons (10⁻³ N) — like a gentle touch. But scientists can measure forces far smaller: down to yoctonewtons (10⁻²⁴ N) in specialised experiments (as of 2026). That's a trillion trillion times smaller than what you can feel! Holding a 100 g object in your palm requires your palm to push up with about 1 N upward — that's what one newton of force feels like.
Section 6.2 · Net Force

Balanced and Unbalanced Forces

When multiple forces act on one object, what matters is the NET force — their combined effect

Balanced Forces
Net Force = Zero
Two forces are equal in magnitude but opposite in direction. The object does not accelerate — it either stays at rest or continues at constant velocity.
🪢 Tug of war — both teams pull equally → rope doesn't move
🏋️ Weightlifter holding barbell steady — gravity balanced by lift force
🏀 Ball floating on water — gravity balanced by buoyant force
Unbalanced Forces
Net Force ≠ Zero
Forces are unequal in magnitude or not perfectly opposite. A non-zero net force acts — the object accelerates in the direction of the net force.
🪢 One team pulls harder → rope moves toward stronger team
🚗 Two people push stalled car → net force = sum of both pushes
📦 Applied force > friction → box accelerates and moves

Net Force Rules: When two forces act on the same object — (1) Same direction: net force = sum of magnitudes, direction = same as both. (2) Opposite directions: net force = difference of magnitudes, direction = toward the larger force. (3) Equal and opposite: net force = zero, object doesn't accelerate.

Example 6.1 📦 Three Force Scenarios on One Block
Forces of 10 N and 6 N act on a block lying on a table. Find the net force magnitude and direction in three cases: (a) both rightward, (b) 10 N right + 6 N left, (c) 6 N right + 10 N left.
Solution
(a) Same direction (both right): Net force = 10 + 6 = 16 N, rightward

(b) Opposite, 10 N right + 6 N left: Net force = 10 − 6 = 4 N, rightward (direction of larger force)

(c) Opposite, 6 N right + 10 N left: Net force = 10 − 6 = 4 N, leftward (direction of larger force)
Key Note: Multiple forces may act on an object simultaneously, but its motion depends only on the net (resultant) force. Forces do not act on individual atoms — the single net force governs the entire object's motion.
⏸️
Pause and Ponder
1. A weightlifter lifts a barbell. List two forces acting on the barbell. Are they balanced if she keeps it steady?
2. In an arm-wrestling match, at the instant the arms tilt forward, are the forces balanced? Which player exerted the larger force?
Ready to Go Beyond — Non-parallel Forces
Forces at an Angle and Rotational Effects
The net force rules above apply only when forces are parallel or anti-parallel. When forces act at angles to each other, you need vector addition to find the resultant — you'll learn this in higher grades. Also, when equal and opposite forces are applied to the two ends of an extended object (like a handlebar or a tap), they cause the object to rotate rather than translate — this is called a torque or turning effect, also covered in higher grades.
Section 6.3 · Friction

The Force of Friction — Always Present, Often Overlooked

Friction opposes motion between surfaces in contact — its magnitude depends on the nature of those surfaces

📦
What Friction Does
Friction acts opposite to the direction of motion (or intended motion). When you push a box, friction pushes back. When you stop pushing, friction is the only force remaining — it decelerates and stops the object.
🌐
Forces on a Pushed Object
Four forces act on a box being pushed on a floor: Applied force (forward), Friction (backward), Gravitational force / weight (downward), Normal force (upward from surface). Weight and normal force are balanced; applied force and friction compete.
🏄
Friction Depends on Surface
The force of friction is different for different surfaces. Rough surfaces (wood) have higher friction; smooth surfaces (polished marble) have lower friction. Lower friction → slower deceleration → object travels farther before stopping.
🔬 Activity 6.1 Investigating Friction — Rubber Band & Coins on Different Surfaces
Stack four ₹10 coins and launch them with a rubber band stretched to the same point (mark C) on different surfaces: wooden table, laminated table, polished marble or tile floor. Measure the distance the coin stack travels on each surface.
Observation: The coin stack travels the largest distance on polished marble and the least on the rough wooden surface.
Why? The same initial force (rubber band stretched to same point C) gives the same initial velocity. After release, only friction acts. Less friction = slower deceleration = greater distance traveled.
Conclusion: Friction depends on the nature of the surfaces in contact. The smoother the surface, the weaker the friction and the further the object travels.
What if friction = 0? The coin stack would never stop — it would continue moving indefinitely at constant velocity. This is the key insight that led to Newton's First Law!
📏 Activity 6.2 Measuring Friction Force with a Spring Balance
Attach a wooden block to a spring balance lying horizontal on each surface from Activity 6.1. Pull the spring balance with gradually increasing force and note the reading when the block just starts moving.
When the block just starts moving at constant velocity (not accelerating), the net force is zero — so the spring reading equals the friction force exactly.
Result: Spring reading is largest on wood (most friction) and smallest on polished marble (least friction).
Cross-check with Activity 6.1: The surface where coins travelled furthest (smallest friction) gives the lowest spring reading, and vice versa. ✓
🧬 Think as a Scientist — Thought Experiment
Imagine: An object on a perfectly frictionless horizontal surface — zero friction. You give it a push (rubber band to C). What happens after it leaves the rubber band?

Answer: With no friction force to decelerate it, the object would never slow down. It would continue moving at the same velocity forever. This thought experiment was the basis of Galileo's revolutionary insight that motion does not require a continuous force to maintain it.
Section 6.4 · Newton's First Law

Newton's First Law of Motion

The Law of Inertia — objects resist changes to their state of motion

Newton's First Law of Motion
The Law of Inertia
"An object at rest remains at rest, and an object in motion continues to move with a constant velocity, unless a net force acts upon the object."
In other words: if the net force = zero, an object cannot begin to move, cannot stop if already moving, and cannot change its direction or speed. Its acceleration is zero. The tendency of objects to resist changes to their state is called inertia — coined by Newton himself.
💤
Object at Rest
An object at rest has zero velocity. Net force = 0 means it stays at zero velocity. Position–time graph: horizontal line. Velocity–time graph: flat line at v = 0.
➡️
Object in Uniform Motion
Constant velocity means both speed and direction are unchanged — straight-line motion. Net force = 0. Position–time graph: straight diagonal line. Velocity–time graph: flat horizontal line above zero.
⚠️
Common Misconception
For centuries people believed a force is needed to keep something moving. Wrong! Once moving on a frictionless surface, no force is needed to maintain constant velocity. Force is only needed to change velocity — to speed up, slow down, or turn.
⚠️ Common Misconception to Avoid
Many students think "if something is moving, a force must be acting on it." This is incorrect. A force is needed to change motion, not to maintain it. A ball sliding on ice at constant speed has zero net force — friction is simply very small. If there were truly no friction, it would slide forever with no force needed.
Example 6.2 📦 Applied Force = Friction — What Happens?
A person pushes a moving box in the forward direction with a force exactly equal to the force of friction. Will the box continue moving or stop?
Solution
Applied force (forward) = Friction force (backward) → they are equal and opposite → balanced forces → net force = 0.

By Newton's First Law, zero net force means the box continues moving at constant velocity. It does not accelerate and does not stop. You need to reduce the applied force below friction to let it slow down.
Example 6.3 📈 Graphs When Net Force = Zero
Draw position–time and velocity–time graphs for an object with zero net force acting on it.
Solution
Case 1 — Object at rest: Position–time graph is a horizontal line (position doesn't change). Velocity–time graph is a flat line at v = 0.

Case 2 — Object moving at constant velocity: Position–time graph is a straight diagonal line (equal position change in equal time). Velocity–time graph is a flat horizontal line above zero (velocity constant).

Key insight: Both cases have the same net force (zero) but look completely different on graphs. What they share is zero acceleration.
Important: If friction = 0, a net force must still be applied to set a resting object moving. But once it's moving, no further force is needed to maintain constant velocity. To change velocity — speed up, slow down, or turn — a force must be applied.

Section 6.5 · Newton's Second Law

Newton's Second Law of Motion

The quantitative law — relating force, mass and acceleration with F = ma

Newton's Second Law of Motion
F = ma — The Fundamental Relationship
"When a net force acts on an object, the object accelerates in the direction of the net force. The magnitude of acceleration is proportional to the net force and inversely proportional to the mass of the object."
Mathematically: a = F/m or equivalently F = ma, where F = net force (N), m = mass (kg), a = acceleration (m s⁻²). The direction of acceleration is the same as the direction of the net force.
Core Formula
F = m × a
Net Force (N) = Mass (kg) × Acceleration (m s⁻²)
Gravitational Force
F = m × g
Weight (N) = Mass (kg) × g (9.8 m s⁻²); g is the same for all objects regardless of mass
Definition of 1 Newton
1 N = 1 kg × 1 m s⁻²
The force that produces 1 m s⁻² acceleration on a 1 kg object. g ≈ 9.8 m s⁻² (use 10 for quick estimates)
💪
Force ↑ → Acceleration ↑
For the same mass, doubling the net force doubles the acceleration. A gentle push → small acceleration. A strong push → large acceleration. They are directly proportional: a ∝ F.
🏋️
Mass ↑ → Acceleration ↓
For the same force, doubling the mass halves the acceleration. A lighter object accelerates more than a heavier one. They are inversely proportional: a ∝ 1/m.
🌍
Gravity is Mass-Independent
g = 9.8 m s⁻² for all objects near Earth's surface regardless of mass — a feather and a hammer fall with the same acceleration in a vacuum. This is because both F and m scale together in F = mg.
🛒 Activity 6.3 Demonstration — Larger Force → Larger Acceleration (same mass)
A cart made from a cardboard box with ball-bearing wheels on a table. A thread over a pulley is attached to a hanging paper cup with objects inside (providing the driving force via gravity). Record a slow-motion video and compare times T₁ and T₂ for the same cart distance with different forces.
Cart starts at rest (u = 0) and travels same distance s in time T. From s = ½aT², we get a = 2s/T².
Since s is fixed: a₁/a₂ = T₂²/T₁². Doubling the cup mass doubles the force → acceleration doubles → time to cover same distance decreases.
Conclusion: For fixed mass, acceleration increases when net force increases. ✓ (Small discrepancy due to friction between wheels and surface)
🛒 Activity 6.4 Demonstration — Larger Mass → Smaller Acceleration (same force)
Keep the hanging cup mass (force) constant. Double the cart's mass by adding objects inside it. Compare the times to travel the same distance.
Same force, double the mass → acceleration halves → it takes longer to cover the same distance.
Conclusion: For fixed force, acceleration decreases when mass increases. a ∝ 1/m ✓
Together with Activity 6.3, these two experiments confirm that F = ma is the relationship between force, mass and acceleration.
Bridging Science and Society
🚗 Airbags — Newton's 2nd Law Saving Lives
In a collision, a vehicle stops abruptly. Without an airbag, a passenger's head hits the hard steering wheel in a very short time — producing huge acceleration (deceleration), which means a huge force on the head (F = ma). An airbag inflates instantly into a soft cushion, increasing the time over which the passenger decelerates. Same change in velocity, more time → smaller acceleration → smaller force → lower risk of serious injury. Seat belts work on the same principle.
🏏
Threads of Curiosity
Catching a Cricket Ball & Cracking a Coconut
When a fielder catches a fast ball, they pull their hands backwards with the ball. This increases the time for the ball to stop → smaller acceleration → smaller force on the hands → less pain and risk of injury. A cricket ball stops via F = ma: less a means less F.

Cracking a coconut works the opposite way — you bring it down at high velocity and stop it in a very short time. Short time → huge deceleration → huge force by the ground → the shell breaks!
Ready to Go Beyond — Momentum
The More Complete Form of Newton's Second Law
The complete form of Newton's second law is stated in terms of momentum — defined as the product of an object's mass and velocity (p = mv). Newton's second law states: "The rate of change of momentum of an object is proportional to the net force and takes place in the direction of that force." This form applies even when mass is not constant (e.g., rockets that burn fuel and lose mass). You will study momentum in detail in higher grades.
Example 6.4 🏋️ Weightlifter Holding a Barbell Steady
A weightlifter holds a barbell: 10 kg on each side + 10 kg bar. How much force is she applying to keep it steady?
Solution
Total mass = 10 + 10 + 10 = 30 kg
Gravitational force (downward) = F = mg = 30 × 9.8 = 294 N
To keep barbell steady (a = 0, net force = 0), she must apply 294 N upward.
Example 6.5 📦 Pushing a Block — With and Without Enough Force
A 25 kg stationary block. Max friction = 50 N. Find displacement in 2 s if pushed with (i) 50 N, (ii) 55 N.
Solution
(i) 50 N push: Applied force = Friction = 50 N → balanced → net force = 0 → block stays stationary. Displacement = 0 m.

(ii) 55 N push: Net force = 55 − 50 = 5 N (forward)
Acceleration = F/m = 5/25 = 0.2 m s⁻²
Displacement = ut + ½at² = 0 + ½ × 0.2 × 4 = 0.4 m forward
Example 6.6 🚗 Sports Car — Force from Velocity-Time Graph (1500 kg)
A 1500 kg sports car moves east. Velocity–time graph shows: 0–5 s (accelerating from 0 to 10 m/s), 5–10 s (constant 10 m/s), 10–15 s (decelerating from 10 to 0 m/s). Find force in each interval.
Solution
(i) 0–5 s: v = u + at → 10 = 0 + a×5 → a = 2 m s⁻². F = 1500 × 2 = 3000 N eastward

(ii) 5–10 s: Velocity is constant → a = 0 → F = 0 N

(iii) 10–15 s: 0 = 10 + a×5 → a = −2 m s⁻². F = 1500 × (−2) = −3000 N (westward, opposite to motion)

Section 6.6 · Newton's Third Law

Newton's Third Law of Motion

Every force has an equal and opposite partner — forces always come in pairs on two different objects

Newton's Third Law of Motion
Action and Reaction — Always in Pairs
"Whenever one object is exerting a force on a second object, the second object is simultaneously exerting an equal and opposite force on the first object."
The forces are equal in magnitude but opposite in direction. Crucially, they act on two different objects — so they never cancel each other. Newton's third law applies to all types of forces — contact forces AND non-contact forces (gravity, magnetism, electrostatics).
Critical Distinction: Third-law force pairs act on TWO DIFFERENT objects — they never cancel. But balanced forces (First Law) are two forces on the SAME object that cancel. Don't confuse these!
🪑 Activity 6.5 Chair on Wheels — Feeling Reaction Force
Sit on a wheeled chair with feet raised. Push a heavy table forward with both hands. Observe what happens to the chair. Then pull the table toward you — what happens?
Push table forward: You apply force on the table → table applies equal force on you backwards → your chair rolls backward.
Pull table toward you: You apply force on the table → table applies equal force on you forward → your chair rolls forward.
Conclusion: The table always applies an equal and opposite force on you. You cannot push or pull without experiencing a reaction in return.
⚖️ Activity 6.6 Two Spring Balances — Verifying Equal Forces
Connect two identical spring balances by their hooks. Fix one end to an immovable object. Pull the free end of the other. Read both scales simultaneously.
Observation: Both spring balances read exactly the same value, every time, regardless of how hard you pull.
This confirms that the force each spring applies on the other is equal in magnitude — action and reaction forces are always equal. Newton's Third Law verified ✓
🎈 Activity 6.7 Balloon Rocket — Action-Reaction in Action
Inflate a balloon. Thread a straw along a taut horizontal thread. Release the balloon neck and observe which way the balloon moves.
The balloon material pushes air molecules OUT through the neck (action force on air, backward).
The air rushing out pushes back on the balloon in the OPPOSITE direction (reaction force on balloon, forward).
The balloon moves FORWARD — in the direction opposite to the escaping air. This is exactly how a rocket works!
Walking
🚶
You Walk
You push the ground backwards with your foot. Ground pushes your foot forward (friction) — making you walk. No friction → foot slips backwards → you fall.
Grooved shoe soles increase friction. Wet floors are dangerous because friction is reduced.
Canoeing
🚣
Rowing
Paddle pushes water backwards. Water pushes paddle (and canoe) forward. Push harder → greater forward force → greater velocity. Harder paddle = faster canoe.
Speed also depends on drag, water currents, style of rowing, and canoe mass.
Rockets
🚀
Rocket Launch
Engine expels hot gas downward. Gas pushes rocket upward. If upward force > rocket weight, net force is upward → rocket lifts off.
Chandrayaan-3's Vikram lander fired engines in its direction of motion to decelerate for a soft Moon landing.
Bridging Science and Society
🧗 Climbing Trees, Grooved Shoes, and Icy Roads
A person climbing a coconut tree pushes their legs down against the trunk. The trunk pushes their legs up via friction — lifting them. Smooth trunk = less friction = harder to climb. The same Third-Law friction that helps you walk also keeps tyres on roads. Tyre treads increase contact area and friction. On icy or wet roads, friction drops drastically — tyres spin without grip, making driving dangerous. Grooved shoe soles use the same principle.
Example 6.7 🍎 Earth and Fruit — Equal Forces, Very Different Accelerations
Earth and a fruit apply equal and opposite gravitational forces on each other. Why does the fruit fall toward Earth, but Earth doesn't visibly move toward the fruit?
Solution
By Newton's Third Law, force on fruit by Earth = force on Earth by fruit (equal magnitude).

By Newton's Second Law: a = F/m. The Earth's mass is enormously larger than the fruit's mass. The same force produces an acceleration on the Earth so tiny (≈10⁻²⁵ m s⁻²) that it is completely undetectable. The fruit, being much lighter, accelerates at g = 9.8 m s⁻² — visibly falling.
Example 6.8 🔫 Gun Recoil — Equal Force, Different Accelerations
A 0.1 kg bullet is fired from a 5 kg gun with a force of 2 N. What are the initial accelerations of bullet and gun?
Solution
By Newton's Third Law: recoil force on gun = 2 N (equal to force on bullet).

Acceleration of gun = F/m_gun = 2/5 = 0.4 m s⁻² (backward)
Acceleration of bullet = F/m_bullet = 2/0.1 = 20 m s⁻² (forward)

Same force → very different accelerations because their masses are very different. The gun barely recoils; the bullet screams forward.

Section 6.7 · Advanced Application

Forces Acting on a System of Objects

Treat connected objects as a single system — internal forces cancel, only external forces matter

The System Approach
Two boxes (mass m₁ and m₂) connected by a string on a frictionless surface. A force F pulls Box 1 to the right. Box 1 pulls Box 2 via string (tension T). By Newton's Third Law, Box 2 pulls Box 1 back via the string. Tension acts as an internal force between the boxes.
Internal Forces
Tension T between Box 1 and Box 2 via the string. Internal forces cancel when treating the whole system — they always occur in equal and opposite pairs within the system.
External Forces
The applied force F pulling Box 1 comes from outside the system. This is the only force that changes the system's total momentum. Gravity and normal force also cancel (balanced, vertical).
System Acceleration
a = F / (m₁ + m₂)
The whole connected system accelerates as if it were a single object of mass m₁ + m₂ with only external force F acting on it.
🧩
Why This Works
Internal forces (tension, normal contact between connected parts) come in equal-opposite pairs — they sum to zero within the system. Only external forces can accelerate the entire system. This is a powerful simplification!
🏃
Your Body as a System
While walking, your arms and legs move in complex ways (internal forces galore). Yet your overall centre of mass motion can be studied by treating your whole body as a single object subject to external friction force from the ground.
🚂
Multiple Connected Objects
A train engine pulling several carriages, a rocket burning fuel, a tractor with a harrow — all are systems. Once external force and total mass are known, a = F_external / m_total gives the acceleration of every part simultaneously.
Ready to Go Beyond — External Force Balance
All External Forces on a Two-Box System
In addition to the horizontal applied force F, the full set of external forces on the two-box system includes: downward gravitational force (m₁ + m₂)g from the Earth, balanced by upward normal force N₁ + N₂ from the ground. These vertical forces cancel. The only unbalanced external force is F (horizontal) — so it alone produces the system's acceleration a = F/(m₁+m₂).
🌌
Threads of Curiosity — The Power of System Thinking
Science Gets Simpler When You Look at the Whole
Newton's laws describe motion across an enormous range of scales — from a marble rolling on a desk to planets orbiting stars. They need modification only in three extreme cases: very close to massive objects (General Relativity), at speeds near the speed of light (Special Relativity), and at very small (atomic) scales (Quantum Mechanics). For everything in between — from engineering to astronomy — Newton's three laws are exact and complete.
Meet the Scientists

The Minds Behind the Laws

Two giants of science who revolutionised our understanding of motion

Meet a Scientist · 17th Century
Galileo Galilei
Italian Physicist & Astronomer · Pioneer of Thought Experiments
In ancient times it was believed that a force was always required to keep an object moving at constant velocity — a concept that went unchallenged for centuries.

In the 17th century, Galileo challenged this through a brilliant series of thought experiments. He argued that if a body moves along a horizontal plane and all impediments to its motion are removed (friction, air resistance), it will continue to move indefinitely — without needing any continuing force. This was a revolutionary idea that overturned ancient Greek physics and laid the foundation for Newton's laws.
Meet a Scientist · 1687
Isaac Newton
English Mathematician & Physicist · Author of the Principia
Building on Galileo's work, Newton coined the word 'inertia' to describe the tendency of objects to resist changes in their state of rest or uniform motion. He used this to frame his first law of motion.

In 1687, Newton published Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica containing all three laws of motion — a defining moment in the history of science. The laws described motion from falling apples to planetary orbits using the same mathematics.

Naming convention: When a unit is named after a person, the full form is in lowercase (newton, not Newton) but the symbol is capitalised (N, not n).

⚡
Force — Vector Quantity
SI unit: newton (N). Has both magnitude and direction. Measured with spring balance. Can start motion, stop motion, change direction, or change shape. Effect depends on NET force, not individual forces.
⚖️
Balanced vs Unbalanced
Balanced (net F = 0): no acceleration. Unbalanced (net F ≠ 0): object accelerates in direction of net force. Same direction → add magnitudes. Opposite directions → subtract, take direction of larger.
🤚
Friction
Acts opposite to direction of motion. Depends on nature of surfaces. Weight (normal force) also acts, balanced by ground reaction. Air friction usually negligible. Four forces on a pushed object: applied, friction, weight, normal.
💤
Newton's 1st Law (Inertia)
Objects resist change. At rest → stays at rest. Moving → stays at constant velocity. UNLESS net force acts. No force needed to maintain constant velocity — only to change it. Zero net force = zero acceleration.
📐
Newton's 2nd Law (F=ma)
Net force = mass × acceleration. Direction of a = direction of F. g = 9.8 m s⁻² for all objects. Weight = mg. More force → more acceleration. More mass → less acceleration for same force.
🔄
Newton's 3rd Law
Every force has an equal and opposite reaction on a DIFFERENT object. Forces are equal in magnitude, opposite in direction. Applies to ALL forces — contact and non-contact. Equal forces don't mean equal acceleration (masses differ).
🔗
Systems of Objects
Connected objects treated as one system. Internal forces (tension) cancel. Only external forces produce system acceleration. a = F_external / (m₁ + m₂). Simplifies complex problems enormously.
🚀
Rocket Propulsion
Engine expels gas downward (action). Gas pushes rocket upward (reaction). If upward reaction > rocket weight → net force upward → liftoff. Chandrayaan-3 used reverse thrust to brake for Moon landing.
