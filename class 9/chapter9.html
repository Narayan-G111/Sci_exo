Section 9.1 · Core Law

Law of Conservation of Mass

Matter can neither be created nor destroyed in a chemical reaction — mass is always conserved

The Big Idea
Total Mass of Reactants = Total Mass of Products

When hydrogen and oxygen combine to form water, the water formed is a completely different substance — it neither burns nor helps burning, unlike its components. Yet the mass of water formed equals the sum of masses of hydrogen and oxygen that combined. This remarkable fact holds true for every physical and chemical change.

⚖️
Physical Change — Mass Conserved
When salt dissolves in water, the mass of the solution equals the mass of water plus the mass of salt added. When paper is torn, total mass doesn't change. Mass is conserved in all physical changes.
🧪
Chemical Change — Also Conserved
Even when vinegar reacts with baking soda to produce carbon dioxide, the total mass of products (CO₂ + other substances) equals the total mass of reactants. Atoms merely rearrange — none created, none destroyed.
🔬
Why Lavoisier's Law Matters
Proposed by Antoine Lavoisier in 1789, this law is the foundation of all quantitative chemistry. It tells us that chemical reactions are about rearrangement of atoms, not creation or disappearance of matter.

Activities 9.1, 9.2 & 9.3

Investigating Conservation of Mass

Three hands-on experiments that prove mass is always conserved

⚗️ Activity 9.1 Physical Change — Salt Dissolving in Water
A beaker is placed on a digital balance and tared to zero. About 50 mL of water is added, then a spatula of common salt is added. The mass is recorded before and after the salt dissolves.
Observation: Mass of solution = mass of water + mass of salt. The balance reading does NOT change when salt dissolves.
Conclusion: Mass is conserved during physical changes. The salt is still there — just broken into invisible particles dispersed throughout the water.
Extension: Tearing paper into pieces also conserves mass. Try it — all pieces together weigh exactly as much as the original sheet.
⚗️ Activity 9.2 Chemical Change — Set-up 1 vs Set-up 2
Vinegar is placed in a conical flask; baking soda is placed in a balloon. The two are weighed together before allowing them to react.
Set-up 1 (Open system): Baking soda is poured from balloon into flask. After reaction, the balance reading drops. Why? The CO₂ gas escapes into the atmosphere — mass appears to decrease, but it hasn't really.
Set-up 2 (Closed system): Balloon is tied to flask mouth. Baking soda is mixed into vinegar by tilting the balloon. The balloon inflates with CO₂ gas — no gas escapes. Final reading matches initial reading exactly.
Key Learning: The Law of Conservation of Mass holds only when the system is closed — all products (including gases) must be accounted for.
⚠️ Experimental Note: Measurements on digital balances have an uncertainty of ±1 in the last digit. Small variations are within experimental error — not violations of the law.
⚗️ Activity 9.3 Group Activity — Sodium Sulfate + Barium Chloride
10 mL of 1% sodium sulfate solution is placed in Flask A, and 10 mL of 1% barium chloride solution is placed in Flask B. Both flasks are weighed together on the balance (initial reading). Then Flask B's contents are poured into Flask A and the two flasks are weighed again.
Observation: A white precipitate of barium sulfate appears. No gas is formed in this open-system reaction.
Result: Final reading = Initial reading. Mass is conserved.
Reaction: Na₂SO₄ + BaCl₂ → BaSO₄ (white precipitate) + 2NaCl
Why keep both flasks on balance? A small amount of solution may remain stuck to the walls — both flasks must be weighed together to avoid this error.
Example 9.1 🧮 Verify Conservation of Mass — Calcium Carbonate + HCl
Students react 4.0 g of calcium carbonate with 2.92 g of HCl in a closed container. They measure 1.76 g of CO₂, 0.72 g of water, and 4.44 g of calcium chloride. Is mass conserved?
Solution
Reactants: 4.0 g CaCO₃ + 2.92 g HCl = 6.92 g
Products: 1.76 g CO₂ + 0.72 g H₂O + 4.44 g CaCl₂ = 6.92 g

Mass of reactants = Mass of products = 6.92 g ✓ Law of Conservation of Mass is obeyed.
Example 9.2 🔥 Carbon Burning in Oxygen
12 g of carbon combines with 32 g of oxygen to form 44 g of carbon dioxide (C + O₂ → CO₂). If 2.4 g of carbon reacts completely with oxygen, how much CO₂ is produced?
Solution
12 g carbon → 44 g CO₂ (given ratio)
1 g carbon → 44/12 g CO₂
2.4 g carbon → (44/12) × 2.4 = 8.8 g of CO₂

Section 9.2 · Proust's Law

Law of Constant Proportions

Also called Law of Definite Proportions — the building blocks of compounds always combine in the same ratio

Joseph Louis Proust's Discovery

In any compound formed by two or more elements, the elements always combine in the same fixed ratio by mass, regardless of the source or method of preparation. This is true no matter where or how the compound is found.

💧
Water — Always H:O = 1:8
Water from rivers, borewells, rain, or the ocean — when purified and analysed — always contains hydrogen and oxygen in mass ratio 1:8. Decomposing 9 g of pure water always gives 1 g H₂ and 8 g O₂.
🧂
NaCl — Always Na:Cl = 23:35.5
Salt made from evaporating seawater, mined from the earth, or synthesised in a lab — always the same ratio. You can't make NaCl with a different ratio of sodium to chlorine.
🪨
Compound vs Mixture
This law applies to compounds only, not mixtures. In a mixture, components can combine in any ratio. Salt water can be 1% or 10% salty — but NaCl is always 39.3% Na and 60.7% Cl.
🪨
Threads of Curiosity
Cinnabar — Known Across Ancient Civilisations
In ancient India, a red rock pigment was known as hingula (in Sanskrit), and as cinnabar in Latin. Across many civilisations, heating cinnabar yielded two elements: mercury (86.22%) and sulfur (13.78%) — always in the same proportion. This consistency across millennia and cultures is a perfect illustration of the Law of Constant Proportions. Grinding mercury and sulfur back together in this exact ratio reforms cinnabar — though the toxic nature of both elements made this uncommon practice.
Example 9.3 🧂 Sodium Chloride — Chlorine Needed
NaCl contains Na and Cl in mass ratio 23:35.5. If 46 g of sodium reacts completely, how much chlorine is needed?
Solution
Na : Cl = 23 : 35.5
For 46 g of sodium: Cl needed = (35.5 ÷ 23) × 46 = 71 g of chlorine

Section 9.3 · Atomic Theory

Dalton's Atomic Theory

The theoretical framework that explained both conservation laws — proposed in 1808

The two laws of mass conservation and constant proportions raised a deeper question: why do substances combine in fixed ratios and why is mass never lost? John Dalton answered both by proposing that matter is made of atoms — tiny particles that cannot be created, destroyed, or split in chemical reactions — they only rearrange.

The Six Postulates of Dalton's Atomic Theory
1
All matter is made up of very tiny particles called atoms, which participate in chemical reactions.
2
Atoms are indivisible particles — they cannot be created or destroyed in a chemical reaction.
3
All atoms of a given element are identical in mass and chemical properties.
4
Atoms of different elements have different masses and chemical properties.
5
Atoms combine in the ratio of simple whole numbers to form compounds.
6
The relative number and kinds of atoms are constant in a given compound.
💧
Water — Atoms Rearranging
Hydrogen atoms and oxygen atoms combine to form water. The hydrogen and oxygen atoms aren't destroyed — they just bond together. Postulate 2 explains Conservation of Mass.
🔥
Magnesium Burning
When magnesium burns in air, magnesium atoms combine with oxygen atoms to form magnesium oxide (white powder). Atoms rearrange; no new atoms are made and none disappear.
🧱
Fixed Ratios Explained
Because atoms of an element are all identical (postulate 3) and combine only in whole-number ratios (postulate 5), a compound always has the same elemental ratio — explaining Proust's Law.

Section 9.4 · Chemical Bonding

How Atoms Combine

Atoms seek stability through their valence shell — two pathways to a full octet

Why Do Atoms Bond?

A molecule is an electrically neutral entity of more than one atom that can exist independently and shows all the properties of that substance. Atoms bond because doing so lowers the total energy of the system — making the arrangement more stable. Atoms with fewer than 8 valence electrons (or 2 for K-shell only atoms) seek to complete their outer shell. The force holding atoms together is called a chemical bond.

Sharing of Electrons
Each atom contributes electrons to a shared pool. Both atoms "own" the shared electrons → Covalent Bond
Transfer of Electrons
One atom gives electrons; the other receives them. Both become ions → Ionic Bond

H needs 1 electron; Cl needs 1 electron. They share 1 pair → H—Cl (single covalent bond). HCl is a covalent compound. The shared electrons give both atoms a complete valence shell.

Water (H₂O)

The prefix "co-" means together; "-valent" refers to valence electrons. Covalent = valence electrons shared together. These bonds form mainly between non-metal atoms and result in electrically neutral molecules.

Section 9.4.2 · Ionic Bonding

Ionic Bonds — Transfer of Electrons

When metals give electrons to non-metals — creating oppositely charged ions that attract each other

If a valence shell has fewer than 4 electrons, the atom generally donates those electrons to achieve a stable configuration. The receiving atom (with more than 4 valence electrons) accepts them. The result: one positively charged cation and one negatively charged anion, held together by electrostatic attraction — an ionic bond.

Formation of Sodium Chloride (NaCl) — The Classic Example
⚡
Step 1 — Sodium Loses 1 Electron
Sodium (atomic number 11) has electronic configuration 2,8,1. Its valence shell has only 1 electron. Sodium loses this electron → becomes Na⁺ (11 protons, 10 electrons → net +1 charge). Na⁺ is called the sodium cation.
⚡
Step 2 — Chlorine Gains 1 Electron
Chlorine (atomic number 17) has configuration 2,8,7. It needs 1 more electron to complete its octet. Chlorine gains the electron from Na → becomes Cl⁻ (17 protons, 18 electrons → net -1 charge). Cl⁻ is the chloride anion.
🔗
Step 3 — Electrostatic Attraction
Na⁺ (positive) and Cl⁻ (negative) attract each other with a strong electrostatic force. This force of attraction between oppositely charged ions is the ionic bond. Together they form NaCl.
🔷
Crystal Structure of Ionic Compounds
NaCl — A 3D Crystal, Not Separate Molecules
Ionic compounds don't exist as individual pairs. In sodium chloride, each Na⁺ is surrounded by six Cl⁻ ions, and each Cl⁻ is surrounded by six Na⁺ ions — arranged in a regular 3D repeating pattern called a crystal lattice. This structure gives ionic compounds their characteristic cubic crystal shape and makes them very hard with high melting points.

Section 9.6 · Compound Properties

Properties of Ionic vs Covalent Compounds

How the type of chemical bond determines the behaviour of a substance

🔬 Activity 9.4 Solubility and Electrical Conductivity Experiment
Collect samples of camphor, sodium chloride, copper sulfate, sugar, and naphthalene. Test each for solubility in water, kerosene, and petrol. Then test electrical conductivity of each solid and each dissolved in water using a 9V battery, electrodes, and a light bulb.
Safety First: Do not touch electrodes connected to battery. Petrol and kerosene are flammable — handle carefully away from flames.
Sodium chloride, Copper sulfate (ionic): Dissolve in water. Insoluble in kerosene/petrol. Conduct electricity when dissolved in water (ions move freely). Do NOT conduct in solid state (ions fixed in lattice).
Camphor, Naphthalene (non-polar covalent): Insoluble in water. Dissolve in kerosene/petrol. Do NOT conduct electricity (no free ions).
Sugar (polar covalent): Dissolves in water but does NOT conduct electricity — it forms no ions in solution.

Why can't ionic solids conduct electricity? In solid ionic compounds, ions are held in fixed positions by strong electrostatic forces. To conduct electricity, charged particles must be free to move. This freedom is achieved when ionic compounds dissolve in water (ions dissociate) or are melted (ions can move freely).

Sections 9.7 & 9.8 · Calculations

Molecular Mass and Formula Unit Mass

Calculating the mass of a molecule (covalent) or a formula unit (ionic)

⚖️
Molecular Mass (Covalent)
The total mass of a molecule, calculated by adding up the atomic masses of all atoms in the formula. Used for covalent compounds that form actual molecules. Unit: u (unified atomic mass unit).
🔷
Formula Unit Mass (Ionic)
Ionic compounds don't form individual molecules — they form crystal lattices. The "formula unit" is the simplest whole-number ratio of ions. The mass of one formula unit is the formula unit mass.
Example 9.4 💧 Molecular Mass of Water (H₂O)
Find the molecular mass of H₂O. Atomic masses: H = 1 u, O = 16 u
Solution
Molecular mass of H₂O = (1 u × 2) + (16 u × 1) = 2 + 16 = 18 u
Example 9.5 💨 Molecular Mass of Carbon Dioxide (CO₂)
Find the molecular mass of CO₂. Atomic masses: C = 12 u, O = 16 u
Solution
Molecular mass of CO₂ = (12 u × 1) + (16 u × 2) = 12 + 32 = 44 u
Example 9.6 🔶 Formula Unit Mass of Sodium Oxide (Na₂O)
Find the formula unit mass of Na₂O. Atomic masses: Na = 23 u, O = 16 u
Solution
Formula unit mass of Na₂O = (23 u × 2) + (16 u × 1) = 46 + 16 = 62 u
Example 9.7 🧪 Formula Unit Mass of Calcium Nitrate Ca(NO₃)₂
Find the formula unit mass of Ca(NO₃)₂. Atomic masses: Ca = 40 u, N = 14 u, O = 16 u
Solution
Formula unit mass = (40 u × 1) + [(14 u × 1) + (16 u × 3)] × 2
= 40 + [14 + 48] × 2
= 40 + 62 × 2
= 40 + 124 = 164 u

Meet the Scientists

The Minds Behind the Laws

Three scientists whose experimental work built the foundations of modern chemistry

Father of Modern Chemistry
Antoine Lavoisier
French Chemist · 1743–1794 · Law of Conservation of Mass (1789)
Lavoisier revolutionised chemistry by insisting on precise measurements and quantitative experiments. He proposed that "…in every operation an equal quantity of matter exists both before and after the operation" — a statement that laid the foundation for all of quantitative chemistry. His careful experiments with combustion, respiration, and chemical reactions proved that mass is always conserved. The Law of Conservation of Mass is named in his honour, and he is widely credited as the Father of Modern Chemistry.
Law of Definite Proportions
Joseph Louis Proust
French Chemist · 1754–1826 · Proust's Law
Proust was known for his meticulous experimental work. He spent years carefully analysing chemical compounds and showing that their composition never varied — regardless of how the compound was made or where it was found. His most famous study was on copper carbonate — he showed that it always contained copper, carbon and oxygen in the same proportions by mass, no matter its origin. This directly led to the Law of Definite Proportions (Proust's Law), which became one of the pillars of Dalton's Atomic Theory.
Atomic Theory · 1808
John Dalton
English Chemist & Physicist · 1766–1844
Born in England, Dalton moved to Manchester in 1793 to teach mathematics, physics and chemistry. He spent most of his life teaching and researching there. In 1808, he presented his Atomic Theory — a theoretical framework that explained both the Law of Conservation of Mass and the Law of Constant Proportions by proposing that matter is made of indivisible, identical atoms that simply rearrange during chemical reactions. This was a turning point in the scientific understanding of matter and led to modern chemistry as we know it today.
Bridging Science and Society
Nuclear Energy — Atoms That Power Nations
Atoms can release enormous energy when their nuclei split (fission) or combine (fusion) to form new elements. This is called atomic or nuclear energy. Beyond electricity generation, it is used in medicine, scientific research, and space exploration. In nuclear power plants, thermal energy from nuclear reactions produces steam that drives turbines — a cleaner alternative to fossil fuels. In India, scientists like Raja Ramanna (often called the Father of the Indian Nuclear Programme) made significant contributions in developing the nation's Nuclear Energy Programme and promoting its peaceful use for development.
💡 Value Insight Together We Are Strong
Just as atoms share electrons to form covalent bonds and build stable molecules, we too can share and care to build strong relationships with people around us. This sharing brings unity and stability, laying the foundation for a stronger community — and ultimately, a strong nation.

Dalton's Atomic Theory
All matter is made of tiny, indivisible atoms. Atoms of the same element are identical. Atoms combine in simple whole-number ratios. Atoms are not created or destroyed in reactions — they rearrange.
🤝
Covalent Bond
Formed by sharing of electrons between atoms (usually non-metals). Single bond = 1 shared pair. Double bond = 2 shared pairs. Covalent compounds have low melting points, don't conduct electricity.
⚡
Ionic Bond
Formed by transfer of electrons — donor becomes cation (+), receiver becomes anion (−). Electrostatic attraction holds ions together. Ionic compounds have high melting points, conduct when dissolved or molten.
🧮
Chemical Formulae
Written using criss-cross valency method. Covalent formula: number of atoms of each element. Ionic formula: simplest ratio of ions. Formula unit mass = sum of atomic masses in one formula unit.
