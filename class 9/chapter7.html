Section 7.1 · Foundations

Work Done by a Constant Force

In science, "work" has a precise meaning — force must displace an object in the direction of the force

The Scientific Definition of Work
Work done on an object by a constant force = force applied × displacement in the direction of the force
Work Formula
W = F × s
W = work (J) · F = force (N) · s = displacement in direction of force (m)
SI Unit of Work
1 J = 1 N × 1 m
1 joule = force of 1 newton displacing object by 1 metre · Also: 1 J = 1 kg m² s⁻²

Two key insights from lifting bags: Lifting 3 bags one after another to 1 m = 3× work of lifting 1 bag. Lifting 1 bag to 3 m = 3× work of lifting to 1 m. Work ∝ Force AND Work ∝ Displacement — both scale the work done proportionally.

📊
Force-Displacement Graph
Work done = area under the force-displacement graph. For a constant force of 10 N over 1 m: area = 10 N × 1 m = 10 J. Even for variable forces, the area under the graph gives total work done.
📝
Always Specify
When describing work, always specify: (1) the force (or agency) doing the work and (2) the object on which work is done. Work is not a vector — it has no direction but can be positive or negative.
7.1.1 — When Is Work Done Equal to Zero?
🧱
Zero Displacement (s = 0)
Pushing a rigid wall: you apply force but the wall doesn't move → W = F × 0 = 0. You feel tired because your muscles use internal energy repeatedly contracting and expanding, but no scientific work is done on the wall.
📦
Zero Force (F = 0)
If no force acts on the object, W = 0 × s = 0 regardless of displacement. An object coasting on a frictionless surface — no applied force, no work done by any agent.
🚶
Force ⊥ Displacement
Carrying a box horizontally: you apply an upward force but the box moves horizontally — perpendicular directions. No displacement in the direction of force → W = 0. (Higher grades: W = Fs cosθ, cos 90° = 0.)
7.1.2 — Positive and Negative Work Done
Positive Work
Force & Displacement: Same Direction
Pushing a wheelchair forward — force applied forward, displacement forward → W > 0. Lifting a dumbbell upward — force upward, displacement upward → positive work. Object gains energy.
Negative Work
Force & Displacement: Opposite Directions
Goalkeeper stopping a ball — force backward on ball, ball moves forward → W < 0. Lowering a dumbbell — holding force upward, displacement downward → negative work. Object loses energy.
Example 7.1🏋️Dumbbell — Positive vs Negative Work
A girl lifts a dumbbell up then slowly lowers it. When does she do positive work and when negative?
Solution
Lifting up: Applied force is upward (equal to weight). Displacement is upward. Same direction → Positive work on dumbbell.

Lowering down: Applied force is still upward (to control descent). Displacement is downward. Opposite directions → Negative work on dumbbell.
Example 7.2⚽Goalkeeper Stopping a Ball (F = 200 N, s = 15 cm)
A goalkeeper's hand moved back 15 cm while applying 200 N to stop a ball. How much work did she do on the ball?
Solution
Force is opposite to the ball's displacement → negative work.
Displacement in direction of force = −0.15 m (negative because opposite to force direction).
W = F × s = 200 N × (−0.15 m) = −30 J

Note: The ball does +30 J on the goalkeeper (Newton's 3rd Law — equal and opposite forces, both do work on each other).

Section 7.2 · Core Theorem

The Work-Energy Theorem

Work done on an object equals the change in its energy — the bridge between force, motion and energy

The Work-Energy Theorem
Work done on an object = Change in its energy
When positive work is done on an object, it gains energy. When negative work is done, it loses energy. This theorem holds for any system of objects and even when forces are not constant — making it far more powerful than Newton's laws alone for complex situations.
🏏
Cricket Ball & Wickets
A fielder does work on a ball by throwing it. The ball gains energy. The moving ball then does work on the wickets, transferring its energy — making them fall. Work done → energy gained → energy transferred.
🌸
Flowerpot at Height
Work was done to raise the flowerpot to a height. It gained energy stored as potential energy. When released, that energy does work on objects below — creating damage on impact.
⚡
Ways Energy Transfers
Work is one way to transfer energy. Others: heat (hot to cold), radiation (Sun to Earth), electric circuits, sound waves, nuclear reactions. SI unit of energy = joule (J), same as work.
Example 7.3🎯Carrom — Energy Changes at Each Collision
In a carrom shot: striker hits white coin, which hits black coin. Who does work and what energy changes occur?
Solution
Striker → White coin: Striker does positive work on white coin → white coin gains energy. By Newton's 3rd Law, white coin does negative work on striker → striker loses energy.

White coin → Black coin: White coin does positive work on black coin → black coin gains energy. Black coin does negative work on white coin → white coin loses energy.
Meet a Scientist
James Prescott Joule
English Physicist · Pioneer of Thermodynamics
The SI unit of work and energy — the joule (J) — is named after James Prescott Joule. He conducted painstaking experiments showing that mechanical energy and thermal energy are interconvertible — that doing mechanical work can produce heat, and vice versa. This unified understanding of different forms of energy as manifestations of the same fundamental quantity was revolutionary, and helped lay the foundation of thermodynamics and the law of conservation of energy.

Section 7.3 · Energy Landscape

Forms of Energy

Energy is the capacity to do work — it exists in seven major forms, all interconvertible

Mechanical
⚙️
Mechanical Energy
Energy due to motion (kinetic) or position (potential) of objects. The focus of this chapter. Directly connected to forces and kinematics.
Moving bicycle, raised ball, stretched spring
Thermal
🔥
Thermal Energy
Energy that makes things warm or hot. Related to kinetic energy of atoms and molecules vibrating randomly inside matter.
Electric heater, friction heating, fire
Electrical
⚡
Electrical Energy
Energy related to position or motion of electric charges. Transferred through circuits and used in nearly all modern technology.
Rotating fan, phone charging, light bulb
💡
Light Energy
Energy that allows us to see. Electromagnetic radiation. Electrical energy → light energy in a bulb. Solar panels convert light energy back to electrical.
🔊
Sound Energy
Energy of vibrations propagating through air or other molecules. A ringing bell converts mechanical energy → sound energy. Microphone converts sound → electrical.
⚗️
Chemical Energy
Energy stored in chemical bonds between atoms — in food, fuel, batteries. Food's chemical energy → mechanical energy in muscles. Fuel → mechanical energy in engines.
☢️
Nuclear Energy
Energy stored in the nuclei of atoms. Released by fission (splitting) or fusion (combining) of nuclei. Powers the Sun through nuclear fusion, and nuclear power plants through fission.
Bridging Science and Society
🌊 The Gharat / Panchakki — Traditional Water Mill of the Himalayas
In the Himalayan region, water flowing downhill from heights converts its gravitational potential energy into kinetic energy as it rushes down a pipe (A). The moving water's kinetic energy drives a wheel (B) into rotational motion. The wheel is connected to a grinding stone (C) that grinds grain — converting kinetic energy into mechanical work. Modern hydroelectric dams use the same principle on a massive scale: stored water at height → kinetic energy in turbines → electrical energy for cities.
Section 7.4.1 · Mechanical Energy

Kinetic Energy

The energy an object possesses due to its motion — K = ½mv²

Deriving K = ½mv² from the Work-Energy Theorem
Starting from rest (u = 0), a force F acts on a mass m over displacement s, reaching velocity v. From kinematics: s = (v² − u²)/2a = v²/2a. Work done: W = F × s = ma × v²/2a = ½mv². By the Work-Energy Theorem, this work equals the kinetic energy gained.
Kinetic Energy
K = ½mv²
m = mass (kg) · v = speed (m s⁻¹) · K = kinetic energy (J). No direction — it's a scalar quantity.
General Form (Work-Energy Theorem)
W = ½mv² − ½mu²
Change in KE = Work done. Works for any initial velocity u, not just u = 0.
🚀
Velocity Doubles → KE Quadruples
Since K ∝ v², if v doubles, K increases by a factor of 2² = 4. A vehicle at 2v has four times the kinetic energy of the same vehicle at v. This is why high speeds are so dangerous in collisions — the energy to be absorbed is far greater.
📈
KE Change with Work Done
Positive work → velocity increases → KE increases. Negative work → velocity decreases → KE decreases. Zero work (W = 0) → velocity unchanged → KE constant.
🎳
KE Has No Direction
Kinetic energy is a scalar — it has magnitude only, no direction. A ball moving left at 5 m/s and a ball moving right at 5 m/s have identical kinetic energy. Only the speed matters, not the direction of motion.
Example 7.4🚗Velocity Doubles — How Does KE Change?
If the velocity of a vehicle doubles in magnitude, what happens to its kinetic energy?
Solution
Initial KE = ½mv². New KE = ½m(2v)² = ½m × 4v² = 4 × ½mv².
The new KE = 4 times the original. Kinetic energy scales with the square of velocity.
Example 7.5🏏KE of Indian Cricket Ball at 154.8 km h⁻¹
An Indian cricketer bowls a 0.2 kg ball at 154.8 km h⁻¹. Find its kinetic energy.
Solution
Mass = 0.2 kg · Velocity = 154.8 km h⁻¹ = 43 m s⁻¹
K = ½mv² = ½ × 0.2 × 43² = ½ × 0.2 × 1849 = 184.9 J
Example 7.6✈️Jet Aircraft Stopped by Deck Wire (15000 kg, 100 m stop)
A 15000 kg jet is stopped within 100 m by a wire exerting 367500 N. What was its initial velocity?
Solution
Work done by wire = −367500 × 100 = −36750000 J (negative, opposing motion)
By Work-Energy Theorem: ΔKE = Work done
0 − ½ × 15000 × v² = −36750000
v² = 73500000/15000 = 4900 → v = 70 m s⁻¹ = 252 km h⁻¹

Section 7.4.2 · Stored Energy

Potential Energy

Energy stored due to an object's deformation or the relative positions of objects in a system

🏹
Elastic Potential Energy
A stretched rubber band (gulel/slingshot) or bent bow stores energy due to deformation of shape. When released, this stored energy converts to kinetic energy of the projectile. Internal forces within the elastic material store and release the energy.
🧲
Magnetic & Electric PE
Separating unlike magnet poles against the attractive force stores magnetic potential energy. Similarly, separating unlike electric charges stores electrostatic PE. Both are due to relative positions of objects in the system.
🌍
Gravitational PE
Lifting a ball away from Earth against gravity stores gravitational PE in the Earth-ball system. The greater the height, the more energy stored. When released, both Earth and ball move toward each other — but Earth barely moves due to its enormous mass.
🔬 Activity 7.1 Ball Dropped into Sand — Investigating Gravitational PE
Drop a heavy ball into a container of loose sand from heights of 1 m, 2 m, and 3 m at different positions. Compare the depth of depressions created.
The depression is deepest when the ball is dropped from the greatest height. More height = more work done lifting = more PE stored = more energy released on impact = deeper depression.
Conclusion: The greater the height of the ball above the Earth's surface, the greater its gravitational potential energy.
Deriving Gravitational PE = mgh
To lift an object of mass m slowly to height h, apply a force equal to mg (balancing gravity). Work done W = force × displacement = mg × h = mgh. By the Work-Energy Theorem, this work becomes stored potential energy.
Gravitational Potential Energy
U = mgh
m = mass (kg) · g = 9.8 m s⁻² · h = height above reference level (m) · U = PE (J). Valid only near Earth's surface where g is approximately constant.
Reference Level: Potential energy is defined relative to a reference point (usually the ground, where U = 0). Only changes in PE matter physically — the absolute value depends on where you set your reference.
Example 7.7🏏Cricket Ball Thrown 10 m High — PE at Maximum Height
A fielder throws a 200 g cricket ball 10 m high in celebration. What is its PE at the maximum height? (g = 10 m s⁻²)
Solution
U = mgh = 0.2 kg × 10 m s⁻² × 10 m = 20 J

Section 7.4.3 · Conservation Law

Conservation of Mechanical Energy

As an object falls freely, PE converts to KE — but the total mechanical energy (PE + KE) stays constant

The Law of Conservation of Mechanical Energy
As an object moves under gravitational force only (no friction, no air resistance), its mechanical energy E = KE + PE remains constant throughout the motion.
🔝
Point A (top)
KE = 0 · PE = mgh · E = mgh
→
⬇️
Point B (mid)
KE = ½mg²t² · PE = mgh′ · E = mgh
→
🔽
Point C (bottom)
KE = mgh · PE = 0 · E = mgh
🕰️ Activity 7.2 Simple Pendulum — Verifying Conservation of Mechanical Energy
Set up a simple pendulum. Draw a horizontal line at the height of the bob at rest. Pull the bob to point P (at the level of the line, height h) and release. Observe the bob's motion.
Point P (start, height h): PE = mgh, KE = 0. Total mechanical energy = mgh.
Point Q (bottom): PE = 0 (reference level), KE = mgh. All PE has converted to KE. Total = mgh.
Point R (other side): KE = 0 again, PE = mgh again. Bob reaches almost the same height it started from.
Why "almost" — not exactly? In real life, friction at the support and air resistance cause small energy losses to thermal energy. The pendulum gradually slows down and eventually stops due to these dissipative forces.
Example 7.8🛝Speed at Bottom of Slide — The "Think It Over" Question Answered!
What is the speed of a child at the bottom of a frictionless slide of height h?
Solution
At top: PE = mgh, KE = 0. At bottom: PE = 0, KE = ½mv².
By conservation of mechanical energy: mgh = ½mv² → v² = 2gh → v = √(2gh)

The velocity depends only on height h — NOT on the shape of the slide OR the mass of the child. Two children of different masses on the same slide reach the bottom with the same speed! The taller slide gives a larger v.
Example 7.9🚛Escape Ramp — Minimum Length to Stop a Runaway Truck
A 10000 kg truck at 72 km h⁻¹ with failed brakes enters a 30° sand ramp (rises 1 m per 2 m along ramp). Sand force = 50000 N opposing motion. Find minimum ramp length d.
Solution
Initial KE = ½ × 10000 × 20² = 2000000 J. Initial PE = 0.
Height gained = d/2. Final KE = 0. Final PE = 10000 × 10 × d/2 = 50000d J.
Work by sand = −50000d J.
Work-Energy Theorem: −50000d = (50000d − 2000000)
−50000d − 50000d = −2000000 → d = 2000000/100000 = 20 m
🎢
Threads of Curiosity
Why Conservation of Energy Is More Powerful Than Newton's Laws
Solving problems directly through Newton's laws requires knowing all forces at every instant — which can be extremely complex (e.g., normal force on a curved slide changes with position). Conservation of mechanical energy sidesteps all intermediate steps: just compare total energy at the start and end. This is why Example 7.8's answer (v = √(2gh)) is independent of slide shape — we never needed to know the forces at each point on the curve.

Section 7.5 · Rate of Work

Power

Running up stairs vs walking slowly — the same work, but very different rates. That rate is power.

Power — The Rate of Doing Work
Power Formula
P = W / t
P = average power (W) · W = work done (J) · t = time taken (s). More work in same time = more power. Same work in less time = more power.
SI Unit
1 W = 1 J s⁻¹
1 watt = 1 joule per second. Also: 1 horsepower = 746 W. Named after James Watt who invented the efficient steam engine.
Example 7.10🏋️Weightlifter — Power Required to Lift 75 kg by 2 m in 5 s
A weightlifter lifts 75 kg by 2 m in 5 seconds. How much power does she need? (g = 10 m s⁻²)
Solution
Work done = mgh = 75 × 10 × 2 = 1500 J
Power = W/t = 1500 J / 5 s = 300 W
Example 7.11🚗Car Engine Power — 1000 kg Car Reaches 72 km h⁻¹ in 10 s
A 1000 kg car starts from rest and reaches 72 km h⁻¹ in 10 s. Find engine power.
Solution
Final velocity v = 72 km h⁻¹ = 20 m s⁻¹; initial u = 0
Work done = ΔKE = ½mv² − 0 = ½ × 1000 × 400 = 200000 J
Power = W/t = 200000/10 = 20000 W = 20 kW
Meet a Scientist
James Watt
Scottish Engineer · Inventor of the Efficient Steam Engine
The SI unit of power — the watt (W) — is named in honour of James Watt. He didn't invent the steam engine, but he invented a far more efficient version that could generate rotational motion to drive machinery and wheels — transforming the Industrial Revolution. To market his engines to horse-powered industries, Watt defined horsepower (1 hp = 746 W) by measuring how much work a horse could do per minute. This unit is still used today for car engines and water pumps.
🐎
Threads of Curiosity — Horsepower
Why Do We Still Measure Engines in "Horsepower"?
When engines were first invented, industries used horses to drive their machinery. James Watt needed a way to compare his engines to horses so buyers could understand the power output. He measured that a horse can do work at about 746 W — and called this 1 horsepower. Even though we now have SI units (watts), horsepower persists in car specifications and pump ratings because of historical tradition and consumer familiarity.
Section 7.6 · Applied Physics

Simple Machines

Devices that make work easier — they change the magnitude or direction of force but never reduce total work done

Key Definitions: Effort = the force we apply to a machine. Load = the force that needs to be overcome. Mechanical Advantage = Load ÷ Effort = how much the machine multiplies your applied force. Machines do NOT create energy — they redistribute it between force and distance.

Mechanical Advantage
MA = Load / Effort
MA > 1: machine multiplies force (you apply less effort). MA = 1: direction change only. MA < 1: force reduced, distance multiplied (rare but useful for speed).
7.6.1 — Simple Machine 1
⚙️ Pulley
A pulley is a wheel with a groove that guides a rope. It changes the direction and/or magnitude of applied force.
Fixed Pulley
Changes direction only. Pull downward to lift load upward — more convenient than pushing up. Effort = Load. MA = 1. Used in flagpoles, wells, elevators.
Movable Pulley / Pulley System
Load attached to movable pulley; one end of rope fixed. Effort is less than load. MA > 1. Used in cranes, construction hoists, block-and-tackle systems.
7.6.2 — Simple Machine 2
📐 Inclined Plane
A ramp or sloped surface that allows heavy loads to be raised with less force over a longer distance.
MA of Inclined Plane
MA = L / h
L = length of inclined plane · h = height gained. As L increases (shallower angle), MA increases and effort decreases — but you push over a longer distance.
📏 Activity 7.3Inclined Plane Experiment — Spring Balance & Cart
Pull a toy cart up a plank with a spring balance at two different angles. Compare readings.
Spring balance reads less when pulling up the ramp than when lifting vertically — force required is smaller on the inclined plane.
Making the plank less steep (increasing L, decreasing angle): spring balance reading decreases further but distance moved is greater.
Key insight: Total work done (F × distance) remains constant — if force decreases, distance increases proportionally. Machines don't save work.
Example 7.12📐Inclined Ramp Over a 30 cm Step (width = 40 cm)
A ramp rises 30 cm (height h = 30 cm) over a width of 40 cm. What is its MA?
Solution
By Pythagoras: L = √(40² + 30²) = √(1600 + 900) = √2500 = 50 cm
MA = L/h = 50/30 = 1.67 — you need only 1/1.67 ≈ 60% of the load's weight as effort.
7.6.3 — Simple Machine 3
🔧 Lever
A rigid bar that rotates about a fixed point (fulcrum). A small force at one end can overcome a large load at the other end.
Three Parts
Fulcrum: Fixed pivot point · Load arm: Distance from fulcrum to load · Effort arm: Distance from fulcrum to effort point
Lever Principle
F₁ × d₁ = F₂ × d₂ (effort × effort arm = load × load arm). MA = effort arm / load arm. Larger effort arm → larger MA.
⚖️ Activity 7.4 & 7.5Scale + Pencil + Erasers & Beam Balance Investigation
Activity 7.4: Place a stapler near the pencil fulcrum and erasers far away — lighter erasers lift the heavier stapler! Activity 7.5: Beam balance with coin pans confirms: n₁ × L₁ = n₂ × L₂ (effort × effort arm = load × load arm).
When effort arm L₁ is longer than load arm L₂, effort F₁ needed is less than load F₂.
MA = effort arm / load arm = L₁/L₂. Increasing effort arm increases MA.
A lever reduces the force needed but the effort moves a larger distance — total work done is unchanged.
Lever Classification

Three Classes of Levers

Based on the relative positions of fulcrum, load and effort

Class I
⚖️
Fulcrum in Between
Load — Fulcrum — Effort arrangement. The fulcrum sits between load and effort. Can have MA greater or less than 1 depending on arm lengths.
Scissors, tongs, crowbar, pliers, balance scale, seesaw
Class II
🛒
Load in Between
Fulcrum — Load — Effort arrangement. Effort arm always longer than load arm → MA always > 1. Requires less effort than load.
Lemon squeezer, wheelbarrow, bottle opener, nutcracker
Class III
🧹
Effort in Between
Fulcrum — Effort — Load arrangement. Effort arm always shorter → MA always < 1. You apply MORE force than load but gain speed or range.
Tweezers, broom, fishing rod, hammer (when scooping), oar
Example 7.13🪢Seesaw Balance — Children of Different Masses
A seesaw: AC = EC = 2 m, BC = DC = 1 m (fulcrum at C). Where should a 30 kg child sit if a 15 kg child sits at A (2 m from C)?
Solution
Using lever principle: effort × effort arm = load × load arm
15 kg × 2 m = 30 kg × L → L = 30/30 = 1 m. The 30 kg child should sit at seat D (1 m from C).
Conservation in All Machines: Machines do NOT create energy. The work input (effort × effort displacement) = work output (load × load displacement), ignoring friction. If effort decreases, distance must increase — work remains constant.
