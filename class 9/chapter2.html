Section 1 · Origin & Overview

Cell: The Basic Unit of Life

Where life began and what it is built from

Life is widely believed to have originated in water. Some researchers suggest it began in small warm pools with changing conditions — not the open ocean. India's Puga Valley hot springs in Ladakh (near boiling point, even in cold climate) closely resemble conditions on early Earth ~3.5 billion years ago. Scientists from the Birbal Sahni Institute of Palaeosciences, Lucknow, found that calcium carbonate deposits there may have protected early organic molecules and helped form the first cell membrane.

🦠
Unicellular Organisms
Entire organism = one single cell. That one cell must perform all life functions — nutrition, respiration, excretion, reproduction.

Examples: Bacteria, Yeast, Amoeba
🌿
Multicellular Organisms
Built from millions of specialised cells that work together. Cells → Tissues → Organs → Organ Systems.

Examples: Plants, Fish, Birds, Humans
🏗️
Organisation of Life
Similar cells → Tissue → Different tissues → Organ → Several organs → Organ System.

Example: Nasal pores + trachea + lungs = Respiratory System

Even in multicellular organisms, the cell remains the fundamental unit of structure and function. No matter how complex the organism, all life processes ultimately happen at the cellular level.

Section 2 · How to Study Cells

Microscopy: Seeing the Invisible

Technological tools that unlocked the world of cells

👁️
Limit of Resolution — Human Eye
At a distance of 25 cm (near point), the human eye can distinguish two points separated by at least 0.1 mm. Anything closer appears as one point. This is the limit of resolution of the unaided eye.
🔬
Light Microscope
Uses a convex lens system (objective + eyepiece) to magnify objects. Can resolve structures down to ~1 µm. Used in school labs with 10×, 40× objective lenses. Total magnification = eyepiece × objective.
⚡
Electron Microscope
Uses a beam of electrons instead of light. Resolves structures at the nanometre scale — reveals fine details of organelles, cell membranes, and even individual proteins. One nanometre = 0.000001 mm.
🏛️
Historical Milestone · 1665
Robert Hooke — First Person to Observe a Cell
Using a self-designed microscope capable of 200–300× magnification, Hooke examined a thin slice of cork. He observed small box-like compartments and named them 'cells' — from the Latin word for small rooms. This was the first time a cell was ever described by a human.

🧅 Activity 2.1 Estimate the Size of a Cell
Use a transparent ruler and an onion peel slide to estimate the real size of a cell.
Step 1: Place ruler on microscope stage; measure diameter of circular field of view in mm
Step 2: Convert mm → µm (1 mm = 1000 µm). If diameter = 5 mm → 5000 µm
Step 3: Place onion peel slide. Count cells along the diameter (e.g., 25 cells)
Formula: Cell size = Field diameter (µm) ÷ Number of cells = 5000 ÷ 25 = 200 µm per cell
Magnification check: If both eyepiece and objective are 10×, total = 100×. A 200 µm cell appears 100 times larger under the microscope.

Section 3 · Cell Structure

Cell Membrane — The Universal Feature

The gatekeeper that defines every cell

🛡️
What Is the Cell Membrane?
A thin boundary (7–10 nm thick) that surrounds every cell. Also called the plasma membrane. It defines the cell's individuality and separates cell contents from the external environment.
🔑
Selectively Permeable
The membrane does not let everything through. It allows some substances (like water, oxygen, CO₂) to pass freely while blocking others (like large molecules, ions). This selective control is critical for cell function.
🧱
Fluid-Mosaic Model
The membrane structure is explained by the fluid-mosaic model: a lipid bilayer with embedded proteins. "Fluid" because molecules move; "mosaic" because proteins are embedded like tiles.
Fluid-Mosaic Model — Key Features
🫙
Lipid Bilayer
2 layers of fat molecules. Heads face out (water-attracting), tails face inward (water-repelling)
→
🔵
Embedded Proteins
Act as gatekeepers — channels and carriers for substances to pass through
→
💧
Fluid Nature
Molecules can move sideways, flip, and rotate within the bilayer
→
🧩
Mosaic Pattern
Proteins arranged like tiles give the "mosaic" appearance

The cell membrane is only 7–10 nm thick — that is about 7,000 times thinner than a single human hair. Yet it controls everything that enters and exits the cell. It is made of lipids (fats) and proteins.

Section 4 · Transport Across Membranes

Osmosis and Diffusion

How water and particles move — with and without membranes

🌊
Diffusion
The net movement of particles from a region of higher concentration to a region of lower concentration. Occurs without a membrane. Driven by concentration gradient. Example: fragrance spreading in air.
💧
Osmosis
The diffusion of water across a selectively permeable membrane, from a region of high water concentration (dilute solution) to low water concentration (concentrated solution). Requires a membrane.
🌱
Osmosis in Plants
Water from the soil enters root cells by osmosis — soil water is more dilute than cell sap. This is how plants absorb water. When a plant wilts, its vacuoles have lost water by osmosis.
Effect of Different Solutions on a Cell
Isotonic
⚖️
No Net Water Movement
Solute concentration outside = solute concentration inside. Cell remains the same size. No net osmosis occurs.
Hypotonic
💧
Cell Swells (Turgid)
Solute concentration outside < concentration inside. Water enters the cell by osmosis. Cell swells. In plants → turgidity. In animals → may burst (lysis).
Hypertonic
🥀
Cell Shrinks (Plasmolysis)
Solute concentration outside > concentration inside. Water leaves the cell. Cell shrinks. In plants → plasmolysis (membrane pulls away from wall). In animals → crenation.
🥔 Activity 2.2 Potato Osmosis Experiment
Cut a potato into two equal pieces. Measure initial weight. Place one in plain water (Beaker A), one in 20% salt solution (Beaker B). Leave for 1 hour. Measure final weight.
Beaker A (plain water): Potato swells → gained weight. Cell is in hypotonic solution; water enters cells by osmosis.
Beaker B (salt solution): Potato shrinks → lost weight. Cell is in hypertonic solution; water leaves cells by osmosis.
Why equal-sized pieces? To make it a fair test — the control variable must be the same so results are comparable.

What If…
Mung bean seeds soaked for 12 hours, then placed in concentrated solution
The seeds absorbed water during soaking (hypotonic conditions — turgid). Placing them in concentrated solution creates hypertonic conditions. Water would leave the seeds by osmosis — they would shrink and feel rubbery. This is the same principle behind food preservation using salt and sugar (as done by farmer Deepa with amla and lemons). High solute concentration outside bacteria and fungi draws water out of them by osmosis, preventing their growth.
Section 5 · Outer Covering

Cell Wall — The Outer Shield

Present in plants, fungi, and bacteria — absent in animal cells

🧱
What Is the Cell Wall?
An additional rigid layer outside the cell membrane. Present in plant, fungal, and bacterial cells. Absent in animal cells. Provides structural support and protection. Although rigid, it is permeable — water and minerals pass through.
🌾
Composition
Plant cell wall is primarily made of cellulose — a carbohydrate built from many glucose units linked together. Cellulose in our diet is dietary fibre/roughage that helps digestion. Fungi and bacteria have different cell wall materials.
🌿
Why Plants Need It
Plants can't move to escape wind or rain. The cell wall maintains shape, keeps leaves and flowers firm, and helps the plant stand upright. It prevents the cell from bursting in hypotonic conditions — the wall withstands pressure.
Activity 2.3 🔬 Plant vs Animal Cell in Sugar Solution
Prepare slides of Rhoeo (Cradle lily) leaf peel cells and human cheek cells. Add 20% sugar solution. Observe under microscope after 30 minutes. What do you find?
🌿 Plant Cell (Rhoeo)
Outer boundary (cell wall) stays the same. Inner content shrinks as membrane pulls away from wall. This is plasmolysis. Cell wall maintains shape.
🫀 Animal Cell (Cheek)
No cell wall to provide support. The whole cell shrinks considerably. Without a rigid wall, animal cells can change shape — which is actually useful for movement and flexibility.

Onion peel cells are box-shaped and regularly arranged — because the rigid cell wall forces a fixed shape. Cheek cells are irregularly arranged and variable in shape — animal cells lack the rigidity-giving cell wall, so they can deform freely. This structural difference reflects the different needs of plant and animal tissues.

Section 6 · Cell Interior

Prokaryotic vs Eukaryotic Cells

Two fundamental types of cells — the great divide of life

Prokaryotic
🦠
Prokaryotic Cell
Pro = primitive, karyon = nucleus. No well-defined nucleus. DNA is in the cytoplasm in a region called the nucleoid. No membrane-bound organelles. Most cellular activities in cytoplasm.
Examples: Bacteria (including thermophiles in Puga Valley)
Eukaryotic
🧬
Eukaryotic Cell
Eu = true, karyon = nucleus. Has a well-defined, membrane-bound nucleus. Has membrane-bound organelles. Much larger and more complex. Different processes compartmentalised.
Examples: Plant cells, Animal cells, Fungi
Three Basics
🏠
Every Cell Has
All cells, regardless of type, share three fundamental components: a cell membrane (plasma membrane), cytoplasm (semi-fluid jelly-like substance), and genetic material (in nucleus or nucleoid).
Universal truth: no cell exists without these three

Ready to Go Beyond
🦠 Viruses, Viroids, and Prions — Acellular Agents
These infectious agents are too small for light microscopes. Viruses have genetic material + protein coat. Viroids have genetic material but no protein coat. Prions are misfolded proteins with no genetic material at all. They are acellular (no cells) — and thus challenge our definition of life itself. They infect, replicate, and cause disease without following normal cellular rules.

Section 7 · Cell Organelles

The Cell as a Living Factory

Each organelle is a specialised department with a specific job

A cell is like a tiny living factory where each organelle is a department. The nucleus is the control room. Mitochondria are the power plants. Ribosomes are the manufacturing lines. The Golgi apparatus is the post office. Lysosomes are the recycling centre. All departments work simultaneously and in coordination.

Organelle 1 🧬 Nucleus — House of Coded Instructions
The nucleus has a double-layered nuclear membrane with pores that allow material exchange with the cytoplasm. Inside: the nucleolus (where ribosomal subunits are made) and chromosomes.
Chromosomes contain DNA + proteins. DNA carries genetic information. Visible as rod-shapes only when cell is dividing.
Genes are functional segments of DNA. In a non-dividing cell, DNA exists as loose chromatin — tangled threads. Before division, chromatin condenses into distinct chromosomes.
Prokaryotes: DNA is a single circular molecule in the nucleoid region — no membrane around it.
🏭
Ribosomes
Protein Factories
Tiny structures present freely in cytoplasm or attached to Endoplasmic Reticulum. Site of protein synthesis. Present in ALL cells — prokaryotic and eukaryotic. Assembled from subunits made in the nucleolus.
Found in every living cell — the most ancient and universal organelle
🕸️
Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER)
Manufacturing & Transport Network
Large network spreading through cytoplasm, continuous with the nuclear membrane. Two types: RER (has ribosomes → makes & secretes proteins; e.g., pancreatic cells) and SER (no ribosomes → makes fats, hormones, and stores them).
RER looks rough; SER looks smooth — under electron microscope
📦
Golgi Apparatus
Packaging & Shipping Centre
Stacks of flattened sac-like structures. The cell's post office: modifies, sorts, and packages proteins and lipids into vesicles for transport, secretion, or lysosome formation. Functionally linked to ER and cell membrane.
First observed by Camillo Golgi in 1898 in barn owl nerve cells
♻️
Lysosomes
Clean-up System
Single membrane-bound sacs filled with digestive enzymes. Break down waste proteins, carbs, fats, and damaged organelles. Products are released back to cytoplasm for reuse. Keep the cell clean and healthy.
Sperm cells use lysosomal enzymes to penetrate egg during fertilisation
⚡
Mitochondria
Powerhouse of the Cell
Double-membrane organelle. Outer membrane is smooth; inner membrane is folded into cristae (increases surface area for reactions). Site of cellular respiration — glucose is broken down to release energy stored as ATP.
Has own DNA and ribosomes — evolved from ancient bacteria (endosymbiosis)
☀️
Chloroplasts
Food Factories (Plants Only)
Double-membrane plastid containing chlorophyll (green pigment). Absorbs sunlight for photosynthesis. Inside: stroma (semi-fluid) with disc-shaped membranes that hold chlorophyll. Sugars synthesised here.
Also has own DNA and ribosomes — shares evolutionary history with cyanobacteria
🎨
Chromoplasts & Leucoplasts
Colour & Storage Plastids (Plants)
Chromoplasts (chroma = colour): contain yellow, orange, or red pigments — source of fruit and flower colours. Attract pollinators and seed-dispersers. Leucoplasts (leukos = white): colourless, store starch, oils, or proteins (e.g., potato, taro/Colocasia).
White flowers may have chromoplasts with non-visible pigments or reflect all wavelengths
🫙
Vacuoles
Storage & Support
In mature plant cells: one large central vacuole filled with cell sap (water, minerals, sugars, waste). Maintains cell pressure → keeps plant firm (turgor). When dehydrated → plant wilts. In animal cells: smaller, temporary vacuoles for storage.
A plant vacuole can occupy up to 90% of the cell's volume

Threads of Curiosity
Red Blood Cells Have No Nucleus — and That's the Point
Mature human Red Blood Cells (RBCs) are enucleate — they lose their nucleus during development. This is a deliberate adaptation: removing the nucleus frees space for more haemoglobin, allowing each RBC to carry more oxygen. The trade-off: without a nucleus, RBCs cannot repair or divide. Their lifespan is only about 120 days, after which they must be replaced. Mature platelets are also enucleate. Can you think of other cells without nuclei?
🔬
Italian Scientist 1843 – 1926
Meet a Scientist
Camillo Golgi
1843 – 1926 · Italian · Nobel Prize 1906
In 1898, Golgi observed a thread-like network in the nerve cells of a barn owl using special staining techniques. He called it the "Golgi apparatus." Early microscopes couldn't resolve it clearly — many scientists doubted its existence. Only decades later, with the electron microscope, was its structure confirmed beyond doubt.
Key Takeaway
Scientific discoveries sometimes precede the technology to verify them. Golgi trusted his observations even when tools were limited. The apparatus was later confirmed and named in his honour.
Section 8 · Growth & Division

How Normal Cells Grow and Divide

The process that allows growth, repair, and reproduction

When you get a cut on your skin, it heals. When hair falls out, it grows back. This is because cells grow and divide to replace damaged or dead cells. Growth in your body is not because cells get bigger — it's because cells divide to form new cells. Every day, ~100 billion cells in the human body are replaced (about 1% of total cells).

Bridging Science and Society
🧫 Cell Culture — Growing Cells Outside the Body
Scientists can grow plant and animal cells outside the body in special nutrient-rich media. This is called cell culture. The right temperature, pH, and moisture under sterile conditions must be maintained. Cell culture is used to: study how cells work; produce biochemicals, food ingredients, medicines, and vaccines; test drugs without using whole organisms; and grow tissues for transplant research. It is a cornerstone of modern biotechnology.
Section 9 · Unifying Principle

Cell Theory — The Foundation of Biology

Three simple statements that unify all of life

1838
Matthias Schleiden (German Botanist)
Proposed that all plants are made up of cells. First formal statement linking cells to all plant life.
1839
Theodor Schwann (German Zoologist)
Proposed that all animals are also made up of cells. Extended Schleiden's plant finding to all animal life.
1855
Rudolf Virchow (German Scientist)
Added the third tenet: "All cells arise from pre-existing cells" (Omnis cellula e cellula). Explained life's continuity.
Tenet 1
🌍
All Life = Cells
All living organisms are made up of one or more cells. From the tiniest bacterium to the tallest tree — cells are universal.
Unifies biology from bacteria to humans
Tenet 2
⚙️
Cell = Basic Unit
The cell is the basic unit of structure and function in all living beings. All biological processes ultimately occur at the cellular level.
Even in complex organisms — the cell is fundamental
Tenet 3
♻️
Cell from Cell
All cells arise from pre-existing cells. No new cell spontaneously generates — cells can only come from other cells by division.
Explains life's continuity through cell division
🛑
Contact Inhibition
In many animal cells, cell division stops when cells touch neighbouring cells. This natural brake prevents overgrowth and maintains proper tissue structure. Plant cells do not show contact inhibition due to rigid walls.
⚠️
Cancer Cells
Cancer cells lose contact inhibition and divide uncontrollably. This leads to tumour formation. Tumours may be benign (localised) or malignant (invasive — spreading to other tissues/organs).
💀
Programmed Cell Death (PCD)
Cells have a genetically programmed lifespan. PCD (apoptosis) is an organised, deliberate process of cell destruction — essential for normal development. Example: during embryo development, cells between fingers are deliberately destroyed to form distinct digits.
🧬
Threads of Curiosity
How Do Cancer Cells Grow and Spread?
Normal cells grow, age, and die in a controlled manner. Sometimes, the control system breaks down — abnormal cells start dividing without stopping. This forms tumours (masses of abnormal cells). Benign tumours stay localised. Malignant tumours (cancer) invade neighbouring tissues and spread through blood to distant organs, forming new tumours (metastasis). Cancer treatment targets these out-of-control dividing cells.
Ready to Go Beyond
⚖️ Programmed Cell Death — How Cells Monitor Growth
Cells maintain balance through Programmed Cell Death (PCD) — a genetically regulated, organised self-destruction. It is essential for: normal development (forming fingers by eliminating cells between digits); removing damaged or infected cells; regulating immune responses. Without PCD, we would have webbed hands as embryos. This balance between cell division and cell death is fundamental to healthy life.
🤖
Threads of Curiosity · 2010
J. Craig Venter — The First Synthetic DNA Cell
In 2010, Venter's team sequenced the complete DNA of Mycoplasma mycoides, then chemically synthesised a copy. They transplanted this synthetic DNA into a related bacterium (whose own DNA was removed). The cell began growing and dividing following the instructions from the synthetic DNA. This proved that DNA controls cell structure and activity. However, the cell itself was not created from scratch — only the DNA was synthetic; the cytoplasm and membrane came from an existing living cell. The ethical questions this raises are still debated: What is the future of synthetic cells? What are the related ethical issues?
Meet Scientists

Scientists Who Shaped Cell Biology

🌿
Indian Botanist 1924 – 2013
Meet a Scientist
Arun Kumar Sharma
1924 – 2013 · Indian Botanist · Padma Bhushan
A famous Indian scientist known for his work on chromosomes. As a botanist, he contributed to plant taxonomy, evolution, and development. He invented many laboratory methods to study chromosomes in plants — methods still used today in cytogenetics research.
Received the Shanti Swarup Bhatnagar Award and Padma Bhushan for his outstanding contributions to botany.
Key Takeaway
Sharma's work bridged classical botany and modern genetics. His chromosome studies in plants helped understand plant evolution and diversity. Indian scientists have made foundational contributions to cell biology.
🌱
Austrian Botanist 1854 – 1945
Meet a Scientist
Gottlieb Haberlandt
1854 – 1945 · Austrian Botanist
In 1902, Haberlandt proposed a radical idea: any living plant cell — even a fully mature one from permanent tissue — can develop into a complete plant if given the right nutrients and conditions. He called this special ability totipotency.
This visionary idea, dismissed at the time, later became the foundation for Plant Tissue Culture Technology — used today to propagate plants, conserve endangered species, and produce disease-free crops at scale.
Key Takeaway
Totipotency: every plant cell carries the complete genetic blueprint to become an entire organism. This idea, ahead of its time by decades, now underpins an entire agricultural biotechnology industry.

🩸
Threads of Curiosity
Sperm Cells and the Role of Lysosomes in Fertilisation
Human sperm cells contain lysosomal enzymes in a specialised structure called the acrosome. When a sperm meets an egg, these enzymes are released and break down the outer protective layer (zona pellucida) of the egg, allowing the sperm to penetrate and fertilisation to occur. Without these lysosomes, reproduction would be impossible in humans and many animals. This is a perfect example of organelle specialisation for a specific function.
🌿
Threads of Curiosity
Why Do Mitochondria and Chloroplasts Have Their Own DNA?
Both mitochondria and chloroplasts have their own DNA and ribosomes — they can make some of their own proteins independently. This suggests they were once free-living bacteria that were engulfed by larger cells billions of years ago and became permanent residents. This is called the endosymbiotic theory. Mitochondria resemble alpha-proteobacteria; chloroplasts resemble cyanobacteria. Today they cannot survive outside the cell — but their bacterial ancestry is still written in their genome.
🔬
Threads of Curiosity · Puga Valley, Ladakh
Hot Springs and the Origin of Life — Research from Birbal Sahni Institute, Lucknow
Scientists from the Birbal Sahni Institute of Palaeosciences, Lucknow, studied the hot springs of Puga Valley, Ladakh. These springs maintain near-boiling temperatures even in freezing climate — similar to early Earth conditions ~3.5 billion years ago. They found that calcium carbonate forms rapidly around these springs. These deposits may have: (1) protected early organic molecules from UV radiation, and (2) helped form the first cell membrane. The thermophile bacteria living in these springs (unicellular, heat-loving) may be living models of early life forms.
Ready to Go Beyond
🔬 Electron Microscopy — Seeing at the Nanometre Scale
Electron microscopes use a beam of electrons instead of visible light to create images. Because electrons have a much shorter wavelength than light, they can resolve structures at the nanometre (nm) scale — a thousand times better than light microscopes. They revealed the fine structure of ribosomes, membrane proteins, the cristae of mitochondria, and the detailed architecture of viruses. The Scanning Electron Microscope (SEM) creates 3D surface images (like the stunning stomata on a Colocasia leaf). The Transmission Electron Microscope (TEM) reveals internal structure. These instruments transformed cell biology from a science of outlines into a science of molecular detail.
Ready to Go Beyond
🌱 What Is Totipotency? — Haberlandt's Vision Made Real
Haberlandt proposed in 1902 that every plant cell is totipotent — containing the full genetic instructions to develop into a complete organism. Modern Plant Tissue Culture proves this: a single cell from a carrot root, cultured in the right medium, can grow into an entire carrot plant. This has enabled: rapid propagation of orchids and banana plants; regeneration of virus-free crop varieties; conservation of endangered plant species; production of secondary metabolites (medicines); and the entire field of transgenic plant technology. All from one idea by one botanist in 1902.
Ready to Go Beyond · Synthetic Biology
🤖 J. Craig Venter's Synthetic Cell — Where Are We Headed?
Venter's 2010 experiment created the first cell controlled by synthetic DNA. It raised profound questions: What is the future of synthetic cells built entirely from non-living chemicals? If a truly synthetic cell is made, who owns it? Can it be patented? Could synthetic organisms escape labs and affect ecosystems? What are the military, medical, and ecological implications? The science is advancing faster than the ethical frameworks to govern it — making this one of the most important scientific-ethical frontiers of the 21st century. Scientists, ethicists, policymakers, and the public must all be part of this conversation.
Chapter Summary

At a Glance — Key Points

🔬
Cell = Basic Unit
The cell is the basic structural and functional unit of all living organisms. The limit of resolution of the human eye is 0.1 mm; cells need microscopes to be studied.
🛡️
Universal Membrane
All cells have a selectively permeable cell membrane (plasma membrane). Plants, fungi, and bacteria also have a cell wall outside. Animal cells lack a cell wall.
💧
Osmosis & Diffusion
Diffusion = movement of particles high → low concentration (no membrane needed). Osmosis = diffusion of water across a selectively permeable membrane.
🦠
Prokaryote vs Eukaryote
Prokaryotes: no nucleus (nucleoid), no membrane-bound organelles (1–10 µm). Eukaryotes: true nucleus, membrane-bound organelles (10–100 µm), unicellular or multicellular.
🏭
Organelles = Factory Departments
Nucleus (control), Ribosomes (protein synthesis), ER (transport), Golgi (packaging), Lysosomes (cleanup), Mitochondria (energy/ATP), Chloroplasts (photosynthesis), Vacuoles (storage).
✂️
Mitosis vs Meiosis
Mitosis → 2 identical daughter cells (same chromosomes) for growth/repair. Meiosis → 4 genetically varied cells (half chromosomes) for sexual reproduction.
📜
