Section 8.1 · History of the Atom

Rediscovering the Roots of Atomic Theory

Two civilisations, 2000+ years ago, asked the same question — what is everything made of?

Ancient India · ~600 BCE
🇮🇳
Acharya Kanada
In the Sanskrit text Vaisesika Sutras, Kanada proposed that repeatedly dividing matter (dravya) leads to the smallest indivisible particles he called parmanus. Infinitely small, imperceptible by senses. Combinations of parmanus (dyads, triads) form all material things — living and non-living.
⚠️ Did not specify proportions in which parmanus combine
Ancient Greece · ~460 BCE
🏛️
Leucippus & Democritus
Called indivisible particles atomos (Greek: a = not, tomos = cuttable). Like Kanada, proposed all matter is made of these particles separated by void. A remarkably parallel idea — yet both emerged without experimental evidence.
The word "atom" comes directly from atomos
Modern Era · 1808
⚗️
John Dalton
First scientific (not philosophical) atomic theory — based on experiments. All matter made of indivisible atoms; atoms of same element are identical; atoms combine in fixed ratios to form compounds. Starting point for modern atomic science.
Key gap: What are atoms made of?

Key Insight: The concept of 'atom' originated as an imaginary idea — a philosophical construct — rather than from experimental observation. It took 2,400 years to transform that idea into testable, evidenced science through Dalton's work. After Dalton: three burning questions — What are atoms made of? What do they look like? What makes one element's atoms different from another's?

Section 8.2 · Discovery of Electrons

The Electron is Discovered

1897 — J. J. Thomson's cathode ray tube experiment splits the "indivisible" atom

The Cathode Ray Tube Experiment
Thomson used a sealed glass tube with two metal electrodes. Air was pumped out. A high voltage was applied. Rays were seen travelling from the cathode (−) to the anode (+) — called cathode rays. Thomson studied these rays in electric and magnetic fields.

Key Conclusions from Cathode Rays
Rays are streams of negatively charged particles (electrons) with much smaller mass than atoms. Crucially: the nature of cathode rays was independent of the cathode material and the gas in the tube — proving electrons are fundamental to ALL atoms.
⚖️
Electron Properties
Charge = −1.602 × 10⁻¹⁹ C (represented as −1 by convention). Mass ≈ negligible compared to protons and neutrons. Since atoms are neutral, positive charge must exist somewhere to balance the negative electrons — Thomson's question.
Model 1 · 1904
🍉 Thomson's Plum Pudding Model
Thomson's puzzle: electrons are negative and atoms are neutral — so where is the positive charge? His answer: the atom is a solid sphere of uniformly distributed positive charge with electrons embedded throughout it — like plums in a pudding. Also analogous to a watermelon: red pulp = positive charge, seeds = electrons.
✅ What it explained
First genuine attempt to describe charge balance in a neutral atom. Electrons embedded in positive "pudding" — total charge sums to zero.
❌ What it couldn't explain
Results of Rutherford's gold foil experiment — particularly the large-angle deflections and backward bouncing of alpha particles, which should not happen if charge is spread uniformly.

Section 8.2.2 · The Pivotal Experiment

The Gold Foil Experiment

1911 — Geiger, Marsden and Rutherford fire alpha particles at gold atoms and make a stunning discovery

🔬 The Experiment α-Ray Scattering Experiment (Rutherford, Geiger & Marsden, 1911)
A narrow beam of positively charged alpha (α) particles was aimed at an extremely thin gold foil. α particles are tiny, positively charged particles emitted by certain radioactive elements — specifically a helium nucleus (2 protons + 2 neutrons). Detectors surrounded the gold foil to record where particles went after passing through.
Expected (Thomson's model prediction): Since positive charge is spread uniformly throughout the atom, α particles should pass straight through or be deflected only slightly. No large angles, no bouncing back.
Observed 1 — Most passed straight through: ✅ Confirmed — most of the atom is empty space.
Observed 2 — Some deflected at large angles: 😲 Unexpected! Something dense and positively charged must be deflecting them — this thing must be very concentrated.
Observed 3 — A few bounced straight back: 🤯 "As if you fired a cannonball at tissue paper and it bounced back!" — Rutherford. This is impossible if charge is spread out. A tiny, extremely dense, positively charged centre must exist.
🧬 Think as a Scientist
Predict: What would happen if the gold foil were made thicker?

Answer: A thicker foil means more layers of gold atoms — more nuclei along the path. The chance of an α particle encountering a nucleus increases. More α particles would be deflected (at various angles) and more would bounce back. The fraction passing straight through without deflection would decrease. The overall pattern of scattering would intensify.
Model 2 · 1911
☀️ Rutherford's Planetary (Nuclear) Model
From the gold foil experiment, Rutherford concluded:
What he proposed
• Most of the atom is empty space (most α passed through)
• A tiny, dense, positively charged nucleus at the centre holds all positive charge and most mass
• Electrons revolve around nucleus like planets around the Sun (Planetary Model)
Incredible size comparison
Atom diameter ≈ 10⁻¹⁰ m
Nucleus diameter ≈ 10⁻¹⁵ m

The nucleus is 10⁵ (one lakh) times smaller than the atom. If the atom = a cricket ground (100 m), the nucleus = a black pepper grain at the centre!
⚠️ Limitation — The Stability Problem
An electron moving in a circle is always accelerating (direction is constantly changing). Classical physics says an accelerating charged particle must radiate energy. Losing energy → electron spirals inward → crashes into nucleus. Atoms should collapse in nanoseconds. But they don't! Rutherford's model cannot explain why.
Ready to Go Beyond — Scale of the Atom
One Million Atoms in a Sheet of Paper
A sheet of paper 0.1 mm (10⁻⁴ m) thick, with each atom diameter ≈ 10⁻¹⁰ m:
Number of atoms stacked = 10⁻⁴ ÷ 10⁻¹⁰ = 10⁶ = one million atoms. What feels like a simple flat sheet is actually a vast assembly of a million atomic layers, all neatly arranged. The unseen atomic world shapes everything we see and use every day.

Section 8.2.3 · The Stability Solution

Bohr's Model of the Atom

1913 — Niels Bohr solves the stability problem with the revolutionary concept of fixed energy shells

Model 3 · 1913
🪐 Bohr's Model — Fixed Energy Shells
Bohr's Postulates
Electrons move in fixed circular paths called stationary states, orbits, or shells
Shells are named K, L, M, N, … or numbered n = 1, 2, 3, 4, … from the nucleus outward
Electrons only exist IN these shells, never between them. While in a shell, an electron does NOT lose energy
K-shell (n=1) is closest to nucleus and has the least energy. Energy increases as shells move outward
Electrons jump between shells by absorbing or releasing a fixed quantum of energy equal to the difference between shell energies
Each shell has a maximum capacity of electrons (2n²)
Shell Energy Levels
ENERGY INCREASES OUTWARD →
K (n=1): lowest energy · nearest nucleus
L (n=2): more energy than K
M (n=3): more energy than L
N (n=4): highest energy shown
✅ Stability Solved!
In a stationary state, the electron's energy is CONSTANT even though it moves. It doesn't lose energy → no spiral → no collapse. Atoms are stable!
🔤
Threads of Curiosity
Why K, L, M, N — Not A, B, C, D?
The naming came from early X-ray experiments by physicist Charles Barkla, who labelled the first observed X-ray spectral line "K". He deliberately skipped A–J to leave room for a possible series discovered earlier than K — though none ever was. When Bohr developed his model, he adopted Barkla's shell notation. Later, even Bohr's model was refined into the quantum mechanical model — where electrons exist as probability clouds (orbitals), not fixed circular paths. You'll study this in higher grades.
Section 8.3 · The Building Blocks

What Contributes to the Mass of an Atom?

The discovery of the neutron in 1932 explained why helium is 4× heavier than hydrogen, not 2×

The Puzzle: Hydrogen has 1 proton. Helium has 2 protons. But helium's mass is about 4× that of hydrogen — not 2×. If electrons and protons are the only particles and electrons are nearly massless, something else must be adding mass. What is it?

⚡
Electron (e⁻)
Discovered by J. J. Thomson (1897). Charge = −1. Located in shells orbiting the nucleus. Mass so tiny it's negligible in calculations. Number of electrons = number of protons (in a neutral atom).
➕
Proton (p⁺)
Discovered and named by Rutherford. Charge = +1 (equal and opposite to electron). Located in the nucleus. Much heavier than electron. Number of protons = atomic number Z = determines the element's identity.
⭕
Neutron (n⁰)
Discovered by James Chadwick (1932). Charge = 0 (neutral). Located in the nucleus. Mass ≈ mass of proton. Found in all nuclei except ¹H. Explains the "missing mass" — helium: 2 protons + 2 neutrons = 4× mass of hydrogen ✓

⚛️
Threads of Curiosity — Why Neutrons Are Essential
Why Don't Protons Repel Each Other Out of the Nucleus?
Every proton in the nucleus repels every other proton (like charges repel). So why don't nuclei blow apart? Neutrons help in two ways: (1) they increase spacing between protons, reducing repulsion, and (2) they strengthen the nuclear force — an extremely strong short-range force that binds nucleons together. Heavier elements need increasingly more neutrons: Carbon has 6p + 6n, Iron has 26p + 30n, Uranium has 92p + 146n — the ratio of neutrons to protons increases as elements get heavier.
India's Scientific Contributions
🔬 BARC — Bhabha Atomic Research Centre, Mumbai
The Bhabha Atomic Research Centre (BARC), Mumbai, leads advanced neutron-scattering experiments using reactors including Dhruva. These experiments reveal atomic-level insights into materials like superconductors, battery electrodes, and drug molecules — helping develop better medicines, energy storage, and industrial alloys right here in India. Founded by Homi Jehangir Bhabha, the father of India's nuclear programme, alongside TIFR. Research and explore more about Dhruva!
Ready to Go Beyond — The Atomic Age
Chadwick's Neutron Sparked a Revolution
Neutrons, being uncharged, can easily penetrate nuclei without being repelled. This immediately enabled scientists to probe nuclear interiors more deeply, leading to: creation of artificial radioactive elements, and the splitting of uranium atoms (fission) — giving birth to the "atomic age" with both nuclear power plants and nuclear weapons. Today, 118 elements are known; some are artificially created by bombarding nuclei with neutrons or other particles.
Section 8.4 · Chemical Symbols

Symbols of Elements

From Dalton's pictorial symbols (1803) to today's IUPAC alphabetic standards

📜
Dalton's System (1803)
John Dalton introduced the first pictorial symbols for elements — small circles with various inner markings. While creative, they were not universally adopted and were difficult to write or typeset.
🔤
Berzelius & IUPAC System (1813 onwards)
Jöns Jacob Berzelius proposed using letters derived from element names. Today IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) approves all names and symbols — universally recognised across all languages.
📋 IUPAC RulesHow to Write Element Symbols Correctly
Follow these rules when writing or checking chemical symbols:
Rule 1 — One or two letters: Many symbols are the first letter or first two letters of the element name (H = Hydrogen, Ca = Calcium).
Rule 2 — Case matters: First letter ALWAYS uppercase, second letter (if any) ALWAYS lowercase. Al NOT AL; Co NOT CO; Ne NOT NE.
Rule 3 — Non-sequential letters: Some symbols use the first + a non-second letter. Cl (chlorine, not Ch), Zn (zinc, not Zi).
Rule 4 — Latin / Greek / German origins: Fe = Iron (Latin: ferrum), Hg = Mercury (Greek: hydrargyros), W = Tungsten (German: Wolfram), Au = Gold (Latin: aurum), Na = Sodium (Latin: natrium), K = Potassium (Latin: kalium), Ag = Silver (Latin: argentum), Pb = Lead (Latin: plumbum), Cu = Copper (Latin: cuprum).

Sections 8.5 & 8.6 · Quantifying the Atom

Atomic Number and Mass Number

Two numbers that uniquely identify every atom in the universe

Atomic Number (Z)
Z = Number of Protons
Uniquely identifies the element. Since atoms are neutral: Z also = number of electrons. Different elements have different Z values. Examples: H → Z=1 · He → Z=2 · Li → Z=3 · Na → Z=11
Mass Number (A)
A = Protons + Neutrons
Protons + neutrons = nucleons. Electron mass negligible — not counted. Neutrons = A − Z. Examples: H → A=1 (1p, 0n) · He → A=4 (2p, 2n) · Li → A=7 (3p, 4n) · C → A=12 (6p, 6n)
Standard Atomic Notation
ᴬZX
X = element symbol · A = mass number (top left) · Z = atomic number (bottom left)
Example: ¹²₆C → Carbon, 6 protons, 6 neutrons, 6 electrons

Section 8.7 · Electron Arrangement

How Are Electrons Distributed in Energy Levels?

Bohr-Bury Rules for filling electrons into shells — the foundation of electronic configuration

Bohr-Bury Rules for Electronic Configuration
Rule 1 — Maximum electrons per shell = 2n²: K(n=1): 2×1²=2 · L(n=2): 2×2²=8 · M(n=3): 2×3²=18 · N(n=4): 2×4²=32
Rule 2 — Outermost shell maximum = 8: Even if a higher shell can hold more (e.g. M holds 18), the outermost shell can never hold more than 8 electrons at any time. (Exception: K shell maximum = 2.)
Rule 3 — Fill from inside out: Electrons fill shells starting with K, then L, then M, then N. The L shell will not start filling until K is full. The M shell will not start filling until L is full.

Section 8.8 · Combining Power

Combining Capacity of an Atom: Valency

How many hydrogen atoms can one atom combine with? That number is the valency.

🔗
Valence Shell & Valence Electrons
The outermost shell of an atom = valence shell. Electrons in it = valence electrons. These are the electrons involved in chemical bonding and reactions.
⬡
The Octet Rule
Atoms are most stable with 8 electrons in their outermost shell (octet), or 2 electrons for helium (one shell only). Atoms with complete octets (Noble gases) are largely unreactive. Incomplete → reactive → gain/lose/share to achieve stability.
⚡
Valency = Electrons to Complete Octet
Valency = electrons gained, lost, or shared to complete the octet. <4 valence electrons → tends to lose. >4 valence electrons → tends to gain. =4 → tends to share (covalent bonding).
⚗️ Valency ExamplesWorked Valency Calculations
Determine the valency of sodium, oxygen, and carbon from their electronic configurations:
Sodium (Na) — Z=11 — Config: 2,8,1: 1 valence electron. Loses 1 electron → 2,8 (stable neon config). Valency = 1
Oxygen (O) — Z=8 — Config: 2,6: 6 valence electrons. Gains 2 electrons → 2,8 (stable). Valency = 2. Confirmed: O combines with 2H → H₂O ✓
Carbon (C) — Z=6 — Config: 2,4: 4 valence electrons. Cannot easily gain (needs 4) or lose (needs to remove 4). Instead shares 4 electrons covalently. Valency = 4
Neon (Ne) — Z=10 — Config: 2,8: Already has complete octet. Neither gains nor loses electrons. Valency = 0 (unreactive noble gas)
Nitrogen (N) — Z=7 — Config: 2,5: 5 valence electrons. Gains 3 → 2,8. Valency = 3. Confirmed: N combines with 3H → NH₃ ✓
Magnesium (Mg) — Z=12 — Config: 2,8,2: Loses 2 valence electrons. Valency = 2. Confirmed: Mg combines with 2Cl → MgCl₂ ✓
Section 8.9 · Advanced Atomic Concepts

Isotopes and Isobars

Same atomic number but different neutrons — or same mass number but different elements?

Isotopes
Same Z, Different A
Atoms of the same element (same Z, same electrons) with different numbers of neutrons (different A). "Twin atoms" — same chemical behaviour, different physical properties (mass, melting point, boiling point).
Hydrogen isotopes:
¹₁H (protium) — 1p, 0n, 1e — 99.98%
²₁H (deuterium) — 1p, 1n, 1e — 0.015%
³₁H (tritium) — 1p, 2n, 1e — trace

Carbon isotopes:
¹²₆C — 6p, 6n (most abundant)
¹³₆C — 6p, 7n
¹⁴₆C — 6p, 8n (used in radiocarbon dating)
Isobars
Same A, Different Z
Atoms of different elements with the same mass number (same total nucleons) but different atomic numbers. They are NOT the same element — just happen to have the same total nucleon count.
Example — Mass number 40:
⁴⁰₁₈Ar (Argon) — Z=18, 22n
⁴⁰₁₉K (Potassium) — Z=19, 21n
⁴⁰₂₀Ca (Calcium) — Z=20, 20n

All have A=40 but are completely different elements with different chemical properties.

Why isotopes have same chemical but different physical properties: Chemical properties depend on electronic configuration (valence electrons). Isotopes have the same Z → same electrons → same electronic config → same chemical behaviour. But different neutron counts → different mass → different melting/boiling points, diffusion rates, etc.

Average (Weighted) Atomic Mass — Chlorine Example
Chlorine has two isotopes: ³⁵Cl (75%) and ³⁷Cl (25%). Since they don't occur in equal amounts, the simple average (36 u) doesn't represent nature accurately. We use the weighted average — each isotope weighted by its abundance.
Simple Average (inaccurate)
(35 + 37) ÷ 2 = 36 u
Assumes equal abundance — doesn't match nature
Weighted Average (accurate)
(35×75/100) + (37×25/100) = 35.5 u
Accounts for 75%:25% abundance ratio — reflects natural chlorine
Note: 35.5 u does not mean any single chlorine atom has that mass. It means in a large sample of chlorine atoms, the average mass per atom works out to 35.5 u (because 75% weigh 35 u and 25% weigh 37 u).

Meet the Scientists

The Minds Behind the Atom

Five extraordinary scientists who transformed our understanding of matter

1897 · Nobel Prize Physics 1906
J. J. Thomson
Discoverer of the Electron
Discovered the electron through cathode ray tube experiments — the first subatomic particle ever identified. As head of the Cavendish Laboratory, Cambridge, he guided many scientists including Rutherford. Received the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1906 for studies of electrical conductivity of gases. Proposed the plum pudding model — first attempt to describe atomic structure.
1908 Nobel Prize Chemistry · Born NZ
Ernest Rutherford
Father of Nuclear Physics
Born in New Zealand, moved to Cambridge to work with Thomson. Discovered the atomic nucleus through the gold foil experiment (1911). Explained radioactive decay. Won the 1908 Nobel Prize in Chemistry. Named and characterised the proton. His portrait now appears on New Zealand's $100 banknote.
1922 Nobel Prize Physics · Copenhagen
Niels Bohr
Atomic Energy Levels
Professor at Copenhagen University, Denmark. Proposed his model of fixed energy shells (1913) — solving Rutherford's stability problem. His postulate of stationary states explained why electrons don't spiral into the nucleus. Received the Nobel Prize in 1922 for his work on the structure of the atom.
1935 Nobel Prize Physics
James Chadwick
Discoverer of the Neutron
Worked under Rutherford at the Cavendish Laboratory. In 1932, he discovered the neutron — solving the mystery of "missing mass" in nuclei. This immediately enabled scientists to penetrate nuclei more easily (uncharged particles face no repulsion) and sparked discoveries in nuclear fission, artificial radioactive elements, and the atomic age.
Father of Indian Nuclear Programme
Homi Jehangir Bhabha
Indian Physicist & Visionary
Indian physicist who pioneered the development of atomic energy in India for peaceful purposes — electricity, agriculture, and medicine. Established key institutions: TIFR (Tata Institute of Fundamental Research) and the Bhabha Atomic Research Centre (BARC), both world-class research institutions that continue to advance science in India today.
