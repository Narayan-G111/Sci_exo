Opening Concept

What is Biodiversity?

Why can some plants survive long droughts while others cannot? Why do certain insects protect crops while others destroy them?

An Immense Variety of Living Forms

From microscopic organisms invisible to the naked eye to giant trees, from glowing jellyfish to soaring eagles, life exists in countless forms and habitats — from the snow-clad Himalayas to the coral reefs of the Andaman Sea. This immense variety of living organisms is known as biodiversity. Every organism plays a role in keeping nature stable: microscopic ocean algae release most of the oxygen we breathe, fungi and bacteria decompose fallen leaves into fertile soil, birds, bees and bats pollinate flowers, and plants capture sunlight to feed nearly all life on the planet.

🌾
Why Farmers Value Diversity
For centuries, farmers relied on practical knowledge to conserve crop varieties with useful traits — drought tolerance, pest resistance, and the ability to grow in poor soils. Diversity reduces the risk of crop failure and strengthens food security.
🔗
Shared Similarities, Many Differences
Within this vast diversity, organisms share certain similarities while showing many differences. Scientists group and classify them based on shared characteristics and evolutionary relationships.
🗺️
Why Classification Matters
Classification helps us understand how organisms are related, how they function, and how this knowledge applies to ecosystem management, biodiversity conservation and sustainable farming.

Section 12.1 · A Land of Many Landscapes

India as a Biodiversity Hotspot

Mountains, deserts, rainforests, plateaus and coastlines — each region shapes its own community of species

India's natural landscape is remarkably diverse — mountains in the north, desert in the west, rainforests in the North East, plateaus in the south, and long coastlines along the Arabian Sea and Bay of Bengal. Each region has distinct soil types and climatic conditions, together supporting a wide variety of species.

🐐
Endemic Species
Species restricted to a particular region of the world and found naturally nowhere else are called endemic species. The Nilgiri tahr, Lion-tailed macaque, the Indian pitcher plant (Nepenthes khasiana) and Neelakurinji are found only in India.
🔥
Biodiversity Hotspots
Regions that support a large number of endemic species and have undergone significant habitat loss are called biodiversity hotspots — making them especially important for conservation.
🗺️
India's Global Hotspots
The Western Ghats, Indo-Burma (including North East India), the Himalayas, and Sundaland (including the Nicobar Islands) are recognised as global biodiversity hotspots — rich in both the number and diversity of organisms.
Why protect hotspots? These regions support entire food webs and help keep ecosystems healthy. Protecting them is therefore just as important as protecting the individual endemic species that live within them.
Section 12.2 · A Story Written Over Time

How Has Biodiversity Evolved?

The biodiversity we see today was not always the same — it is the outcome of continuous change

Small differences among individuals affected their chances of survival and reproduction by helping them adapt to changing conditions. These differences accumulated over many generations and gave rise to new forms of life. The diversity we see today is therefore an outcome of continuous changes occurring over a vast span of time, shaped by interactions between organisms and their surroundings. The study of this biological diversity is made possible through the systematic framework of classification.

India's Scientific Contributions
Sangam Tinai & Sacred Groves
Ancient Indian traditions such as the Sangam Tinai classification of landscapes, and the protection of sacred groves, demonstrate a sophisticated literary and cultural understanding of landscapes and their biota. These customs effectively preserved locally diverse habitats — aligning with contemporary ecological principles even though they were never articulated as a formal ecological theory.

How to Classify Organisms?

The same organism can fit into different groups, depending on which feature you choose

Key insight: The very same organism can fit into different groups depending on which criterion you choose — which raises the question of how scientists decide which features matter most.
12.3.1 Some Criteria to Classify Living Organisms

Scientists often look at broad, easily visible features first, then move to more detailed ones. Similar features in different organisms suggest they have evolved from common ancestors.

👁️
External Features
Visible characteristics such as shape, size and body organisation.
🍃
Mode of Nutrition
Whether the organism is autotrophic (makes its own food) or heterotrophic (depends on others).
🦴
Internal Structures
Skeletal patterns, and the presence or absence of organs and tissue types.
🔬
Cell Structure
Unicellular or multicellular; eukaryote or prokaryote; presence or absence of a cell wall.
🌳
Ecological Role
Whether the organism functions as a producer, consumer, or decomposer in its ecosystem.
🧬
Reproduction & Genetic Similarity
Asexual and/or sexual reproductive methods, plus similarities in inherited features studied in detail using DNA.

Section 12.4 · Order from Chaos

The Need for Classification

A library with thousands of books scattered on the floor — and the Earth, with millions of species

📚 The Library Analogy

Imagine walking into a huge library where thousands of books are scattered all over the floor. You want to find On the Origin of Species by Charles Darwin — where would you begin? Without books arranged into subjects, authors, or sections, finding any one book is hopeless. The Earth, home to millions of organisms, faces exactly this problem — and classification is the solution that brings order to this vast biological "library."

🔬 Activity 12.2 Case Study — The Pakke Tiger Reserve
In Pakke Tiger Reserve, Arunachal Pradesh, scientists have recorded nearly 300 bird species — striking, given India as a whole has about 1,300 bird species. Pakke is also known for supporting four hornbill species: the Rufous-necked, Oriental Pied, Great and Wreathed Hornbill. These large birds nest only in large, old trees with suitable cavities, and feed on specific fruits, so different hornbill species end up distributed across different parts of the forest depending on tree size and fruit availability.

Many organisms show similarities in external features, internal structure (tissues and organs), and cellular organisation. To make sense of this vast diversity, scientists group organisms based on their similarities and/or differences — a scientific system called biological classification.

Protista, then Monera
Protista was added for unicellular microscopic organisms. As microscopes improved, scientists found bacteria lack a true nucleus (unlike Amoeba) — so Monera was separated out.

India's Scientific Contributions
The Rigveda & Brihat Samhita
Ancient Indian texts, the Rigveda and the Brihat Samhita, classify animals on the basis of their habitat (terrestrial, aquatic and aerial), behaviour patterns and ecological roles — an early form of systematic classification recorded centuries before modern taxonomy.

Section 12.6.1–12.6.2 · The Single-Celled World

Kingdom Monera & Kingdom Protista

Two single-celled kingdoms separated by one crucial difference: a true nucleus

🔬 Activity 12.4 Observing Bacteria & Cyanobacteria Slides
Observe permanent slides of bacteria and cyanobacteria under the microscope in your school laboratory and compare them with reference images.
🦠
Kingdom Monera
Single-celled prokaryotes found virtually everywhere — soil, water, air, hot springs, and even inside the human body. They live in the gut of ruminants and help produce biogas from animal dung. Some are harmful pathogens; many, like Lactobacillus and Rhizobium, are beneficial. Cyanobacteria are autotrophic decomposers, and some bacteria break down pollutants like oil and sewage.
🔬
Kingdom Protista
Single-celled eukaryotes, with or without a cellulose cell wall, that are microscopic and highly diverse. Some are autotrophic, others heterotrophic. Protists are an important link in aquatic food chains — some produce oxygen, others serve as food for small animals, and some act as decomposers in nutrient cycling.
Bridging Science and Society
Ram Bux Singh — Father of Modern Biogas
Ram Bux Singh was a pioneering Indian scientist who advanced biogas technology. In 1957, he established India's first scientifically designed biogas plant at Ramnagar, Sitapur, Uttar Pradesh — marking the start of systematic modern biogas research in the country. He later developed low-cost, efficient biogas plants for rural areas, served as an international consultant, and helped set up biogas plants in several other countries — championing renewable energy and sustainable waste management throughout his career.
🪨
Threads of Curiosity
Stromatolites — Fossils of the First Oxygen-Makers
Cyanobacteria (blue-green algae) were among the first organisms to produce oxygen through photosynthesis. About 2.5 billion years ago, oxygen accumulated in the atmosphere, making Earth suitable for other forms of life. Fossils of ancient cyanobacteria are preserved in structures called stromatolites, found in Rajasthan and Madhya Pradesh — among the earliest evidence of life on Earth.

Caution: The hay infusion may smell bad — wear a lab coat, mask and hand gloves, and discard the infusion only after autoclaving.

Section 12.6.3 · Nature's Decomposers

Kingdom Fungi

Multicellular, heterotrophic eukaryotes with cell walls made of chitin

Fungi do not make their own food. Instead, they absorb nutrients from dead or decaying matter through fine filaments that together form a network called mycelium. Most fungi are saprophytes, making them vital ecological decomposers — they break down complex organic matter (fallen leaves, twigs, dead organisms) into simpler substances available to the soil as minerals. Some fungi form mutualistic (symbiotic) relationships with other organisms, while others live as parasites and cause disease. Fungi reproduce both sexually and asexually — often by forming spores — and thrive in warm, moist conditions.

🦠
Yeast — A Unicellular Exception
Yeast is unusual: a unicellular fungus. It is classified under Fungi specifically because its cell wall is made of chitin — the defining structural feature of the kingdom.
💊
Aspergillus & Penicillium
These fungi are used to manufacture enzymes and antibiotics — Penicillium gave the world penicillin, one of medicine's most important discoveries.
🍄
Mushrooms — Macroscopic Fungi
Mushrooms are large, visible fungi that reproduce by spores — the most familiar face of this otherwise mostly microscopic kingdom.
Why fungi matter: Without fungi, the decay of dead plants and animals would be greatly reduced — adversely affecting soil fertility and the entire ecological balance.
Bridging Science and Society
Wild Mushrooms — Food, Medicine and Livelihood
Wild edible mushrooms are a valuable dietary food with high nutritional and medicinal value. They are important forest resources used and conserved by many communities, including tribal communities in India, who possess traditional knowledge of edible and poisonous mushrooms based on folk taxonomy. Some are used as food, others as medicine for various ailments. Today, mushroom cultivation is becoming a promising livelihood — easy and accessible thanks to its minimal space requirement, low investment, and fast life cycle of just 30–45 days.
Section 12.6.4 · The Plant Kingdom

Kingdom Plantae — From Water to Land

Multicellular, autotrophic eukaryotes with a cellulose cell wall — divided into five classes that trace a journey from water onto dry land

Plants perform photosynthesis and form the base of most food chains, releasing the oxygen essential for life on Earth. Their rigid cellulose cell wall provides support and protection. Kingdom Plantae is divided into five classes: Thallophyta, Bryophyta, Pteridophyta, Gymnosperm and Angiosperm.

🌊
Thallophyta — Primitive Plants
Thallos (undifferentiated body) + phyton (plant). The simplest plant forms, found mostly in water or moist environments. They form a thallus — a simple body allowing direct exchange of gases, nutrients and water. Example: Spirogyra.
🌿
Bryophyta — First Steps on Land
Bryon (moss) + phyton (plant). Mosses and liverworts mark an important shift from water to land. They have root-like rhizoids and simple stem/leaf-like structures, but still need water for reproduction — earning them the nickname "amphibians of the plant kingdom."
🌾
Pteridophyta — True Transport Tissue
Pteron (feather) + phyton (plant). Ferns possess true roots, stems and leaves, with vascular tissue — xylem and phloem — to transport water and food. They still require water for reproduction and produce no seeds.
🌲
Gymnosperm — Seeds Without Water
Gymnos (naked) + spermos (seed). Pines and cycads are adapted to cold, dry regions with needle-like leaves that reduce water loss. They produce seeds exposed on cones (not enclosed in fruit) and do not need water for fertilisation.

Bridging Science and Society
Lichens — Living Air-Quality Monitors
Those white-green patches on tree trunks, damp walls or stones are lichens — and villagers have long recognised them as a sign of pollution-free air. Lichens change colour with air pollutants, so researchers use them as natural bioindicators of air quality. Some types, called patthar ke phool, are used as a spice, as medicine, or to dye woollen and silk fabrics in maroon, violet or burgundy shades. Biologically, a lichen is a symbiotic partnership: an autotrophic alga (which photosynthesises and provides food) paired with a heterotrophic fungus (which provides protection). Some lichens are poisonous, so proper identification before use is essential.

Section 12.6.4 continued · The Most Diverse Plant Group

Angiosperms & Comparing the Five Plant Classes

Flowering plants close the loop on reproduction — and a side-by-side comparison reveals each group's trade-offs

🌸 Angiosperms — Efficient Reproduction & Seed Dispersal

Angeion (vessel) + spermos (seed). Flowering plants represent the most complex plant body organisation. They produce flowers that attract pollinators, increasing reproductive efficiency, and fruits that help spread seeds to new locations. These features let angiosperms occupy an enormous range of environments — making them the most diverse plant group on Earth.

🔬 Activity 12.8 Sorting Leaves into Monocots and Dicots
Collect different leaves from your surroundings and observe their shape and venation. Group them as monocots or dicots, and discuss how each leaf structure helps the plant adapt to its conditions.
Bridging Science and Society
Hortus Malabaricus — An Early Indian Botanical Text
One of the earliest scientific books on Indian plants, Hortus Malabaricus, was compiled in the 17th century by Hendrik van Rheede with the help of Itty Achudan — an Indian herbalist, botanist and physician — along with other local experts. It describes hundreds of plant species and their medicinal uses, showing how traditional knowledge and formal science can work powerfully together.

Section 12.6.5 · Increasing Complexity

Kingdom Animalia — The Invertebrates

From simple sponges to spiny-skinned echinoderms, body organisation grows steadily more complex

Animals are multicellular, heterotrophic organisms that depend on others for food. Most show locomotion, rapid response to stimuli, and coordinated behaviour, allowing them to actively search for food, avoid predators and interact with their surroundings. One major classification criterion is the notochord — a flexible rod-shaped structure. Animals without one are non-chordates (invertebrates); those with one (at least at some life stage) are chordates, further split into protochordata and vertebrata.

🧽
Porifera — Pore-Bearers
Sponges show one of the simplest animal body plans — multicellular, but without true tissues or organs. Numerous pores let water flow through continuously, bringing food and oxygen to individual cells and carrying waste away. They remain fixed in place in aquatic environments.
🪼
Cnidaria — True Tissues
Hydra, jellyfish and corals show tissue-level organisation for the first time — specialised cells form tentacles that actively capture prey, rather than relying on water currents like sponges. However, a single opening still serves both food intake and waste removal.
🪱
Platyhelminthes — Flatworms
Flatworms introduce bilateral symmetry — a body divisible into two halves along one plane, with distinct head-tail and front-back regions, enabling directional movement. Flattened bodies allow gas diffusion without special respiratory organs, though there is still just one opening for food and waste. Many flatworms are parasitic, using hooks and suckers to attach to host tissue.
🪤
Nematoda — Roundworms
Roundworms have elongated, cylindrical bodies that move efficiently through soil, water or host tissue. They are the first group with two separate openings (mouth and anus), and show organ-system level organisation, distinct between male and female worms.
🧽
Threads of Curiosity
Nature's Water Filter
Research studies show that just one kilogram of sponge can filter up to 24,000 litres of seawater per day — a staggering natural water-purification service performed by one of the simplest animals on Earth.
Bridging Science and Society
Parasitic Worms & Personal Hygiene
Some infectious diseases are caused by parasitic worms that enter the body through contaminated water and food, living inside the alimentary canal and taking nutrients from their host. To protect against them: wash your hands properly, maintain personal hygiene and good sanitary habits, eat properly cooked food, and drink only boiled or filtered water.
🪢
Annelida — Segmented Worms
Earthworms show a notable advance: cylindrical bodies divided into segments, with organ-system level organisation. Muscles power locomotion, a nerve cord enables control and coordination, and segmentation allows greater flexibility and precise movement. A true body cavity is present.
🦗
Arthropoda — Jointed Limbs
Arthro (limbs) + poda (appendages). Insects, crabs and spiders have segmented bodies with segments specialised for different functions, defined by a hard external skeleton (exoskeleton) — protecting the body, reducing water loss, and supporting powerful muscles for life in dry, exposed environments.
🐌
Mollusca — Soft Bodies, Hard Shells
Snails, squids and octopuses show organ-system organisation in soft bodies, often protected by a shell. The body has a distinct head, a muscular foot, and a hump — a basic plan modified in many directions to suit different environments.
⭐
Echinodermata — Spiny-Skinned
Echinos (spiny) + derma (skin). Starfish and sea urchins possess a hard internal skeleton made of calcium carbonate. Though they lack a notochord, this skeleton gives protection and controlled movement — a gradual shift toward the internal support systems seen in more complex animals.
Looking across invertebrates: From sponges to echinoderms, body structure shows a clear pattern of increasing complexity. New structural features improve feeding, movement and protection — but often introduce new challenges, explaining how survival strategies diversified across environments.
🔗 Protochordates — The First Notochord Appears

Protochordates, such as Amphioxus, possess a notochord at least once during their life — internal support that doesn't restrict movement. This represents a crucial transition in body organisation, helping us understand how chordates with a notochord may have arisen from simpler invertebrate forms.

Section 12.6.5 continued · 12.7 · Backbones & Body Forms

Vertebrates & Adaptations as Outcomes of Structural Change

A backbone changed everything — bigger bodies, faster movement, and five great groups of animals

🦴 The Vertebral Column

Vertebrates possess a vertebral column (backbone) — an internal skeletal structure supporting the body and protecting vital organs like the brain and spinal cord. This framework allows larger body size, efficient movement, complex organ systems, and advanced sensory and coordinated behaviour. Vertebrates are classified into five groups — fish, amphibians, reptiles, birds and mammals — based on habitat use, body covering and reproduction.

🐠
Fins & Gills
Allow fish to move and breathe in water — a structural solution to an aquatic life.
🦅
Feathers & Hollow Bones
Make flight possible in birds, combining light weight with structural strength.
🐫
Fat Storage & Thick Fur
Camels store fat; polar bears grow thick fur — both illustrate adaptation for survival under extreme conditions.
🍼
Mammary Glands
A defining mammalian feature that improves the survival of young ones through direct nourishment after birth.
The big picture: These structural and functional features are interconnected, reflecting how different vertebrates tackle the challenges of survival in different environments. Animal diversity overall reflects a wide range of body forms shaped by the environmental conditions organisms live under.
Bridging Science and Society
How Biodiversity Acts as a Disaster Barrier
Forests with rich biodiversity create physical, biological and chemical barriers against disasters. The large diversity of trees in mangrove forests reduced destruction during the 1999 super cyclone that hit Orissa — villages with more mangroves experienced less damage. In the Western Ghats, rich forest diversity acts as a biological barrier against Monkey Fever (Kyasanur Forest Disease), since many animal hosts in a diverse ecosystem cannot let the virus replicate inside their bodies. Diverse microorganisms in forest soils and plant roots also absorb and break down pollutants, while mangrove soils trap sediments and heavy metals — preventing pollution from spreading into oceans and rivers.

12.8 Scientific Naming — The Binomial System

A tiger is bagh in Hindi, puli in Tamil, "tiger" in English, and tigre in French — a recipe for confusion across regions. To avoid this, scientists use binomial nomenclature, a universal naming system introduced by Carolus Linnaeus in the 18th century. Every organism gets a scientific name with two parts, written in Latin or a Latinised form.

Common NameGenusSpecies
TigerPantheratigris
MangoMangiferaindica
1️⃣
Two Parts
Every scientific name has exactly two parts — genus, then species.
🔠
Capitalisation Rule
The genus name begins with a capital letter and comes first; the species name is all lower case and comes second.
✍️
Italics or Underline
The name is written in italics when printed, or underlined when handwritten.
Why genus matters: A genus groups closely related species sharing common features — Panthera tigris (tiger) and Panthera leo (lion) share the genus Panthera, the "roaring cats," with similar skull structure and the ability to roar. The species name itself refers to a group of organisms capable of interbreeding and producing offspring. Together, genus + species form one unique name recognised worldwide.

🧬 Carl Woese's Three Domain System (1977)

The five kingdom classification was more comprehensive than earlier systems, but it still couldn't fully explain the diversity of life. As scientists began comparing organisms at the DNA level, they found that organisms with similar DNA share a common ancestry. Based on this genetic data, Carl Woese (1977) proposed a three domain system — Bacteria, Archaea and Eukarya — revealing that microscopic life forms are far more diverse than previously believed.

Why Do Classification Systems Keep Changing?
Does this mean science is unreliable? Quite the opposite — it shows science evolves as we learn new things. Aristotle's system worked for his time, but new tools like microscopes and staining techniques revealed microorganisms nobody had seen before, and classification changed to reflect that new understanding. Biological classification — and science itself — is an ongoing process of reasoning and change, not a fixed, final answer.
12.9 Fossils as Evidence
🦴
What Are Fossils?
Preserved remains of plants and animals found in layers of rocks, sand and mud. Generally, older layers contain simpler organisms, while newer layers show more complex forms.
🇮🇳
Fossils Found in India
From giant dinosaurs to early humans and ancient plants, several important fossils have been discovered across India — natural records of life changing over millions of years.
🐸
Threads of Curiosity
The Purple Frog — A Living Fossil's Cousin
When scientists discover a new organism, they often name it after its place of discovery or in honour of a scientist. The Purple Frog (Nasikabatrachus sahyadrensis) from Kerala carries the Sahyadri Hills in its very name. It lives underground for most of the year, emerging only during the monsoon to breed, and belongs to an ancient frog family understood through fossils. Its discovery in 2003 helped scientists understand ancient amphibian groups and highlighted the urgent need for biodiversity conservation in the Western Ghats.
12.10 Biodiversity Under Threat

Each species, large or small, plays a role in nature: plants produce food and oxygen, animals pollinate flowers and disperse seeds, and microorganisms recycle nutrients. Human activities — pollution, deforestation, overuse of resources and climate change — are reducing biodiversity today. When one species disappears, others that depend on it may decline in number too, sometimes eventually disappearing as well.

Bridging Science and Society
Phumdis & the Sangai — A Floating Habitat Under Threat
Floating grasslands locally called phumdis are a unique habitat in Loktak Lake's Keibul Lamjao National Park, Manipur — floating beds of soil with rooted vegetation, ranging from a few centimetres to two metres thick. They are home to the endangered Sangai, the dancing deer endemic to Manipur, which was declared extinct in 1951 and rediscovered in 1953 thanks to naturalists identifying its unique hooves and elongated antler pattern. Today the phumdis are degenerating, and the Sangai is listed in the IUCN Red Data list — habitat loss threatens its survival, and conservation efforts are ongoing.

Indian Pioneers of Biology & Earth Sciences

From renewable energy through bacteria to reading the history of life in stone

Father of Modern Biogas
Ram Bux Singh
Pioneering Indian Biogas Technologist
Ram Bux Singh contributed enormously to the development of biogas technology in India. In 1957, he established India's first scientifically designed biogas plant at Ramnagar, Sitapur, Uttar Pradesh — marking the beginning of systematic modern biogas research in the country. He later developed low-cost, efficient biogas plants for rural areas, served as an international consultant, and helped set up biogas plants in several other countries. His lifelong efforts promoted renewable energy, waste management, and sustainable development through technology grounded in the humble work of Kingdom Monera's bacteria.
Founder, Birbal Sahni Institute of Palaeosciences
Birbal Sahni
Eminent Palaeobotanist · Lucknow
Birbal Sahni was an eminent scientist who studied fossil plants. He founded the Birbal Sahni Institute of Palaeosciences (BSIP) in Lucknow, which continues his work on ancient plants and past environments to this day. His studies helped link present-day plants with their ancestors, showing that life on Earth has a long, connected history — and his work continues to inspire young scientists exploring how fossils reveal the story of our planet.

India's varied landscapes support many endemic species. Regions rich in endemics that have also suffered habitat loss — like the Western Ghats and Himalayas — are global biodiversity hotspots.

Why We Classify
Classification organises the study of life, reveals relationships, supports naming new species, aids conservation, and gives scientists worldwide a common system.
🍄
Five Kingdom System
Monera (prokaryotes), Protista (unicellular eukaryotes), Fungi (chitin wall, decomposers), Plantae (cellulose wall, autotrophs), Animalia (no wall, heterotrophs).
🌿
Plant Kingdom's Five Classes
Thallophyta → Bryophyta → Pteridophyta → Gymnosperm → Angiosperm trace a journey of increasing independence from water, ending in flowers and fruits.
🐾
Animal Kingdom
Split by the notochord into invertebrates (8 phyla, Porifera to Echinodermata) and chordates — protochordates and vertebrates (fish, amphibians, reptiles, birds, mammals).
🏷️
Hierarchy & Naming
Kingdom → Phylum → Class → Order → Family → Genus → Species works like an address. Binomial nomenclature gives every organism one universal two-part Latin name.
